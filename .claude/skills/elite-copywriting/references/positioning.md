# Positioning

Most "bad copy" is bad positioning. This is the upstream-of-words reference. Run it before writing a single headline.

Dunford's law: **positioning is the INPUT to messaging, not the output.** Wiebe's law: **research > wireframe > copy.** Halbert's law: **market > message > media.** If your position is wrong, even Schwartz can't save the page.

## Why Positioning Comes First

Brilliant words on the wrong target lose to mediocre words on the right one. Before any copywriter touches a hero section:

| Layer (in order)       | Question                                                                    | Owner                                     |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| 1. Positioning         | What are we the best in the world at, for whom, and why does it matter now? | Founder / CMO / Head of Product Marketing |
| 2. Audience research   | Who are they, what have they tried, what words do they use?                 | PMM / Researcher                          |
| 3. Strategic narrative | What's the story of the change that makes us inevitable?                    | CEO / CMO                                 |
| 4. Messaging           | What promises, in what order, in what voice?                                | Copywriter                                |
| 5. Copy                | The literal words on the page.                                              | Copywriter                                |

Most teams skip 1–3 and start at 4–5. The result is feature soup that fails to convert.

The order below is also non-negotiable: Dunford first (it's the modern operator's framework), then the classical strategists who underwrite it, then JTBD for the inside view of the buyer, then Raskin for the executive pitch built on top.

## April Dunford — The Operating System

April Dunford spent 25 years as a VP of Marketing at seven B2B tech companies (and consulted with 200+ more) before codifying her methodology in _Obviously Awesome_ (2019) and _Sales Pitch_ (2023). Treat the two books as one connected operating system: _Obviously Awesome_ defines the position; _Sales Pitch_ delivers it.

### Definition

> Positioning is the act of **deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about.**

Three things this definition forces:

- **Deliberate.** Default positioning is what happens when you don't choose. Almost always wrong.
- **Best at something.** Not best at everything. The "something" is a chosen axis.
- **A defined market that cares a lot.** Not "all SaaS companies." Tight segment. High intensity.

Positioning is not branding. Positioning is not messaging. Positioning is the **input** that produces both.

### The 5 (+1) Components — Non-Negotiable Order

The components are dependencies. Change an earlier component and everything downstream changes. The most common failure mode is starting at the bottom (category) and working backwards — the tail wagging the dog. Run them in order, top to bottom.

| #   | Component                    | Diagnostic Question                                                                                 | Common Mistake                                                                                                   |
| --- | ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Competitive Alternatives** | If we didn't exist, what would our best customers do instead?                                       | Listing direct competitors only; ignoring status quo, spreadsheets, internal builds, manual labor, doing nothing |
| 2   | **Unique Attributes**        | What capabilities, features, qualities, or relationships do we have that the alternatives do _not_? | Listing parity features; calling things "unique" that competitors also have                                      |
| 3   | **Value (Themes)**           | What outcome the attributes enable — and can we prove it?                                           | Stopping at the attribute; abstract claims ("more efficient") with no proof                                      |
| 4   | **Customers Who Care A Lot** | Who values that value the most? What characteristics predict love?                                  | Trying to serve everyone equally; ICP that's a demographic instead of a behavioral predictor                     |
| 5   | **Market Category**          | What frame of reference do we want the customer to use when evaluating us?                          | Defaulting to the obvious category instead of choosing strategically                                             |
| +1  | **Trends**                   | What's changing in the world that makes this matter NOW?                                            | Trend-chasing without anchoring in attributes ("now with AI" tacked on)                                          |

**Critical insight:** Category is **last**, not first. Most teams pick a category, then reverse-engineer attributes to fit. Dunford's method picks the attributes first and then asks: "Given these attributes and who values them, what frame of reference helps us look obviously best?"

### Dunford's 10-Step Positioning Process

1. **Identify your best customers.** The ones who buy fast, expand fast, stay forever. Not your average customer — your _best_ customer.
2. **Form a positioning team.** Cross-functional: sales, marketing, product, customer success. Positioning is not a solo marketing exercise.
3. **Align on what positioning is.** Get the team out of "branding" and "messaging" mindset.
4. **List your true competitive alternatives** — including status quo, internal builds, spreadsheets, doing nothing.
5. **Isolate your unique attributes / features.** What only you have, or have more of.
6. **Map attributes to value themes.** Group attributes into 2–3 themes of customer-felt value.
7. **Determine who cares a lot.** Build the best-fit customer description.
8. **Find the best market frame.** What category makes your value most obvious? Sometimes existing, sometimes adjacent, sometimes new.
9. **Layer on trends** (if any). What macro shift makes this urgent?
10. **Capture the positioning in a document.** One page. Living document. Audited annually.

### The Sales Pitch Arc (Dunford 2023)

Once the position is set, the _Sales Pitch_ delivers it. Structure: **The Setup** (align the buyer on a frame) → **The Follow-Through** (show you are the perfect world).

**THE SETUP — earn the right to pitch**

1. **Insight** — The market insight your best-fit customers need to know to understand why your unique value matters. ("Here's the thing nobody else has named yet.") Stakes are seeded here.
2. **Alternatives** — Honestly survey what they could do: competitors, manual processes, spreadsheets, do-nothing. Acknowledge pros and cons of each. This earns trust.
3. **Perfect World** — Describe what an ideal solution looks like, _given your insight_. Subtly seeds purchase criteria that favor you. This is your **wedge** — buyers who agree are now measuring all alternatives against your criteria.

**THE FOLLOW-THROUGH — show you are the perfect world**

4. **Introduction** — Your company, your product, your category. One of three structures: family of products / platform with components / marketecture diagram.
5. **Differentiated Value** — Walk through unique attributes → value each delivers. Use demos + slides.
6. **Proof** — Case studies, customer quotes, statistics. Proof should mirror the prospect's situation.
7. **Objections (optional, recommended)** — Address common objections head-on, even ones the prospect hasn't raised. Inoculates against later doubts.
8. **The Ask** — Specific next step: POC, security review, expanded demo, NDA.

Web-translation: a B2B homepage that doesn't first establish the **insight** and **perfect world** is just a feature dump. The hero, sub-hero, and "why us" sections must do the work of Setup before the page asks for anything.

### Dunford Failure Modes

- **Treating positioning as marketing-only.** Sales pitches, onboarding, support docs, pricing — all leak positioning. Positioning has to be operationalized everywhere.
- **Skipping competitive alternatives.** Half the deals you lose aren't to a competitor; they're to "we did nothing." If you can't beat the status quo, your features don't matter.
- **Confusing attributes with value.** "We have AI" is an attribute. "Your team stops manually tagging 4,000 invoices a month" is value. Copy on attributes underperforms copy on value every time.

## Trout & Ries — The Positioning Canon

The foundational texts: _Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind_ (Ries & Trout, 1981) and _The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing_ (Ries & Trout, 1993). Read together with Dunford — Trout/Ries give the _philosophy_; Dunford gives the _operating procedure_.

### Core Premise

> Positioning is what you do **to the mind of the prospect** — not what you do to the product.

Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products. The best product rarely wins. The product that owns the clearest position in the prospect's mind wins.

### The 10 Most Operationally Useful Laws

| #   | Law                | Operational Implication for Copy                                                                                                                                                 |
| --- | ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Leadership**     | Better to be first than better. If you're first, _say so_ — and keep saying it.                                                                                                  |
| 2   | **Category**       | If you can't be first in a category, create a sub-category you _can_ be first in. (Light beer. Sparkling hard seltzer. AI for legal discovery.)                                  |
| 3   | **Mind**           | Better to be first in the mind than first in the marketplace. Many "firsts" were not the first product — they were the first claim.                                              |
| 4   | **Perception**     | Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products. The best product loses to the best-perceived product.                                                                        |
| 5   | **Focus**          | Own a word in the prospect's mind. Volvo = safety. FedEx = overnight. Google = search. Stripe = developer-friendly payments. If you don't own a word, you don't have a position. |
| 6   | **Ladder**         | Strategy depends on which rung you occupy. The #2 brand attacks the leader's strength; the #3 brand finds a different ladder.                                                    |
| 7   | **Duality**        | Long-term, every market becomes a two-horse race. Coke/Pepsi. Boeing/Airbus. iOS/Android. Plan for it.                                                                           |
| 8   | **Sacrifice**      | What you give up determines what you can own. You can't own "fast and cheap and high-quality." Pick.                                                                             |
| 9   | **Singularity**    | One bold move beats many cautious moves. One sharp idea executed clearly outperforms ten hedged ideas.                                                                           |
| 10  | **Line Extension** | The most damaging force. Extending a brand into adjacent categories erodes the original position. (Coors → Coors Light → Coors Banquet → Coors hard seltzer = brand confusion.)  |

### Repositioning the Competition

You don't have to attack the competitor's product — you can move them in the prospect's mind. Tylenol famously repositioned aspirin as "for some, dangerous" (gastric irritation) and took the headache category. The technique:

1. Find a structural weakness the competitor _can't fix_ without becoming you.
2. State it factually, often.
3. Force the buyer to add a new evaluation criterion that favors your strength.

Web translation: A "why not [competitor]" comparison page that doesn't reposition the competitor is just a feature table. Done right, it changes what the buyer is measuring.

### The Origin of Brands — Divergence

Ries & Ries argue that markets don't converge — they **diverge**. New categories emerge by splitting off existing ones, not by combining them. The lesson: **bet on divergence, find a sub-niche to dominate, refuse line extension.** TV diverged from radio. Computer diverged into laptop, tablet, phone. Beer diverged into craft, light, NA, hard seltzer.

## Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm

_Crossing the Chasm_ (1991) and _Inside the Tornado_ (1995). The single best framework for understanding why messaging must _change_ as a product matures.

### The Technology Adoption Lifecycle

```
Innovators → Early Adopters → [ CHASM ] → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards
   2.5%          13.5%                         34%              34%           16%
```

The chasm is the gap between **early adopters** (visionaries; buy on potential and novelty) and the **early majority** (pragmatists; buy on proof and references from peers). The vast majority of products die in the chasm because they keep using early-adopter messaging when the market has moved on.

| Segment        | What they buy for                | Copy tone                                               |
| -------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Innovators     | The technology itself            | Technical, future-facing, raw capability                |
| Early Adopters | Strategic advantage              | Bold claims, visionary outcomes, transformation         |
| Early Majority | Productivity gains with low risk | Proof, references, "industry standard," peer adoption   |
| Late Majority  | Don't get left behind            | "Safe choice," widely deployed, ease of use, support    |
| Laggards       | Forced to                        | Compliance, last vendor standing, drop-in compatibility |

**Copy implication:** A homepage that wins early adopters ("the first AI-native ledger") will lose the early majority ("we're not sure anyone we know uses this yet"). You must explicitly rewrite for each segment as you cross.

### Bowling Alley → Tornado Strategy

- **Bowling alley:** Win one beachhead segment so completely that adjacent segments fall like pins. Niche dominance precedes mass adoption.
- **Tornado:** Once the early majority commits, demand explodes. Pricing power, distribution wars, market share land grab.

The bowling-alley implication for copy: pick **one** vertical, **one** ICP, **one** use case for V1 of the website. The "we serve everyone" homepage signals you're not yet through the chasm.

### Whole Product

The minimum viable solution the pragmatist needs to actually succeed. Includes: core product + integrations + services + references + community + training + documentation. If you sell software but the whole product (integrations, partners, training) isn't there, the early majority won't buy at any price. Copy should make the whole product visible, not just the core SKU.

## Blue Ocean Strategy — Value Innovation

_Blue Ocean Strategy_ (Kim & Mauborgne, 2005). Compete only when you must — otherwise, make the competition irrelevant by creating uncontested market space.

### The ERRC Grid (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create)

For each factor the industry competes on, ask:

| Action        | Question                                                                         |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Eliminate** | Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated entirely? |
| **Reduce**    | Which factors should be reduced well below industry standard?                    |
| **Raise**     | Which factors should be raised well above industry standard?                     |
| **Create**    | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?             |

Classic examples:

- **Cirque du Soleil:** Eliminated star performers and animals. Reduced thrill/danger. Raised venue, artistic music. Created theme, refined environment.
- **Yellow Tail wine:** Eliminated enological terminology, aging quality. Reduced complexity, range. Raised retail involvement. Created easy drinking, easy selection, fun.

The ERRC grid is the most underused tool in B2B SaaS positioning. Most positioning conversations stay on Raise + Create ("we do more, better"). The Eliminate + Reduce moves are where blue ocean differentiation actually lives.

### Strategy Canvas

Plot the industry's factors of competition on the x-axis and the offering level on the y-axis. Draw a line for each competitor. Your "value curve" should diverge from competitors — ideally cross them, not run parallel. If your curve looks like everyone else's, your differentiation is illusory.

### The Three Tiers of Noncustomers

To find a blue ocean, look at **noncustomers**, not customers:

1. **First-tier (soon-to-be noncustomers)** — Minimally use the category; will jump ship for any better alternative. On the edge of your moat.
2. **Second-tier (refusing noncustomers)** — Considered the category and consciously rejected it. They've seen options and said no.
3. **Third-tier (unexplored noncustomers)** — Never considered the category at all. The industry has never targeted them. Hidden adjacent demand.

**Copy implication:** Most copy speaks to existing customers. The leverage is in writing for noncustomers — what would convince a refuser? An unexplored buyer? The story changes radically. Often the third-tier insight is the seed of category creation.

## Category Design — Play Bigger

_Play Bigger_ (Ramadan, Peterson, Lochhead, Maney, 2016). The companion to Blue Ocean for tech companies.

Core finding from analyzing 16 years of tech IPOs: **category kings capture ~76% of the market cap of their category.** The #2 player gets a sliver. Everyone else fights for crumbs. Therefore the move that captures asymmetric upside is to design and dominate a new category — not to compete in an existing one.

### The POV Document

A category king publishes a **POV (Point of View)** — a manifesto that:

1. Names a problem the market doesn't yet name.
2. Explains why existing categories fail to solve it.
3. Names the new category.
4. Positions the company as the king of it.

Famous examples: Salesforce's "End of Software," Marc Benioff's anti-software campaign. Drift's "Conversational Marketing." Zuora's "Subscription Economy." Gainsight's "Customer Success."

The POV becomes the strategic narrative (see Raskin below), which becomes the homepage, which becomes the pitch deck, which becomes the keynote. It's positioning operationalized end-to-end.

### Languaging (Christopher Lochhead)

The strategic use of language. The right name, the right vocabulary, the right enemy. Categories are won by whoever names them first and most clearly. If you're stuck describing your product as "like X but with Y," you haven't named your category yet.

## Jobs to be Done

Clayton Christensen formalized it; Bob Moesta, Chris Spiek, and Alan Klement operationalized it; Tony Ulwick built the quantitative variant (Outcome-Driven Innovation).

### The Three Layers of a Job

| Layer              | Question                     | Example (a CRM)                          |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Functional job** | What task is being done?     | Keep all our deals in one place          |
| **Emotional job**  | How do they want to feel?    | Confident I'm not dropping balls         |
| **Social job**     | How do they want to be seen? | Like a sales leader who runs a tight org |

Most B2B copy addresses only the functional layer. Elite copy hits all three. Identity (social job) is often the most-decisive layer at high market sophistication (Schwartz Stage 5).

### The Milkshake Study

Christensen's most-cited example. A fast-food chain asked customers what they wanted in a milkshake. Got demographic and feature data. Sales didn't move. Then a researcher asked: _what job were people hiring this milkshake to do?_ Two jobs emerged:

1. **Morning commute job:** keep me occupied during a boring 30-minute drive, last until I get to work, don't mess up my shirt.
2. **Evening kid job:** be a small treat I can give my kid that makes me feel like a good parent, but not so big that it ruins dinner.

The product changes for each job. The morning shake should be thicker and last longer. The evening shake should be smaller and faster. Demographics (age, income, gender) told you nothing. The _job_ told you everything.

**Copy implication:** Customers don't buy products; they hire products to do a job. Headlines that name the job out-convert headlines that name the product every time.

### JTBD Switch Interviews (Moesta)

Bob Moesta's method: interview people who **recently switched** from one solution to another (or from nothing to your product). Reconstruct the full timeline of the switch.

**The Switch Timeline — a copywriter's gold mine**

| Phase               | What's happening                                         | What to capture                                    |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **First Thought**   | Initial trigger; "huh, maybe there's a better way"       | The triggering event; the moment something cracked |
| **Passive Looking** | Background research, occasional venting                  | Where they search; what words they use             |
| **Active Looking**  | Serious comparison shopping                              | Evaluation criteria; alternatives considered       |
| **Deciding**        | Final stakeholders, final concerns, final justifications | What tipped them; what almost stopped them         |
| **First Use**       | Onboarding, first value                                  | The "I made the right call" moment (or not)        |
| **Ongoing Use**     | Habit formation                                          | What makes them stay or leave                      |

Sticky phrases from switch interviews go directly into hero copy, FAQ questions, objection handlers, and onboarding emails. Customer language always out-performs marketing language.

### The Four Forces of Progress (Moesta / Klement)

Customer change is governed by four forces. Two pro-change, two anti-change:

| Pro-Change                                                  | Anti-Change                                   |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Push** of current situation (frustration with status quo) | **Anxiety** about the new (fear of switching) |
| **Pull** of new solution (attraction to better future)      | **Habit** of the present (inertia)            |

You win when **Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.**

**Copy implication:** Most copy sells only the **Pull** (features, benefits). Elite copy:

- **Amplifies the Push** — makes the pain of the current situation explicit and unbearable.
- **Reduces Anxiety** — addresses switching costs, learning curve, "what if it doesn't work," buyer remorse.
- **Breaks the Habit** — gives a specific reason to act _now_ (event, deadline, trigger).

The "compared to your current setup" framing in B2B is a Push amplifier. The "go live in under 14 days" framing is an Anxiety reducer. The "next pricing tier kicks in Jan 1" framing breaks Habit.

### Outcome-Driven Innovation (Ulwick)

Tony Ulwick's quantitative variant of JTBD. Instead of asking _what job?_, ask: _what are all the **desired outcomes** of doing that job, and how important / satisfied are they on each?_ A 2×2 of importance × satisfaction reveals **underserved outcomes** — the richest product and copy opportunities.

Underserved outcomes are gold for copy. They're problems the market admits are important and admits are unsolved.

### Use the Customer's Hire / Fire Language Verbatim

Switch-interview transcripts contain phrases like _"I needed something that just worked,"_ _"I was tired of having to babysit our books,"_ _"I hired Notion because Evernote felt like it stopped caring."_ These verbatims belong in headlines and FAQ entries — never paraphrased. The phrase that appears in 7+ interviews IS your headline.

## Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative

The bridge between Dunford's positioning and the actual sales/marketing artifacts (decks, websites, keynotes). Where Dunford gives you the position, Raskin gives you the **story** that delivers it to executives.

### The 5-Element Framework

1. **Name a big, undeniable, urgent shift in the world.** Not your product. Not your category. A _change in the world_ the buyer can't ignore. ("The way people buy software has fundamentally changed.")
2. **Show the stakes.** There will be winners and losers in the new world. Make it clear which side the listener wants to be on.
3. **Tease the Promised Land.** The new world the audience could occupy — described in concrete, evocative terms.
4. **Show the obstacles.** Why getting to the Promised Land is hard. What old approaches fail to do. (Repositions competitors as belonging to the old world.)
5. **Position your product as the bridge.** With proof — case studies, named customers, results.

Note the structure: this is **the Hero's Journey for B2B**. The buyer is the hero. The big shift is the call to adventure. Your product is the magic gift / mentor / bridge — never the hero.

### Famous Applications

| Company        | The Big Shift                                                    | Promised Land                                       |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Drift**      | "Conversational Marketing" — buyers won't fill out forms anymore | Real-time conversation with every site visitor      |
| **Zuora**      | "The Subscription Economy" — ownership is over                   | Recurring revenue businesses that compound forever  |
| **Salesforce** | "The End of Software"                                            | Software-as-a-service; no installs                  |
| **Slack**      | "The death of email"                                             | Async, channel-based, searchable team communication |
| **Gong**       | "Revenue intelligence"                                           | Sales decisions based on data, not gut              |

Raskin's famous Medium post — _"The Greatest Sales Pitch I've Ever Seen All Year"_ (Zuora's deck) — is required reading. The Zuora deck is the canonical example.

### Where the Narrative Lives

- Homepage hero + sub-hero (3-second compressed version)
- "Why now" section (full version, 60-second read)
- Investor pitch deck slides 1–5
- Sales deck Setup section
- Founder keynote
- CEO LinkedIn manifesto post

If the narrative is consistent across all five, positioning has been operationalized.

## The 30+ Positioning Question Bank

Diagnostic battery for positioning any product. Use this before writing copy. Most questions require research (interviews, mining, surveys) — not assumption.

### Category & Frame

1. What category does the customer **currently** place us in (mentally)?
2. What category do we _want_ them to place us in?
3. What's the gap between (1) and (2) — and is it bridgeable?
4. What category names already exist? What name do we need to coin?
5. Who else is in the category we want to claim? Are we obviously among the top 2?
6. What category is _adjacent_ where we could be #1 instead of #5?

### Competitive Alternatives

7. If we didn't exist, what would our best customers do?
8. List status quo, spreadsheets, internal build, manual labor, "do nothing" — which is the actual #1 competitor?
9. Which competitor do we lose to most? Why? (Not what they have; what _story_ they tell.)
10. Who do customers compare us to in _their own words_ (not our marketing team's words)?

### Differentiation

11. What attributes do _only we_ have? (Sanity-check: would the competitor say they have it too? If yes, it's parity.)
12. Of those attributes, which actually create value the customer cares about?
13. What's our "Volvo = safety" word? What single word do we want to own?
14. What's the _opposite_ attribute we could claim vs. the market leader? (Law of Opposites)

### Audience

15. Who values our value the most? (Tight ICP, not "all SaaS companies.")
16. What characteristics predict love? (Industry, size, role, technographic, behavior, philosophy.)
17. What's our smallest viable audience?
18. Who do we _not_ serve well — and are we honest about that?

### Outcome & Identity (JTBD)

19. What's the functional outcome our customer wants?
20. What's the emotional outcome? (Feel competent, in control, smart, calm, in command.)
21. What's the social outcome? (Look good to their boss, team, board, spouse.)
22. What identity does our customer want to _claim_ by using us?
23. What identity does our customer want to _stop being associated with_?

### Trends & Urgency

24. What's changing in the world that makes us urgent NOW (not 5 years ago, not 5 years from now)?
25. What's the enemy / villain / failed status quo our category is overthrowing?

### Story & POV

26. What's the "story the customer tells themselves" that we plug into?
27. What's our POV (Lochhead) — what do we _believe_ that competitors don't?
28. What's our strategic narrative (Raskin: shift → stakes → Promised Land → obstacles → bridge)?

### Defensibility

29. What's our defensible angle — a moat that's hard to copy?
30. Where are we structurally advantaged? (Network effects, data, distribution, brand, capital, regulation, time-in-market.)

### Operational

31. Where does positioning currently leak? (Sales deck says X, website says Y, onboarding says Z, support says nothing.)
32. Who owns positioning internally? Is it operationalized across every customer touchpoint — hire forms, support macros, sales scripts, in-product copy?

If you can't answer 25 of these 32 from research (not opinion), positioning isn't ready. Don't start writing copy.

## Naming, Brand, & Positioning Overlap

### When Positioning Forces a Name Change

- The name implies the _old_ category and you're trying to claim a new one.
- The name was a feature-name that's now too narrow. (Burbn → Instagram. Tote → Pinterest.)
- The name confuses the buyer about what you do.
- The name aligns with a different ICP than the one you've now identified.

### The "X for Y" Naming Trap

Pattern: _"Uber for X."_ Variations: _"Stripe for X." "Airbnb for X." "Notion for X."_

| When it works                                                                  | When it traps                                                                                                          |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Buyer is in an early-stage emerging category and needs an analogy              | The analogy is shallow ("Uber for haircuts" — but haircuts aren't on-demand, low-frequency, network-effect businesses) |
| The analogy is _structurally accurate_ (network effects, marketplace dynamics) | The analogy makes your category dependent on the analog brand's future                                                 |
| Used as internal/investor shorthand, not public-facing position                | Anchors expectations you can't deliver on (Uber's scale, Uber's speed)                                                 |
|                                                                                | Signals to investors and buyers you don't have your own thesis yet                                                     |

Reid Hoffman's caution: reasoning by analogy in startups is _"running across a minefield where details matter."_ Use the X-for-Y construction sparingly, and only when you and the analog share the structural property that makes the analog valuable.

### Category Names (Best Practices)

- 2–4 syllables.
- Descriptive of the job, not the technology.
- Newly created or repurposed — not pre-owned by another brand.
- Easy to pluralize and verb-ify ("CRMs," "to slack someone").
- Searchable — Google the name; if the SERP is crowded with adjacent meanings, abandon.

## Win-Loss Interviews

Wins teach you what you already get right. **Losses teach you what's actually broken in your positioning.** Run win-loss interviews quarterly. Outsource if you must — but never skip them.

### Method

- Interview 10–15 deals per quarter: 5 wins, 5 losses, 5 no-decisions.
- Talk to the actual decision-maker — not just the champion.
- 30–45 minutes, recorded with consent, transcribed.
- Use a neutral interviewer (not the AE who lost the deal).

### Question Bank

**Background / Trigger**

1. What problem were you trying to solve when you started looking?
2. When did you first realize you needed something? What changed?
3. Who initiated the search? Who else was involved?

**Evaluation** 4. What vendors did you consider? How did you find them? 5. What were your top criteria? Did they change? 6. How did you weight features vs. price vs. support vs. fit?

**Decision Dynamics** 7. Who was the final decision-maker? Who had veto power? 8. Was there a champion advocating for any specific vendor? Why?

**Why Us / Why Them** 9. Why did you choose [winner]? 10. What ultimately tipped the decision? 11. What concerns did you set aside?

**About Us Specifically** 12. When did we drop off your list (if lost)? Why? 13. What could we have done differently? 14. How did our pricing compare to expectations? 15. Was there a single moment we lost it / won it?

**Closing** 16. Knowing what you know now, would you make the same decision? 17. What advice would you give us?

### What to Look For

- **Common loss reasons you can affect** — positioning, pricing, sales process.
- **Repeated buyer phrases your marketing doesn't use** — insert into copy.
- **Misperceptions about your product** — messaging gap; fix at source.
- **Competitor moves you didn't know about** — competitive intel.
- **Decision-maker mismatches** — you're selling to the wrong persona.

Win-loss data feeds directly back into the Dunford 5+1. If 4 of 5 losses cite the same alternative you didn't even list, your competitive alternatives are wrong.

## Positioning Failure Modes

The patterns that show up over and over. Audit every positioning document against these.

### 1. "We Do Everything" Generalism

The homepage that targets "modern teams" or "growing businesses." No segment. No specific job. No specific outcome. Outcome: nobody feels like the page is _for them_. Generalism is a position; it's just a losing one.

**Fix:** Pick a beachhead. Lose the wrong customers on purpose. The page should make 80% of visitors bounce and the other 20% feel violently understood.

### 2. Category-Defaulting

Picking a category because it's the obvious one ("we're a CRM") instead of strategically choosing the most favorable frame ("we're a revenue intelligence platform" / "we're a deal coaching system" / "we're a pipeline forecasting tool"). Different frames make different attributes feel valuable. The default frame usually puts you 5th on a ladder.

**Fix:** Run Dunford 5+1 in order. Let the attributes and the segment-who-cares dictate the category, not vice versa.

### 3. Listing All Features as Differentiators

The "Why us?" section that lists 12 attributes — but 10 of them are also true of your two main competitors. Differentiators are the ones that **survive the parity test**: would your competitor's CEO claim this on stage with a straight face? If yes, it's not a differentiator.

**Fix:** Cut the list to the 2–3 attributes that pass the parity test. Build the entire value proposition around them.

### 4. Trend-Chasing Without Anchor

Adding "powered by AI" / "Web3-native" / "agentic workflows" / [latest hype] without anchoring in actual unique attributes. The +1 component in Dunford's framework (Trends) is _context_, not the position itself. Trends amplify a real position; they can't substitute for one.

**Fix:** If the trend disappeared tomorrow, would you still be differentiated? If no, your real position is unknown.

### 5. Skipping Competitive Alternatives

Treating positioning as "what makes us different from CompetitorCo and CompetitorTwo" while ignoring that 60% of lost deals went to "we just kept using spreadsheets." Status quo is the most common competitor — and the hardest to beat.

**Fix:** In every positioning doc, list status quo / internal build / spreadsheets / doing-nothing as the first competitive alternative. Build the Push (JTBD Force) into your hero copy explicitly.

### 6. Marketing-Only Positioning

Positioning lives only in the marketing deck. Sales pitch says something else. Onboarding says a third thing. Pricing page implies a fourth. Buyers get whiplash; conversion suffers; churn rises.

**Fix:** Run a "positioning audit" — read your home page, pricing page, sales deck, demo script, onboarding emails, and top 5 support macros back-to-back. Any drift = a leak. Fix the leaks before optimizing copy.

### 7. Founder-Centric Positioning

Positioning written from the company's view of itself, not from how the best-fit customer actually experiences the value. ("We're the most advanced…" "We're the only platform that…") Buyers don't care about your view of yourself; they care about their own jobs.

**Fix:** Re-write every positioning sentence from the customer's perspective. If you can't, you don't understand the customer well enough to write copy yet.

## Worked Examples (Dunford 5+1 Applied)

Three positioning examples, run through the full 5+1, to show how the framework changes shape across business types.

### Example 1 — A B2B SaaS (fictional: "Ledgerline")

A double-entry bookkeeping tool for venture-backed startups with ARR between $1M–$20M.

| #   | Component                    | Ledgerline's Answer                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| --- | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Competitive Alternatives** | (a) QuickBooks + a part-time bookkeeper, (b) outsourced firms like Pilot/Bench, (c) full-time in-house controller, (d) doing the bare minimum quarterly with a CPA                                         |
| 2   | **Unique Attributes**        | Event-sourced ledger (full audit trail, no destructive edits); built-in investor reporting; automated revenue recognition for SaaS; native Stripe / Mercury / Ramp integrations; SOC 2 by month 6 of usage |
| 3   | **Value (Themes)**           | (a) Always-investor-ready books (no scramble before fundraising) (b) No bookkeeper babysitting (c) Audit-ready from day one                                                                                |
| 4   | **Customers Who Care A Lot** | Seed/Series A/B SaaS founders without a CFO yet; have raised institutional capital so investor reporting is non-optional; growing fast enough that quarterly catch-up has become painful                   |
| 5   | **Market Category**          | "The accounting system for venture-backed SaaS." Not "small business accounting" (QuickBooks owns that). Not "outsourced finance" (Pilot owns that). A new frame: the _built-for-VC-backed-SaaS_ category. |
| +1  | **Trends**                   | AI tooling has made manual bookkeeping economically irrational; rising scrutiny on revrec and ASC 606; founders are running leaner finance teams longer; LPs demanding faster portfolio reporting          |

**Hero implication:** Not "modern accounting software" (generalism). Not "QuickBooks alternative" (ladder-attacking the leader; bad position). The hero says: _"The ledger built for venture-backed SaaS. Investor-ready from day one — no bookkeeper babysitting."_ That's all five components in one sentence.

### Example 2 — A Design Agency (fictional: "North Atlas Studio")

A two-person agency that designs and builds high-end marketing sites for premium B2B SaaS.

| #   | Component                    | North Atlas's Answer                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| --- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Competitive Alternatives** | (a) Generalist agencies that bid 6-figures and produce template-feeling work, (b) Webflow freelancers who lack design depth, (c) in-house teams stretched too thin, (d) "we'll just use a template and customize"                                                         |
| 2   | **Unique Attributes**        | Both partners are senior product designers (not agency-trained); ship in 6 weeks via a fixed-scope sprint; design + build in-house (no hand-off losses); only 6 clients/year (capacity scarcity); every site built with custom-coded GSAP animation, not template effects |
| 3   | **Value (Themes)**           | (a) Award-grade design without award-grade agency overhead (b) 6 weeks to live, not 6 months (c) The hero on the homepage looks like it cost $200k (because it should have)                                                                                               |
| 4   | **Customers Who Care A Lot** | Series B+ B2B SaaS CEOs/CMOs about to do a brand refresh; budget $80–150k; previously burned by a big agency; want a site that signals premium without taking 9 months                                                                                                    |
| 5   | **Market Category**          | "A boutique product-design studio for premium B2B SaaS sites." Not "a web design agency" (commoditized). Not "a marketing agency" (wrong job). The frame: _product-design quality, marketing-site speed_.                                                                 |
| +1  | **Trends**                   | Marketing sites are increasingly the primary brand artifact; AI-generated design is making generic sites cheaper, making high-craft sites _more_ valuable; B2B buyers expect product-quality marketing experiences                                                        |

**Hero implication:** Not "we build websites" (generalism). Not "the best agency in [city]" (geography-trap). The hero says: _"Award-grade marketing sites for premium B2B SaaS. Six weeks. Six clients a year."_ Capacity scarcity (Sacrifice; Trout/Ries Law 8) is built into the position, not bolted on.

### Example 3 — An Info-Product (fictional: "The Switch Brief")

A $597 self-paced course teaching JTBD switch interviews to early-career product marketers.

| #   | Component                    | The Switch Brief's Answer                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| --- | ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Competitive Alternatives** | (a) Free YouTube content on JTBD, (b) Reforge / Maven cohort courses ($1,500–4,000), (c) Christensen's _Competing Against Luck_ book ($30) plus self-teaching, (d) doing nothing and learning on the job             |
| 2   | **Unique Attributes**        | Built around 12 fully-recorded real switch interviews with annotated transcripts; downloadable interview-guide templates; one-on-one transcript review on Loom; certification badge accepted by 40+ hiring companies |
| 3   | **Value (Themes)**           | (a) Ship a switch-interview study on the job in week 2 (b) Show measurable PMM impact in your next perf review (c) Walk into your next interview with a portfolio piece                                              |
| 4   | **Customers Who Care A Lot** | 0–3 year product marketers in B2B SaaS; on a perf plan or angling for promotion to senior PMM; manager who values qualitative research but the company has no formal research function                               |
| 5   | **Market Category**          | "The hands-on certification for JTBD switch interviews." Not "a PMM course" (saturated; Reforge owns). Not "a JTBD course" (academic-feeling). A wedge: _certification + portfolio + tactical templates_.            |
| +1  | **Trends**                   | PMM job market tightening; hiring managers screening for evidence of qualitative research skill; AI is automating quantitative analysis, raising the value of qualitative skill                                      |

**Hero implication:** Not "Learn JTBD" (Schwartz Stage 4 problem-aware language for a Stage 5 sophistication market). Not "Become a better PMM" (generalism). The hero says: _"Ship your first JTBD switch-interview study in 14 days. Get the certification 40+ hiring companies recognize."_ Concrete outcome + identity claim + social proof in one sentence — all derived directly from the 5+1.

## The One-Page Positioning Document

Output of running all of the above. Should fit on one page. Should be re-read before writing every piece of copy.

```
POSITIONING — [Product] — [Last reviewed: YYYY-MM-DD]

Competitive alternatives the best customer would otherwise use:
  1. [status quo / internal build / spreadsheet]
  2. [direct competitor]
  3. [adjacent competitor]
  4. [doing nothing]

Unique attributes (parity-tested):
  - [Attribute 1]
  - [Attribute 2]
  - [Attribute 3]

Value themes (attribute → outcome):
  - [Theme 1: ...]
  - [Theme 2: ...]
  - [Theme 3: ...]

Customers who care a lot (predictive characteristics):
  - Industry / size / role / behavior / philosophy

Market category (chosen, not defaulted):
  - [Category name + why this frame]

Trends making this urgent now:
  - [Macro shift 1]
  - [Macro shift 2]

The one word we want to own:
  - [Word]

The story the customer tells themselves that we plug into:
  - [Story in one sentence]

Strategic narrative (Raskin):
  - Shift: ...
  - Stakes: ...
  - Promised Land: ...
  - Obstacles: ...
  - Bridge: [Product]

Owner: [Name]
Audited every: [quarter / six months / year]
```

If this document doesn't exist, no copy should be written. If it does exist, every line on every page should trace back to it. **Positioning is the input. Copy is the output. Never the other way around.**
