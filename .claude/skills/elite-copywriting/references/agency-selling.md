## 1. Why Agency Sales Is Brutal

Every problem below is structural — not a discipline or hustle problem. The first job of any agency sales strategy is acknowledging the terrain.

### 1.1 The commodification trap

"Creative agency," "design studio," "branding consultancy," and "digital agency" are functionally indistinguishable category labels to the average buyer. A founder Googling "branding agency" sees 50 sites with similar aesthetics — Inter font, large hero, monochrome reel, "we build brands that…" copy. David C. Baker's diagnosis: when the category becomes commodity, the buyer collapses the decision to **price, speed, and "vibe."**

### 1.2 The trust crisis

The dominant baseline among B2B buyers is: _"We tried agencies before and got burned."_ The industry has chronically over-promised and under-delivered — missed deadlines, scope balloon, junior bait-and-switch after the kickoff dinner, dropped contact at the 60% mark.

Premium agencies must do double work: sell themselves _and_ un-sell the previous bad experience. The remedy is **structural transparency**: named senior team on every project, weekly written updates, fixed scope, defined kill-fees, and a refund policy visible in the contract.

### 1.3 Hard to differentiate (the "pretty case studies" problem)

Every agency website shows: a moving reel, a "selected works" grid, "what we do" (brand, web, motion), "process" (discover/define/design/deliver), "team" (faces in a grid), "contact." This homogeneity means **the case study is the only true differentiator** — and most are aesthetic (pretty mockups) rather than narrative (problem → method → quantified outcome → named voice).

### 1.4 Hard to price (no SKU, no MSRP)

A logo is "worth" $500 on Fiverr, $5,000 from a freelancer, $50,000 from a studio, and $500,000 from Pentagram — and _all four_ can be technically correct, depending on whose problem is being solved. There is no commodity reference price, which means **the agency must construct the value reference itself** during the sale. This is the entire premise of Enns' _Pricing Creativity_: price the client and the outcome, not the deliverable.

### 1.5 Buyers are emotional and irrational

Three high-volatility buyer states:

- **Founder buying brand identity**: buying themselves; ego-fragile; afraid of looking unprofessional; impatient; will spend disproportionately on visuals.
- **Marketing leader (CMO / VP)**: budget-anxious; pipeline-pressured; worried about CEO reaction; career-risk-averse on agency selection.
- **CEO buying rebrand**: strategic narrative + board optics; "I want a Pentagram-level brand" without Pentagram-level patience.

In every case, "rational ROI math" arrives _second_ to the emotional safety question: _will this make me look smart to the people I report to?_

### 1.6 The free-pitch race-to-the-bottom

Hard data: the average RFP costs an agency $15,000 in unbilled hours; final-round pitches average ~$45,000. Small-to-mid agencies spend **~$450,000/year just pitching**. Industry average win rate is 25–43%, but profitability requires 60%+. Result: agencies that say yes to every RFP go bankrupt slowly. The ones opting out — Pentagram, Bond, the Win Without Pitching cohort — thrive. AIGA and No!Spec have public anti-spec-pitch positions.

### 1.7 The escape route: specialize

Per Baker's _Business of Expertise_: the ideal specialization has **~10 competitors and 2,000–10,000 prospects**. Generalists ("we do branding, web, marketing, video") face 100,000+ competitors in indistinguishable categories. Specialists ("brand identity for VC-backed B2B SaaS at Series A/B") earn word-of-mouth velocity inside a finite network where executives migrate within the vertical and bring the agency along. Specialists charge **2–4× more** for the same hours because pattern-recognition compounds.

Four specialization axes:

| Axis                        | Question                                         | Examples                                                                     |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Vertical**                | Whose industry do you know cold?                 | "Branding for fintech." "Web for DTC food brands."                           |
| **Role / buyer**            | Whose problem do you solve?                      | "VC-backed B2B founders pre-Series-B."                                       |
| **Format / deliverable**    | What single output do you do better than anyone? | "Brand identities only — never websites." (Pia Silva model.)                 |
| **Problem**                 | What named pain do you fix?                      | "Positioning for SaaS companies losing deals to feature-parity competitors." |
| **Methodology** (secondary) | What process is uniquely yours?                  | "1-day brand intensives. No revisions, finished by 6pm."                     |

Baker's sequence: **Category → Specialization → Secondary differentiators**. Most agencies start with secondary differentiators ("we're collaborative, we're strategic") and skip the first two — which is why they sound the same.

## 2. Pricing Models

Six pricing modes. Each has a sweet spot and a failure mode. The wrong model for the wrong work is the most common cause of agency unprofitability.

| Model                         | When it works                                                        | When it fails                                                  | Copy implication                                                |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Hourly**                    | Tiny ad-hoc jobs, unknown scope                                      | Caps income at hourly rate × hours awake. Misaligns incentives | Don't publish; signals vendor tier                              |
| **Project-based (fixed-fee)** | Defined scope, defined deliverable, predictable timeline             | Scope creep, interpretation drift                              | Publish floors ("starts at $X") not full rates                  |
| **Value-based**               | Quantifiable outcome, decision-maker has authority                   | Diffuse outcomes, shared attribution                           | Don't list price; sell outcomes ("our last 4 rebrands lifted…") |
| **Retainer / monthly**        | Ongoing creative direction, brand stewardship, fractional CD         | Defined-scope projects sneak in; service drift                 | Publish monthly band ("$5–25k/mo") if at all                    |
| **Productized**               | Repeatable scope, fast turnaround, brand-light work                  | Strategy-heavy, multi-stakeholder, full rebrands               | Publish exact price + inclusions + exclusions                   |
| **Performance / equity**      | High trust, controllable variables, mutual upside, client can't cash | Most cases — too risky for the agency                          | Almost never used as primary positioning                        |

### 2.1 Hourly (Jonathan Stark's "nuts")

Stark's central argument from _Hourly Billing Is Nuts_: _"If you bill by the hour, you are incentivized to be slow. The faster you get, the less you make."_ Hourly creates structural misalignment — the agency profits from inefficiency; the client wants efficiency. It also caps pricing at the local labor market.

Rate bands when you must:

- Junior: $75–125/hr
- Senior: $150–250/hr
- Partner-level: $300–600/hr
- "Celebrity" designers (Paula Scher tier): $1,000+/hr

**Verdict:** valid only as a last-resort billing mode, never a pricing strategy.

### 2.2 Project-based (fixed-fee)

The default. How to scope:

1. **Pre-scope discovery** is its own billable engagement ($5–15k typical) — never free.
2. **Build a deliverables matrix** with line items, but price the _outcome_, not the matrix.
3. **Build in a 15–25% buffer** for revision rounds; cap rounds explicitly (typically 2–3).
4. **Walk-away triggers:** budget is <50% of your floor; client wants to "see something first"; client refuses to sign a scope doc; client calls you "vendor."
5. **Payment structure:** 50/50 standard; 33/33/33 for longer engagements; milestone-based for $100k+.

### 2.3 Value-based pricing (Blair Enns / _Pricing Creativity_)

The most sophisticated and the most profitable. Method:

**Discovery questions:**

1. _"What is this problem costing you right now?"_ → surfaces Cost of Inaction (CoI). Example: "$30K/month in support costs + $80K/quarter in patient churn." That's a $600K/year problem.
2. _"What would solving this be worth over the next 12 months?"_ → reveals upside.
3. _"What are your alternatives, and what would they cost?"_ → establishes ceiling.

**Pricing math (Haus Advisors formula):**

- Find combined annual economic value.
- **Price = 10–25% of first-year value.**
- On a $600k/yr problem: $60–150k. At $120k, the client gets 5:1 ROI.

**The reframe:** you're not selling 200 hours — you're selling $480k of unlocked value, captured at 25%. Same hours, 4–10× the fee.

### 2.4 Retainer / monthly

Three flavors:

- **Fixed-fee retainer**: set $X/month for defined services (most common, most stable).
- **Hours-bucket retainer**: paid for N hours/month with overage rules. Stark hates these for the same reasons he hates hourly.
- **Hybrid**: base fixed fee + variable for additional scope.

**Fractional creative director:** $4–15k/month for senior strategic oversight, no execution. Ideal for funded startups too small for full in-house but too big for project-only.

**Ongoing brand stewardship:** post-rebrand retainer at $5–25k/month for guideline enforcement, asset production, evolution. Highly defensible — the client has switching costs (brand-consistency risk).

### 2.5 Productized (Designjoy model)

Brett Williams runs Designjoy solo. **$4,995/month, unlimited design requests, one active at a time, 48-hour turnaround, pause anytime.** ~35 clients = ~$1.75M ARR. Solo. No sales calls. No proposals.

**Mechanics:**

- Fixed scope (defined inclusions + explicit exclusions).
- Fixed price (e.g., $4,995/mo).
- Fixed turnaround (48 hours).
- Defined channel (Trello board, async only).
- Cancel-anytime / pause-anytime.

**Margin reality:** Productized services hit **65%+ gross margin** vs. 45–55% on custom. The 2025 Agency Growth Benchmark (300+ agencies) showed productized agencies at **40–75% gross margin** vs. industry average **18–22%**.

**When productization works:** repeatable deliverables (web design, motion graphics, presentation design, social content, copy). **When it fails:** strategy-heavy work, full brand identities, multi-stakeholder enterprise.

**The ceiling:** per-client value caps at the subscription price (~$5k/mo = $60k/yr/client). To grow: add clients or raise prices.

### 2.6 Performance / equity / hybrid

- **Performance-based:** works when you control all variables (paid ads, CRO, lead gen). Fails for brand, design, anything delayed or multi-attribution. Required premium: ≥3× the cash fee.
- **Equity:** highest-risk pricing mode. Most agencies regret it. Only consider if shares are liquid OR you'd invest cash in this startup anyway. Never replace cash entirely. Rule of thumb: equity component should be valued at 3× the cash forgone.
- **Hybrid (the realistic premium model):** base fee + success kicker. Example: _"$80k retainer + 2% of incremental revenue above $X baseline."_

### 2.7 Payment terms (across all models)

- 50/50 — small projects.
- 33/33/33 — mid-size.
- Milestone — large, $100k+.
- **Net-7 or Net-15**: never Net-30. Net-30 is enterprise nonsense designed to float your money.
- **Late fees:** 1.5%/month standard; some studios charge 5% flat after 14 days.
- **Kill fees:** 25% (cancel post-briefing) / 50–60% (cancel mid-project) / 75%+ (cancel late stage).

## 3. Win Without Pitching — The Bible

Blair Enns' _Win Without Pitching Manifesto_ (2010) and _Pricing Creativity_ (2018) are the load-bearing texts for premium-agency sales. If you read one set of books in your career, read these.

### 3.1 The core stance

**Stop pitching for free.** Treat the agency as a professional practice — like law, accounting, or medicine — not a vendor that auditions. A doctor doesn't show you the X-ray for free; an architect doesn't draft three different houses on spec; an attorney doesn't argue a hypothetical case at the consultation. Why does the agency?

The free pitch is what Enns calls "the cardinal sin." It collapses positioning, signals vendor-tier desperation, trains the client to expect free work, and rewards the agencies most willing to bleed for the deal — not the ones best at the work.

### 3.2 The Four Conversations

| #   | Conversation   | Objective                                                                                        | Where it happens                                                            |
| --- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Probative**  | Move you from "vendor" to "expert" in their mind                                                 | _Happens without you present._ Reputation, content, referrals, the website. |
| 2   | **Qualifying** | Mutual fit assessment. You vet them; they vet you.                                               | First call.                                                                 |
| 3   | **Value**      | Define the value to be created; establish your share. **Pricing reveal happens here, verbally.** | Second call.                                                                |
| 4   | **Closing**    | Convert to engagement.                                                                           | Final call. _Highest risk of giving away leverage through eagerness._       |

> "You're going to push harder, smile brighter — and undermine your expert positioning." — Enns on what kills closing calls.

### 3.3 Diagnose before prescribe

Proclamation #3 of the Manifesto: never propose a solution before you've diagnosed the problem. **Charge for the diagnosis if it's significant.** A doctor charges for an MRI before prescribing surgery; an agency should charge for the discovery before designing.

The free-discovery trap: agencies do 4 hours of free strategy to "scope the project," hand it over in a proposal, and watch the prospect implement the strategy with a cheaper vendor. Charge for diagnosis. _Always._

### 3.4 The 12 Proclamations (condensed)

1. We will specialize.
2. We will replace presentations with conversations.
3. We will diagnose before we prescribe.
4. We will rethink what it means to sell.
5. We will be selective.
6. We will build expertise rapidly.
7. We will address issues of fit first.
8. We will not solve problems before we are paid.
9. We will not write or design speculatively.
10. We will charge more.
11. We will price based on value.
12. We will refuse to be ranked against competitors.

Each one inverts what most agencies do.

### 3.5 The Magic Email (the ghost-revival template)

For prospects who've gone dark, Enns published this template, which has become a small legend in agency circles:

```
Subject: Closing the loop

Hi [first name],

I haven't heard back from you on [project], so I'm going to assume
you've gone in a different direction or your priorities have changed.

Feel free to reach out if I can be of assistance in the future.

Best,
[Name]
```

**Why it works:** removes the emotional pressure on the recipient to "be polite," strips your neediness, and triggers loss aversion. Reported response rates are extraordinary (8/8 in some informal tests). Many prospects reply within minutes.

## 4. The Proposal as Sales Document

> The proposal IS the sale, not the meeting. Most proposals fail.

### 4.1 The proposal IS the decision document

Most agencies treat the proposal as a _summary of an already-made decision_. It's not. The proposal **is the decision document** — every objection, every comparison, every internal-stakeholder pushback happens inside that PDF. The proposal must close the sale by itself when read alone at 11pm by a CFO who didn't attend the call.

### 4.2 Enns' anti-proposal stance

Enns advocates: **price the project in conversation, then send a contract — not a proposal.**

> When a client says "Great. Now can you get me a proposal?" Enns' recommended response: _"Sure. I propose that we do X for you for Y dollars in Z timeframe."_ The document is the contract. It comes out once the deal has been struck.

> "We're not in the proposal-writing business." — Enns

His logic: a proposal is an unpaid free-pitch. The document should be a 1-page agreement, not a 30-page sales artifact. You sell in the value conversation; the doc records the deal.

### 4.3 Components of a winning proposal (when one is required)

| Section                | What it does                                                                                                 |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Positioning recap**  | "What we heard you say…" — mirrors back their words. Shows listening.                                        |
| **Context / why now**  | Reframes the problem in a way _they_ didn't articulate. This is where you earn the fee.                      |
| **Approach / method**  | Your specific, named process. Not "discovery, design, deliver" — actual differentiated method.               |
| **Team**               | Named humans with photos and 2-line bios. _Not_ "our team of 30 designers."                                  |
| **Timeline**           | Specific weeks, specific milestones, with dependencies.                                                      |
| **Investment options** | Always 3. Always anchor high. Single-page where possible.                                                    |
| **Case studies (1–2)** | Problem-twin clients. Quantified outcomes. Named voices.                                                     |
| **Terms**              | Payment, scope, IP, kill fee, late fee, revisions, cancellation.                                             |
| **Next steps**         | A specific verb-action with a specific date. "Sign and return by Friday; we hold the start date until then." |

### 4.4 The 3-tier anchor (Enns' single most consequential frame)

**Always present three options, always anchor with the highest.**

- **Option 1 (Anchor / Deluxe):** "What would we do if money were no object?" Premium price, premium scope.
- **Option 2 (Middle / Recommended):** The intended fit. Most clients choose this.
- **Option 3 (Floor / Budget):** The minimum to deliver value. Bare scope.

> "If you resolve from hereon to always put three options in your proposals, you will increase the percentage of positive outcomes by half." — _Pricing Creativity_

Why it works: it changes the buyer's question from _"Does this represent good value?"_ (binary, defensive) to _"Which represents the best value?"_ (comparative, exploratory). One firm reported an **11× increase in profit and 2× revenue** from switching to 3-option pricing.

### 4.5 Common proposal failures

- **Scope balloon** ("…and also strategy workshop, also social templates, also…").
- **Generic team bios** ("award-winning designers passionate about brand").
- **Vague timeline** ("approximately 8–12 weeks").
- **Single-price** (no anchor → buyer benchmarks against Fiverr).
- **Buried CTA** (signature page on p. 32).
- **No urgency** (no expiry date, no "calendar holds until X").
- **Hedged language** ("we'll do our best to," "approximately," "subject to scope changes").
- **Reads like a contract** instead of a sales document.

### 4.6 1-page vs 30-page debate

| Length        | When to use                                                                                                            |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1-page**    | Enns ideal. Value is clear, decision-maker is single, project is well-scoped. Used as a conversation guide.            |
| **5–8 pages** | Practical middle. Premium positioning with enough surface area for committee review. The 1-page version as Appendix A. |
| **30+ pages** | Decision is committee, "look the part" matters, champion needs internal selling ammunition. Beware: easy to drown.     |

The 2Bobs "Killer Proposal" episode is famously satirical, mocking 75-page proposals with team-childhood-photos and Wikipedia copy as the _opposite_ of what works.

### 4.7 "Investment" vs "Price" language

Use "investment" _only if the math actually supports it_ (you've quantified ROI in the proposal). Otherwise it's a euphemism that reads as insecure. **"Price" is fine when you're confident**; "investment" earns its keep only when the proposal explicitly shows the return.

## 5. Premium Positioning for Creative Services

What makes Pentagram, Method, Ueno, Bond, &Walsh, Mucho, Pony expensive — and how their copy reflects it.

### 5.1 Heritage signals

- **Pentagram:** _"Five decades. Many industries. Clients of every size."_ Time embedded in the first paragraph.
- **Mucho:** _"For over 20 years we've helped brands stand tall, grow big, and stay relevant in a changing world. And we're just getting started."_
- **Bond:** _"Independent since 2009. 15 years of expertise."_

Heritage compounds. Every year of operation is a barrier no competitor can replicate. Each decade adds roughly **30% pricing power**.

### 5.2 Process signals (your method is the differentiator)

- **Bond's named programs:** Shakeup, Slingshot, Ignite, And Action — each tied to a stage of brand growth. Naming the process makes it a _product_.
- **Pia Silva's intensives:** _"Branded in a day. Paid in full. Done by 6pm."_ The process _is_ the offer.

When you have a named process, you're no longer comparing on hours. You're selling Acme™ Methodology.

### 5.3 Selectivity signals

- **Pentagram:** rejects spec work as policy; partners describe it as "contrary to our ethos of client-led, compensated engagements."
- **Pia Silva:** "We only work with 1–3 person businesses."
- **Designjoy:** explicit exclusion list (no 3D, no animation, no Adobe InDesign documents).
- **Wesley Kao-tier consultants:** "I take 3 clients per quarter."

Saying no is the highest-conversion sales asset on a premium site.

### 5.4 Founder / principal as personality

- **Pentagram:** "the only major design studio where the owners of the business are the creators of the work and serve as the primary contact for every client." Every partner has a bio page; Paula Scher is a brand within the brand.
- **&Walsh:** Jessica Walsh's identity carries the agency.

Founder-led is a trust accelerant — buyers trust people faster than they trust brands.

### 5.5 Restraint in claims (the confident understatement)

**Pentagram opens with:** _"Pentagram is a multi-disciplinary, independently owned design studio."_

No "award-winning." No "industry-leading." No "passionate." The most expensive studios use the least hyped language. The pattern: **the more confident, the less hype.**

Compare to amateur agency copy:

> "We're a world-class, award-winning, full-service creative agency that helps innovative brands disrupt their industries with bold, transformative design experiences that drive measurable ROI."

Each adjective halves the price.

### 5.6 No portfolio gallery — only deep case studies

Mid-tier agencies show 50 thumbnails. Premium studios show 6–12 deep case studies, each its own page, each with narrative, process artifacts, and named voices. The shift signals: **we don't have many clients, but each one matters.** Depth signals expertise; breadth signals desperation.

### 5.7 Pricing transparency strategy

- **Total transparency**: Designjoy publishes $4,995/mo on the homepage. Productized services _must_ be transparent — that's the offer.
- **Floor + range**: Pia Silva publishes "$35–50k" intensives. Pre-qualifies budget without committing to ceiling.
- **"Starts at $X"**: most premium studios. Pre-qualifies budget.
- **Total opacity**: Pentagram publishes nothing. Custom strategy work uses opacity to protect price-discovery.

**Rule:** publish price floors that pre-qualify; opacity protects high-ceiling work; total opacity reads as either premium or scammy depending on the rest of the site. **Never wishy-washy "starting at" with no number.**

### 5.8 Language matters: studio vs agency vs consultancy

| Word                 | Implication                                               | Pricing anchor         |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| **Studio**           | Craftsmanship, small team, partner-led (Pentagram, Mucho) | Premium                |
| **Agency**           | Scale, account managers, multi-discipline (BBDO, Anomaly) | Variable               |
| **Consultancy**      | Strategy-first, named principals, advisory fees           | Highest premium        |
| **Collective / Lab** | Experimental, network, fluid teams                        | Best for project-based |
| **Shop**             | Bespoke, two-person, hand-built                           | Boutique premium       |

Pentagram says _"design studio"_ — not agency. That word choice anchors $250k+ projects.

### 5.9 Your own site is your strongest portfolio piece

If you sell brand design and your own brand is mediocre, all case studies are discounted. Ueno, Bond, Mucho, Method, and &Walsh each invested the equivalent of a top-3 client engagement into their own homepage. The implicit promise: _if we did this for ourselves, imagine what we'll do for you._

**Trellis note:** the studio's own site needs to be Awwwards-grade or higher. It's the load-bearing trust signal.

## 6. Case Study Architecture

The most-undervalued agency asset. A great case study sells more than a great homepage.

### 6.1 The narrative arc

| Section         | Length        | Job                                                                                                                                                  |
| --------------- | ------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Context**     | 50–100 words  | "Their world before." Establish situation pre-engagement: pressures, market, previous attempts.                                                      |
| **Problem**     | 100–150 words | Specific named pain. Not "they wanted a new brand." Rather, "they were losing demo conversions to a competitor that looked more 'enterprise-ready.'" |
| **Approach**    | 200–400 words | _Your specific method_, not "discovery → design." Show artifacts. Show workshops. Show wrong directions you discarded.                               |
| **Outcome**     | 100–150 words | Specific numbers. Time-bound. "6 months post-launch: demo conversion +47%, ACV up 22%, sales cycle reduced 11 days."                                 |
| **Testimonial** | 50–100 words  | Named client. Title. Photo. Verbatim quote. Specific (not "they were amazing").                                                                      |

### 6.2 Why most agency case studies fail

- **Too aesthetic, no narrative.** Beautiful mockups, zero story.
- **No quantified outcome.** "Increased brand awareness" is meaningless.
- **No named client.** Anonymous "Fortune 500 financial services" = unverifiable = ignored.
- **No specific approach.** Could be any agency.
- **No specific problem.** Could be any client.
- **No "wrong direction."** Showing the design path you rejected creates trust by proving you tried alternatives.
- **No buyer's perspective.** Written entirely from the agency's POV.

### 6.3 Two formats

**The Ueno / Bond / Pentagram pattern (long-form):** scrollable, sequential. Begin with a single hero image and a 1-sentence subhead that summarizes the entire engagement. Then narrative. Then artifacts inline. Then numbers near the end. Then a named voice. Then a thumbnail link to the next case.

**The Designjoy "before/after snippet" pattern (productized):** For agencies where each client is too small for a deep case study — a 2-tile image (before/after) + 1-sentence problem + 1-sentence outcome + a client tweet or testimonial. Volume over depth.

### 6.4 Case study as SEO asset

Long-tail intent: "fintech rebrand case study," "DTC food brand identity refresh." These pages compound traffic for years and rank for the bottom-of-funnel queries enterprise buyers actually use when evaluating agencies. Treat case studies as content marketing assets, not portfolio garnish.

## 7. Discovery / Sales Call Frameworks

### 7.1 Voss-calibrated questions (for agency discovery)

Chris Voss's _Never Split the Difference_ applied to creative sales. Every question is "how" or "what," never "why":

- "What happens if you don't solve this in the next 6 months?"
- "How does the board measure success of the rebrand?"
- "What's the cost of staying where you are for another year?"
- "How would you describe success when we hand off the final files?"
- "What would have to be true for this to be the easiest 'yes' you've ever signed?"
- "Who else needs to weigh in?"
- "Walk me through the day this is fixed."

### 7.2 The tier-reveal move

In the value conversation, walk through three options _verbally_ — anchor first. Watch the client self-select. The middle option converts ~60% of the time when anchored correctly.

Example script:

> "Based on what you've told me, there are three ways we could engage. The deluxe version is around $X — that includes full strategy, identity, motion, web, and 12 months of stewardship. The recommended version is around $Y — identity, web, and a launch package. The floor version is around $Z — identity-only, no web. Which of those sounds closest to what you had in mind?"

Their answer tells you their budget _and_ their psychology in 30 seconds.

### 7.3 Pricing in the call vs. by proposal

Enns recommends **verbal price-floors stated before the proposal**:

> "Projects of this type with us typically range from $80k to $250k. Does that fit your thinking?"

This surfaces objections early. **Early objections are friends; late objections are enemies.** Many deals die because the agency waited to reveal price in the document instead of pressure-testing in the call.

### 7.4 Diagnose before prescribe

Don't propose work until you've understood the problem. The first call is qualification. The second call is diagnosis. Pricing happens in the diagnosis call, only after you've heard the cost of inaction. Most agencies collapse these into one call and price blind.

### 7.5 The walking-away move

The single most powerful position in agency sales is **more demand than capacity**. When you can credibly say "our next slot is October" or "we're at capacity but I can refer you to [name]," everything changes. Pricing power is downstream of scarcity.

Build a waitlist before you need it. Reveal capacity constraints visibly on the website.

### 7.6 Handling "Send us a proposal"

Enns' response: _"Sure — I propose that we do X for Y dollars in Z weeks."_ Not until you have clarity on fit.

If they push:

> "We're not in the proposal-writing business. Once we've agreed on what success looks like, the document is just the record. Can we book 30 min to align on success criteria first? Otherwise I'd be guessing."

### 7.7 Handling "We need to think about it"

Sandler's _Negative Reverse Selling_: don't fight it.

> "Fair enough — when most people tell me that, it usually means 'no, but I want to be polite.' What's the actual hesitation?"

Names the elephant. Forces honesty. Often unsticks the deal.

## 8. Common Agency Objections + Pivots

| Objection                                                 | Pivot                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "We have an in-house team."                               | Position as augmentation. "We collaborate often with in-house teams — we typically own [specialized slice] while your team handles [in-house slice]. The combination is what most premium brands use." Then: _"What's the work your team is currently stretched on?"_ The objection becomes the entry.                             |
| "Let's just use Fiverr / 99designs / a freelancer."       | TCO math: _"At Fiverr you're paying for the file. With us you're paying for the strategy that means the file works. The cost question is: what's a wrong brand decision worth at this stage? If a logo refresh tanks your retention by 2%, the saved $40k cost you $400k."_                                                        |
| "We tried agencies before and got burned."                | Don't defend the industry. Name the failure pattern: _"That's the standard pattern — bait-and-switch from senior to junior after kickoff, scope balloon, dropped contact at the 60% mark. Here's structurally how we make that impossible: [named senior, weekly written update, fixed revision rounds, kill fee in your favor]."_ |
| "Why are you so much more expensive than X?"              | Don't justify; reframe. _"Compared to [X], we're 3×. Here's what that 3× buys you: [outcome], [team signal], [risk signal]. If those don't matter for this project, [X] is genuinely the right call and we'll happily refer you."_ The referral sentence is the close. Signals abundance.                                          |
| "Can we do a paid pilot first?"                           | Sometimes yes — structured. Define the pilot as a _complete deliverable_, not a "preview." Full margin, not discounted. Pre-define the path to full engagement, with pilot fee credited only if signed within 30 days. Never let the pilot become free pitching.                                                                   |
| "Can you do it for half if we sign for 12 months?"        | **Enns: No.** Volume discounts make sense when marginal cost approaches zero (software); they don't for senior creative work. Counter: _"At our standard rate, here's what we'll throw in for the commitment that wouldn't otherwise be included."_ Bonus, not discount.                                                           |
| "Send us a proposal and we'll review."                    | Enns: not until alignment on fit, scope, and budget. _"I want to make sure what I send is actually useful. Can we book 30 min next week to align on success criteria first?"_                                                                                                                                                      |
| "We need to think about it."                              | Sandler negative-reverse. _"Of course. When most people say that, it's usually a polite 'no.' What's the actual hesitation?"_                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| "We need to talk to other agencies first."                | Don't fight it. _"Smart. Here's a checklist of questions to ask each one — including us."_ Become the trusted advisor of the buying process. Most prospects come back to the agency that helped them buy.                                                                                                                          |
| "Can you show us a quick sketch / mockup before we sign?" | The free-pitch trap. _"We don't do speculative work — but if you'd like to engage us for a paid 1-week positioning sprint, you'll see exactly how we think. That fee is credited to the full engagement if you proceed within 30 days."_                                                                                           |

For deeper objection theory, see [objection-handling.md](objection-handling.md) and [objection-prediction.md](objection-prediction.md).

## 9. The Productized Services Trend

### 9.1 Landscape

- **Designjoy (Brett Williams)**: $4,995/mo, unlimited design, 48-hour turnaround. ~$1.5–2M ARR solo. The category-defining model.
- **ManyPixels**: $549–1,099/mo, team-based, higher volume / lower price.
- **Penji**: graphic design subscription, mid-market.
- **Superside / Awesomic / Wedge**: venture-backed scale-ups productizing creative.
- **Refresh, Kalli, Quarter Studio**: newer premium-productized players ($5–15k/mo).

### 9.2 Why it works (when it works)

- Predictable MRR (the metric that creates enterprise value at exit).
- Massively reduced sales motion — no quotes, no proposals.
- Operational efficiency compounds (every repeat task gets faster, margins climb).
- Anti-quote-fatigue for both buyer and seller.
- Easy to communicate — _"$5k/mo, unlimited design, cancel anytime"_ is a tweet.
- 65%+ gross margin vs. 45–55% on custom.

### 9.3 Why it doesn't work for premium brand work

- Ceiling on per-client value (the $5k/mo cap).
- Brand-quality risk — productized work is rarely strategic; cheap-feeling work degrades reputation.
- Support load — the more you accept, the more revision cycles you carry.
- Hard to scale beyond founder capacity without hiring (which erodes the model).
- Buyer perception cap: _"big players never choose this; they think it's for broke clients."_
- No room for senior creative direction.

### 9.4 Who should productize, who shouldn't

| Productize if                                                         | Don't productize if             |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Solo or small operator with a defined operational moat                | Strategy-led firm               |
| Serving clearly-bounded buyer (startup founders, indie hackers)       | Premium brand identity studio   |
| Work is _operationally repeatable_ (web pages, motion, social, decks) | Serving Fortune 1000 enterprise |
| Volume of small clients > value of fewer large clients                | Unit value > $25k/project       |

**Trellis note:** Trellis sits firmly in the "don't productize" column for its primary brand-identity work. A productized tier _might_ exist as a secondary funnel (e.g., "Trellis Light" for $5k/mo social + landing-page support), but it should never represent the agency.

## 10. The Agency Content / Marketing Engine

### 10.1 Inbound (the long game)

- **SEO blog**: long-tail vertical content (e.g., "DTC food brand identity examples"). Compounds over years.
- **YouTube**: process content, behind-the-scenes, founder POV (Brad Hussey, Chris Do model).
- **Case studies**: high-intent SEO assets; outrank generic content for "[industry] rebrand case study."
- **Newsletter**: segmented to founders / CMOs / CEOs.
- **Founder LinkedIn**: the single most-impactful personal asset for B2B agencies in 2025–26. "Founder-led marketing" outperforms branded social by 3–10×.

### 10.2 Outbound (where it actually works)

- **Cold email**: works only for vertical specialists with crisp ICPs. Generalists get filtered out.
- **LinkedIn outreach**: premium agencies use this _only_ for warm-warm leads (commented on, engaged with, mutual-connections-introduced). Cold connection requests don't work for premium services.
- **The trap:** outbound creates a vendor-tier sales process. Premium positioning requires inbound-led.

### 10.3 Referral (the highest-converting channel)

- B2B referrals: **30% higher conversion rate, 16% higher LTV, 4× more likely to refer themselves.**
- For premium agencies, referral is usually **60–80% of new business.**
- **Systematize:** ask after every successful project ("who are 2 people who should hear about this work?"); track referrer credits; reward referrers (cash, gifts, or in-kind credit on future work).
- Build _partner channels_ — designers refer copywriters refer dev shops refer agencies. Tight-knit pro networks are pricing-power generators.

### 10.4 Speaking / podcasts / conferences

**Not a lead-gen channel — a positioning amplifier.** A keynote at a vertical conference doesn't generate leads, but it makes the next inbound lead think "they're the obvious choice."

### 10.5 Build-in-public (the Designjoy playbook)

Brett Williams' loop:

1. Share Stripe screenshots, MRR milestones, behind-the-scenes ops.
2. Build personal trust through transparency.
3. Convert audience into clients.

Worked because the proof (Stripe dashboard) was unfakeable. _"When people saw a solo designer making $150,000/month, they lost their minds. That 'proof' did more marketing than a thousand blog posts ever could."_

Doesn't work for premium agencies that sell discretion. Works for solo / personality-led / productized.

### 10.6 The "one lighthouse content piece per quarter" strategy

Rather than weekly blog spam, invest 80% of content effort into **one major piece per quarter**: a deep report, a State-of-Branding-in-X analysis, a long-form case study, a podcast season. Each becomes a referenceable artifact that lives for 2+ years.

Pentagram does this with its New Year predictions. Reforge does it with reports. Bond does it with vertical industry analyses.

## 11. Pricing Copy That Justifies Premium Fees

### 11.1 Stop competing on price by competing on outcome

Replace any "we offer X" claim with "we deliver Y outcome."

- "We design beautiful brand identities."
- "Our last 4 SaaS rebrands lifted demo conversion 22–47% within 6 months of launch."

The second one prices itself.

### 11.2 Quantify impact wherever possible

On the agency homepage and case studies:

- "Our DTC clients average 2.1× increase in returning-customer rate post-rebrand."
- "Our average B2B client recovers our fee in 8 months via lift in conversion."
- "Our packaging redesigns lift in-store conversion 12–18% on average."

Cross-references: this maps directly to `elite-ux-strategy` (conversion framing) and the data-vis patterns in `elite-design-core`.

### 11.3 Time-cost framing

> "A $50k rebrand pays back if it lifts your homepage conversion by 0.5%. At $2M ARR, that's <60 days."

Make the math visible. Forces the buyer into ROI mode, away from price-comparison mode.

### 11.4 "What you're NOT paying for"

Premium pricing copy that names the absence:

> "You're not paying for: a junior team after the kickoff dinner, account managers translating your feedback to designers, hours-billed for revision rounds, template-driven design dressed up as bespoke, or agency overhead we recoup from your project."

That paragraph alone justifies a 2× premium for many buyers.

### 11.5 "Investment" language done right

Use "investment" _only_ when the proposal _shows the return._ Otherwise use "fee" or "price." Investment without ROI math reads as insecure. With ROI math, it reads as transparent.

### 11.6 The "compared to the cost of a wrong brand" reframe

> "Compared to the cost of a wrong brand at this stage — a Series-B rebrand averages $400–800k, plus the opportunity cost of 6 months of distracted leadership — the right partner at the right moment is the cheapest insurance you'll buy this year."

Anchors against the disaster, not the alternative quote.

## 12. Selling to Different Buyer Types

### 12.1 Founders

| Drivers               | What sells                                                           | What kills the deal                           |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Speed                 | Pia Silva 1-day intensive. Show similar founders you've worked with. | Slow turnaround.                              |
| Identity              | "This feels like me." Show you _get_ them.                           | RFP-style proposals.                          |
| Self-spend            | Willing to spend on themselves; afraid of looking unprofessional.    | Too many people on the call.                  |
| Hate consultant-speak | Be a fellow operator, not a strategist.                              | Jargon, frameworks, slide decks with circles. |

**Pricing band:** They can sign $30–80k decisions without committee. Don't over-engineer the sale.

### 12.2 CMOs / VP Marketing

| Drivers                | What sells                                                                     | What kills the deal             |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Pipeline impact        | Quantified case studies from peer CMOs.                                        | Vague timelines.                |
| Brand defensibility    | "Brand-as-moat" framing.                                                       | Junior team.                    |
| Peer-CMO references    | "[Named CMO at peer brand] would happily take a 10-min reference call."        | No measurable outcome promised. |
| Board exposure fear    | Risk reduction — named senior team, fixed scope, defined milestones, kill fee. | Anything that looks like risk.  |
| LinkedIn-quotable wins | "47% lift in demo conversion."                                                 | Generic process slides.         |

**Pricing band:** $50–250k. Multi-stakeholder. Procurement may enter.

### 12.3 CEOs

| Drivers                | What sells                                                           | What kills the deal                                 |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Strategic positioning  | Strategic narrative — Andy Raskin's Old World → New World.           | Looking junior.                                     |
| Board-level narrative  | Principal-level access. "You'll work directly with [named partner]." | Talking like a vendor.                              |
| Premium quality optics | "Pentagram-level" signaling. Heritage. Selectivity.                  | Mentioning hours.                                   |
| Legacy / reputation    | Restrained confidence. Understatement.                               | Adjective-stacking ("award-winning, world-class…"). |

**Pricing band:** $100k–$1M+. Money is rarely the actual objection; authority and credibility are.

### 12.4 In-house creative leaders

| Drivers                       | What sells                                                               | What kills the deal                                      |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Augmentation, not replacement | "We typically own [specialized slice] while your team handles the rest." | Any hint that you'd replace their team.                  |
| Specialized expertise         | Position as their secret weapon — they take credit for hiring you.       | Competing with the in-house team on generalist work.     |
| Capacity relief               | Surge capacity for launches, peak season.                                | "We could do everything your team does." Threat = death. |
| Fresh outside POV             | "Sometimes the team closest to the work can't see what's needed."        | Implying the in-house team has failed.                   |

**Pricing band:** Retainer + project hybrid.

## 14. Copy That Wins Agency Deals — Real Examples

### 14.1 Homepage hero patterns

**Pentagram (restraint):**

> "Pentagram is a multi-disciplinary, independently owned design studio."

No adjectives. Implied premium: _if you have to ask, you can't afford us._

**Bond (commercial bite):**

> "Magic underpinned by metrics, delivering design that works."
>
> "Design that is easy to scale, expensive to copy and proven by results."

Combines craft signal ("magic") with business signal ("metrics," "proven"). Bridges design buyers and finance buyers.

**Mucho (philosophical):**

> "At Mucho, we bring a unique perspective to every project. More ways of looking. More ways of thinking. More curiosity to discover what is needed now. And imagination to create what's never been seen before."
>
> "We mean more."

Plays on the literal translation of "mucho." Pure positioning — no service description.

**&Walsh (focus on craft):**

> "A creative agency based in New York specializing in brand strategy, art direction, design and production across all platforms."

Minimal. Lets the work and Jessica Walsh's name do the lifting.

**Designjoy (productized — total clarity):**

> "Design subscriptions for everyone. One subscription to rule them all. Pause or cancel anytime."

The polar opposite of premium opacity. For productized, **clarity is the premium.**

### 14.2 About page that builds trust

Strong premium-agency about-page structure:

1. **Origin moment** (specific year, specific frustration that led to founding).
2. **Named principal(s)** with photo and credible past (former Pentagram, ex-IDEO, ex-Apple).
3. **Operating principle** (1–2 sentences that act as filter).
4. **Numbers that matter** (years, clients, NPS, repeat-client rate).
5. **A specific opinion** the agency holds that competitors don't.

### 14.3 Process page that earns the fee

A weak process page: "Discover → Define → Design → Deliver."

A strong process page: a named, sequenced method with artifacts shown for each stage, expected client involvement, and a clear "why this is different from how most agencies do it."

Bond's named programs (Shakeup, Slingshot, Ignite, And Action) earn fees because they're _products_, not stages.

### 14.4 Case study openers that grip

- "We worked with Acme Corp to redesign their brand identity."
- "In Q3 of 2024, Acme's CMO had 90 days to lift demo conversion before the Series B raise. We were given a 6-week window."

The second one starts mid-action. Stakes are visible. Time pressure is visible. Outcome is implied.

### 14.5 Pricing pages that pre-qualify

- **Total transparency** (Designjoy: "$4,995/mo, here's exactly what's included and excluded").
- **Floor + range** (Pia Silva: "$35–50k for a 1–3 day intensive").
- **"Starts at $X"** (most premium studios — pre-qualifies budget without committing to ceiling).
- **No price, with selectivity copy** (Pentagram-tier — implies "if budget is the issue, we're not your agency").

### 14.6 "Not for us" / disqualification copy

Template that converts:

> "We're not the right fit if: you need work in under 4 weeks, you're shopping primarily on price, you want unlimited revisions, you're not in [vertical], you need an agency that handles [excluded service]. If any of those apply, [competitor name(s)] do excellent work and we're happy to introduce you."

That last sentence — recommending competitors — is the highest-trust move in the industry. Almost no one does it.

### 14.7 First reply to an inbound lead (template)

Enns-style premium reply:

```
Thanks for reaching out, [name].

Before I suggest a call, three quick questions to make sure we're a fit:

1. What's the problem you're trying to solve in [your business],
   in one sentence?
2. What's the cost to you of NOT solving it in the next 6–12 months?
3. Roughly what range are you working with — sub-$50k, $50–150k,
   $150k+, or open?

If we look like a fit, I'll send a 30-min slot to talk through scope
and process. If not, I'll point you to 2–3 studios who'd be a better
match.

Thanks,
[Name]
```

This single email pre-qualifies on problem-clarity, urgency, and budget — three things that take three calls to surface otherwise.

### 14.8 Trellis-relevant hero copy patterns

For a brand-design studio at Trellis' tier, signal directions to test:

- **Founder-led + restraint:** "Trellis is a small design studio. We build brand identities for founders who think the visible 10% is what matters and don't realize it's the invisible 90% that pays back."
- **Specialization-led:** "Brand identity for B2B SaaS at Series A–B. Six engagements per year. No websites, no decks, no exceptions."
- **Process-led:** "The 6-week brand sprint. Strategy, identity, and launch system. One named partner from start to finish."
- **Outcome-led:** "Brand identities that pay back in conversion. Our last 4 SaaS rebrands lifted demo conversion 22–47% within 6 months of launch."

Pick one. Don't try to do all four.

## 16. The 10-Point Pre-Engagement Checklist

Before sending any proposal, verify:

- [ ] You've had at least one full **diagnosis call** (not just qualification).
- [ ] You know the **cost of inaction** in the client's own words.
- [ ] You've stated a **verbal price floor** in the call and they didn't flinch.
- [ ] You know **who else needs to weigh in** — and they've been named.
- [ ] You know the **decision timeline** and the consequence of missing it.
- [ ] You have a **named champion** internally who will sell the project up.
- [ ] You've already decided your **walk-away number** and your three tiers.
- [ ] You have a **case study** that mirrors their problem closely enough to forward.
- [ ] You've identified at least one **competitor you'd genuinely refer them to** if it's not a fit — and you know it isn't a threat to mention them.
- [ ] You are **comfortable losing this deal at the price you're going to name.**

The last point is the most important. The agencies that win premium deals are the agencies that walk away from non-premium deals. Pricing power is downstream of capacity-to-decline.
