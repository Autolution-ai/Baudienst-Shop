## Why This Matters Most

**Most underperforming copy targets a Stage-4 audience with Stage-1 copy, or a Stage-1 audience with Stage-4 copy.** Stage-4 prospects (who already know your product) bounce when you re-explain the category they researched a week ago. Stage-1 prospects (who don't feel the problem yet) bounce when you open with an offer for a pain they haven't named. Schwartz's claim — that misjudging the awareness stage is the #1 reason promotions fail — has held for 60 years. Before any other decision (framework, lead type, headline length, CTA placement), diagnose where the reader sits on the two scales: awareness (what they know) and sophistication (what the market has heard). Everything downstream follows from this.

> "The power that makes advertising work comes from the market itself, not from the copy." — Schwartz, _Breakthrough Advertising_ (1966)

## The 5 Awareness Stages

The awareness scale is about the **prospect's individual state of knowledge**. It moves left-to-right as a prospect learns: they feel pain → discover solutions exist → compare options → know your product → are ready to buy.

### Stage 1 — Unaware

**What the prospect knows:** Nothing. They don't feel a problem, don't recognize a need, don't know your category exists. They are not searching for anything related to your product.

**Lead with:** A story, news event, identity hook, or contrarian observation that makes them feel something they hadn't felt before. The lead must _create the felt-problem_ before any product mention.

**Headline formats that work:**

- Story / curiosity ("They laughed when I sat down at the piano…")
- News / proclamation ("A new discovery from MIT changes how we…")
- Identity hook ("If you're a founder over 35…")
- Contrarian ("Why everything you've been told about sleep is wrong")

**Lead type (Ford's 6):** Story, Big Secret, or Proclamation.

**Common failure mode:** Pitching the product to a non-problem-feeling prospect. They bounce in <3 seconds because nothing in the headline maps to anything they care about.

**Examples:**

- WSJ "Two Young Men" letter (1975) — Stage-1 readers didn't know they had a "knowledge gap" problem until the parable revealed it
- Halbert's "Coat of Arms" letter (1971) — recipients didn't know they wanted a family crest until the personal letter arrived
- A modern privacy tool's "What your phone learned about you yesterday" headline — surfaces a problem the reader didn't know they had

---

### Stage 2 — Problem-Aware

**What the prospect knows:** They feel the pain. They have a name for it. They do NOT know solutions exist (or assume nothing works).

**Lead with:** A problem-led headline that names their pain in their own words, agitates it (cost, consequences, what happens if ignored), and _then_ teases the existence of a solution. Don't reveal the solution in the headline — agitation buys attention.

**Headline formats that work:**

- Question ("Do you make these 5 mistakes in [topic]?")
- Negative / warning ("Warning: most [category] users are losing $X per month")
- Problem-amplification ("Why your [thing] keeps [bad outcome] — and what to do about it")

**Lead type:** Problem-Solution.

**Common failure mode:** Jumping to the solution before agitating. Reader thinks "yeah, but I'm not convinced this is actually that bad" and leaves. Stage 2 readers under-buy when under-agitated.

**Examples:**

- Caples' "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano" (1925) — Stage 2 (wants social validation, knows current state is embarrassing)
- A modern HR tech headline: "Why your best engineers are quietly interviewing — and how to spot it before they resign"
- A health funnel: "If you're tired all afternoon despite 8 hours of sleep, this might be why"

---

### Stage 3 — Solution-Aware

**What the prospect knows:** Solutions exist in the category. They're comparing options. They do NOT know yours — or know the name but not the differentiator.

**Lead with:** Category-level differentiation. Introduce your mechanism (the _how_ that makes you different). This is the stage where mechanism beats claim. You're not selling weight loss — you're selling weight loss via _this specific protocol_.

**Headline formats that work:**

- Mechanism-led ("The [unusual mechanism] that lets you [outcome]")
- Contrarian-vs-category ("Forget [common category solution]. Do this instead.")
- "Better than" comparison ("Why [N] [users] switched from [competitor category] to [yours]")

**Lead type:** Big Secret or Promise (mechanism-backed).

**Common failure mode:** Feature-selling instead of category-selling. Stage 3 readers don't care about your features — they care about the _one thing_ that makes you categorically different. Leading with a feature list reads as "another one of those."

**Examples:**

- Linear vs Jira: "Purpose-built for product teams" — buyer arrived solution-aware ("Jira alternative"); mechanism implied is the specific differentiator
- Magic Spoon: "Cereal that tastes like childhood, with the macros of a protein shake" — mechanism-as-promise vs the cereal category
- Notion: "One tool. Every team. Infinite possibilities." — re-frames the category around consolidation

---

### Stage 4 — Product-Aware

**What the prospect knows:** Your product specifically. They've been on your site, read reviews, maybe started a trial. They're on the fence.

**Lead with:** Offer, proof, risk-reversal, urgency. The reader doesn't need education — they need a reason to act now. Re-explaining basics insults them.

**Headline formats that work:**

- Direct offer ("Get [outcome] in [time], or your money back")
- Risk reversal ("Try it free for 30 days — no card required")
- Specific proof ("[Named peer] saved $[X] with [product] in [time]. Here's the case study.")
- Urgency ("Pricing changes on [date] — lock in current rate")

**Lead type:** Offer or Promise (with proof stack).

**Common failure mode:** Re-explaining what the product is and why the category matters. Stage 4 readers are _past_ that — they're stuck on a specific objection (price, fit, timing, risk). Address the objection, don't recap the pitch.

**Examples:**

- Cart-abandon email: "You left a [product] in your cart — here's $20 off if you finish today"
- Trial-expiry email: "3 days left on your trial. Need a 7-day extension? Reply 'yes.'"
- SaaS pricing page: "Annual saves 20%. Average team breaks even in 6 weeks. See the math."

---

### Stage 5 — Most-Aware

**What the prospect knows:** Everything. They're a fan, a past buyer, an existing customer, or someone who's just waiting for the right moment.

**Lead with:** Direct offer with first-name familiarity. Discount, deadline, restock notice, new launch, loyalty perk. The lead is the offer itself — no preamble.

**Headline formats that work:**

- Restock / launch ("It's back. [Product] returns [date].")
- Loyalty / first-name ("Sarah — your VIP access opens at noon")
- FOMO ("Last 12 left. Closes Friday.")
- Direct discount ("48 hours: 25% off everything")

**Lead type:** Offer (lean, no setup).

**Common failure mode:** Selling like a stranger. Stage 5 readers feel insulted by re-introduction copy. Use first name, reference past behavior, drop the pitch.

**Examples:**

- Apple product launch emails to existing customers — single product hero, no explanation of what Apple makes
- A SaaS upgrade email: "Your usage is 3× the Pro tier limit this month. Upgrade and we'll refund the overage."
- A creator's launch email to their list: "It's live. Doors close Sunday at midnight." (No further context — list already knows.)

## The 5 Sophistication Stages

The sophistication scale is about the **market's collective state**, not the individual prospect. It measures how many times the market has heard claims in your category. You can't drag a Stage-5 market back to Stage-1 copy — the market sets the rules.

### Stage 1 — Virgin Market

**Market state:** Your product is the first in the category. No one has heard the claim before. The basic promise is novel.

**Winning move:** Make the **direct claim**, plainly stated. No mechanism needed — the claim itself is the breakthrough.

**Historical example:** Early Listerine ("Kills germs that cause bad breath") — first to claim it; Reeves' early USP era; early air travel ads ("Fly from NYC to LA in hours, not days").

**Modern equivalent:** Genuinely novel categories — early ChatGPT positioning ("Talk to an AI"), early Tesla ("An electric car you actually want to drive"), Cursor's early launch ("AI pair programmer in your IDE"). Rare. If you think you're in a Virgin market, you're probably wrong — recheck.

---

### Stage 2 — Enlarged Claim

**Market state:** Competitors have entered. The basic claim no longer surprises. Buyers now compare claims numerically.

**Winning move:** **Bigger, faster, more.** Specifics, numbers, magnitudes. Out-claim the field with concrete promise.

**Historical example:** Weight-loss ads moving from "Lose weight" to "Lose 30 lbs in 30 days." Razor wars moving from "Sharp" to "Now with two blades." Cereal claims: "Now with twice the vitamins."

**Modern equivalent:** Early productivity SaaS arms-race ("10× faster," "100 templates," "Unlimited users"). Most AI tools currently live here ("Generate 1,000 emails in 60 seconds"). When everyone is claim-stacking, the market is at Stage 2.

---

### Stage 3 — Mechanism

**Market state:** Claims have hit a ceiling. Prospects are skeptical of any new promise. "Yeah right, another one."

**Winning move:** Reveal the **mechanism** — the specific _how_ that makes your claim believable. A new ingredient, process, system, framework, or proprietary approach.

**Historical example:** "Lose weight with ketosis" (vs. "Lose weight"); Listerine moving to oils that target specific bacteria; "The Pomodoro Technique" naming a system; "The South Beach Diet."

**Modern equivalent:** Linear's "purpose-built for product teams" (mechanism = product-team-focused workflow vs Jira's everything-tool); Whoop's "continuous strain & recovery tracking" (mechanism = recovery science); Eight Sleep's "active grounding + thermal regulation" (mechanism implied by proprietary tech). Most B2B SaaS lives here in 2026.

---

### Stage 4 — Enlarged Mechanism

**Market state:** Your mechanism has been copied. Competitors now claim "ketosis" too. The how-it-works is no longer differentiating.

**Winning move:** **Better, faster, easier mechanism** — or stack mechanisms. Add a twist that makes yours superior to the now-commoditized version.

**Historical example:** "Intermittent ketosis"; "OMAD ketogenic protocol"; "Targeted ketosis using exogenous ketones." The mechanism gets enlarged the same way the claim did at Stage 2.

**Modern equivalent:** AI tools that started at "AI writes for you" (Stage 3) now claim "AI trained on YOUR brand voice" (Stage 4); productivity tools that started with "AI assistant" now claim "AI agent that takes action across apps." Fitness moving from "HIIT" to "Zone 2 + HIIT periodization." When competitors are claiming variations of the same mechanism, you're at Stage 4.

---

### Stage 5 — Identification

**Market state:** Market is saturated. Buyers are cynical about every claim and every mechanism. Differentiation by feature is impossible.

**Winning move:** Sell **who they become**. Lifestyle, tribe, identity, values. The product is the price of admission to a way of being.

**Historical example:** Marlboro Country (cowboy identity, not cigarettes); Apple "Think Different" (creative-rebel identity); Patagonia (environmentalist identity); Rolls-Royce (the buyer is "a Rolls-Royce person").

**Modern equivalent:** Liquid Death (anti-establishment, anti-bottled-water politics, sense of humor); Tracksmith (heritage runner identity); Tesla (early-adopter futurist identity); Hims/Hers (modern person who handles their stuff); Patagonia (still). When the product could be anything but the buyer is buying the meaning, you're at Stage 5.

---

### The Core Rule

You can't drag a Stage-5 market back to Stage-1 copy. The market sets the rules. **Diagnose the market, then write to the stage.** A Stage-3 mechanism claim in a Stage-5 market reads as desperate. A Stage-5 identity claim in a Stage-1 market reads as confusing. Match the stage.

## Mass Desire — Channel, Don't Create

Schwartz's foundational claim:

> "Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already existing desires onto a particular product."

**You are not creating want. You are channeling existing want onto your product.** Diagnose mass desire _before_ writing. If the desire isn't already there in the market, no copy will save you.

### Schwartz's 3 Characteristics of Mass Desire

| Characteristic    | Question to Ask                                | Strong Signal                       | Weak Signal                     |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| **Urgency**       | How acutely does the audience feel this _now_? | Pain shows up in their week / month | Pain is hypothetical or distant |
| **Staying Power** | Does it persist over time, or fade?            | Has been a desire for years/decades | Trend-driven, fad-shaped        |
| **Scope**         | How many people share it?                      | Millions feel it the same way       | Niche of a niche                |

The strongest products plug into desires that score high on all three. Weight loss, status, financial security, time, romantic appeal, parental safety, peer respect — these are perennial. Diagnose which mass desire you channel; everything else is mechanism around that core pull.

### Operational Test — Diagnosing Mass Desire

Before writing, answer:

1. **What desire is already alive in the market that this product channels?** (Name it in one sentence — "the desire to look competent to peers without looking like they're trying too hard.")
2. **Where do you see this desire surfacing in the wild?** (Reddit threads, support tickets, sales calls, 3-star reviews — the desire shows up in others' words.)
3. **Is the desire strong enough that the prospect would seek a solution unprompted?** If yes, you have channelable mass desire. If no, you have a product looking for a problem.

If you can't answer (1) crisply, stop writing and do more research.

## The Awareness × Sophistication Matrix

The two scales combine. Awareness tells you what the _individual prospect_ knows; sophistication tells you what the _market_ has heard. Both must be matched simultaneously.

|                                  | **Sophistication 1 (Virgin)**              | **Sophistication 2 (Enlarged Claim)**   | **Sophistication 3 (Mechanism)**                                         | **Sophistication 4 (Enlarged Mechanism)**          | **Sophistication 5 (Identification)**                                      |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Awareness 1 (Unaware)**        | Story → reveal new category → direct claim | Story → reveal category → bigger claim  | Story → reveal mechanism that solves a problem they didn't know they had | Story → reveal _better_ mechanism                  | Story → identity reveal ("people like you are doing this")                 |
| **Awareness 2 (Problem-Aware)**  | Problem agitation → direct claim as relief | Agitate problem → bigger-claim solution | Agitate problem → introduce your mechanism as the _how_                  | Agitate problem → enlarged mechanism vs status quo | Agitate problem → identity solution ("the kind of person who solves this") |
| **Awareness 3 (Solution-Aware)** | Category framing → direct claim            | Category framing → bigger claim         | **Sweet spot:** mechanism-led differentiation                            | Mechanism comparison → why yours is better         | Identity-led differentiation in category                                   |
| **Awareness 4 (Product-Aware)**  | Offer + proof                              | Offer + bigger-number proof             | Offer + mechanism reinforcement                                          | Offer + mechanism-comparison proof                 | Offer + identity reinforcement ("you're already part of this")             |
| **Awareness 5 (Most-Aware)**     | Direct offer                               | Direct offer with specifics             | Direct offer (mechanism is internalized)                                 | Direct offer (no need to compare)                  | Direct offer with identity flag ("VIP / member / insider")                 |

### How to read the matrix

- **Diagonal sweet spots:** Stage-N awareness in a Stage-N sophistication market is the natural fit. A Solution-Aware reader in a Mechanism market wants mechanism-led copy.
- **Most common B2B SaaS landing page in 2026:** Awareness 3 × Sophistication 3 — solution-aware buyer comparing options in a mechanism-driven market. Mechanism-led copy wins here.
- **Most common DTC luxury page:** Awareness 1–3 × Sophistication 5 — buyer arrives at varying awareness, but the market demands identity-led copy. Lead with identity, layer in story.
- **Most common conversion page failure:** Awareness 4 × Sophistication 3 copy applied to Awareness 1 readers — i.e., re-explaining basics with mechanism detail when the reader hasn't even felt the problem yet.

## The Awareness × Persona Matrix

This is the load-bearing insight in the corpus: **Schwartz awareness stage × buyer role**. A Stage-5 Boomer behaves more like a Stage-5 Gen Z than like a Stage-2 Boomer. **Sort by awareness first, role second, demographics third.**

| Role                             | Typical Entry Stage | Time Horizon  | Top Objection                           | Copy Implication                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------- | ------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **CEO**                          | 1–3                 | <5 min/touch  | "Why now / why us"                      | Story or news lead; peer-CEO proof in first 10 sec; business-impact framing                       |
| **CFO**                          | 2–4                 | weeks         | "Show me the math"                      | Problem-agitation → ROI mechanism → conservative claims; transparent contract terms               |
| **CTO / CIO**                    | 3–4                 | weeks–months  | "Will this break / scale / secure"      | Mechanism-led; architecture detail; certifications; "trust us" loses                              |
| **Champion (internal advocate)** | 2–4                 | weeks         | "How do I sell this internally"         | Enable-the-champion content: pitch deck, ROI calc with their variables, named CS contact          |
| **End User**                     | 2–4                 | hours–days    | "Will I use this every day"             | Solution-aware UX-led copy; mechanism = ergonomics; daily-use proof                               |
| **Procurement**                  | 4–5                 | weeks         | "Best TCO, lowest risk"                 | Direct offer + transparent pricing + pre-filled security questionnaires                           |
| **Founder (early-stage)**        | 3–5                 | hours–days    | "Trust + speed"                         | Mechanism + free tier / no-card trial; founder-to-founder voice                                   |
| **Founder (Series B+)**          | 2–4                 | days–weeks    | "Will this scale + integrate"           | Mechanism + enterprise proof; consolidation framing                                               |
| **Mid-manager**                  | 2–4                 | days–weeks    | "Will I look smart"                     | Problem-agitation + peer-named case studies + ROI for their boss                                  |
| **IC developer**                 | 4–5                 | minutes–hours | "Trust + integration + docs"            | Direct: free tier, public GitHub, working API, no marketing-speak                                 |
| **Freelancer / Solopreneur**     | 3–5                 | minutes–days  | "Worth the monthly cost"                | Mechanism + ROI in their own currency (clients won, hours saved); under-$50/mo psychological line |
| **Boomer consumer**              | 1–3                 | days–weeks    | "Can I trust this brand"                | Story or problem lead; traditional trust signals; longer-form welcomed; no condescension          |
| **Gen Z consumer**               | 1–3                 | seconds–hours | "Is this authentic + aligned w/ values" | Story or identity lead; UGC > polished; values-coded; mobile-first; TikTok-native                 |
| **Millennial consumer**          | 2–4                 | hours–weeks   | "Have peers I trust used this"          | Problem-agitation + transparent reviews; transformation framing; sustainability-coded             |
| **Gen X consumer**               | 3–4                 | days–weeks    | "Show me proof, not hype"               | Solution-aware + mechanism; substance > influencer marketing; skepticism-respecting               |

### Operational rule

**ALWAYS diagnose stage and role before writing.** Most underperforming B2B copy assumes Stage 4 ("you know our product, here's the demo") when the buyer arrives at Stage 2 ("I know I have a problem, what category solves it?"). Most underperforming DTC luxury copy assumes Stage 2 ("here's the problem this solves") when the buyer is at Stage 5 ("show me who I'd become").

## Schwartz's 7 Techniques of Breakthrough Copy

When a market reaches Stage 3+ and standard claims fail, Schwartz prescribes seven techniques to break through. These are not stylistic flourishes — they are mechanical levers for increasing the _force_ of copy without raising the loudness.

| Technique                       | Mechanism                                                                                               | Copy Example                                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Intensification**          | Make the existing claim more vivid, more specific, more visceral. Same promise, sharpened.              | "Lose weight" → "Watch the scale drop 7 lbs in your first week — and feel your jeans loosen by Friday."                |
| **2. Identification**           | Tie the product to who the reader _is_ or wants to _be_. (The Stage-5 sophistication move.)             | "The way founders who hit $10M ARR actually run their week."                                                           |
| **3. Gradualization**           | Break a big, hard-to-believe promise into a chain of small, believable steps. Each step earns the next. | "First, you'll notice you're not hungry by 10am. By week 2, the afternoon crash is gone. By week 4…"                   |
| **4. Redefinition**             | Reframe the category itself. Change what the buyer thinks they're buying.                               | "We're not a CRM. We're a system of action." (Attio); "It's not coffee. It's a focus protocol."                        |
| **5. Mechanization**            | Reveal the mechanism — name the _how_, make it tangible, make it proprietary-feeling.                   | "We use a 3-stage cold extraction process called Slow Bloom™ that preserves 4× the antioxidants."                      |
| **6. Concentration**            | Strip everything that isn't the core promise. Force the reader to feel one thing only.                  | A landing page with one sentence, one visual, one CTA. "Your inbox. Empty by 9am. Every day."                          |
| **7. Camouflage (advertorial)** | Make the copy not look like copy. Editorial format, native ad, personal letter, "story" framing.        | Halbert's "Coat of Arms" letter (read as personal genealogy research); modern: long-form Substack-style sales letters. |

### When to use each

- **Stage 1 sophistication:** Direct claim only — no technique needed.
- **Stage 2:** Intensification.
- **Stage 3:** Mechanization + Redefinition.
- **Stage 4:** Gradualization + intensified mechanism.
- **Stage 5:** Identification + Camouflage.

Concentration applies everywhere.

## Diagnostic Flowchart — Identifying Stage

Before writing, run the diagnostic. **Awareness is about the individual prospect; sophistication is about the market.** Diagnose both.

### Awareness Diagnostic — 5 Signal Tests

| Signal                                             | What to look at                                                                                                                                                                                                     | Stage Reading        |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Where they came from**                           | Traffic source: organic search for problem terms ("why am I tired all the time") → Stage 2. Branded search ("[your product] pricing") → Stage 4. Direct / email list → Stage 5. Cold ad to cold audience → Stage 1. | Maps directly        |
| **What they searched**                             | "How to [outcome]" → Stage 2–3. "[Category] vs [category]" → Stage 3. "[Competitor A] vs [Competitor B]" → Stage 3–4. "[Your product] reviews" → Stage 4. "[Your product] discount code" → Stage 5.                 | Maps directly        |
| **What they say in support tickets / sales calls** | "I didn't know I needed this until…" → Stage 1. "I've been dealing with this for months…" → Stage 2. "I tried [competitor] but…" → Stage 3. "I'm ready to buy but [objection]…" → Stage 4.                          | Maps directly        |
| **Language they use**                              | Generic ("I want to feel better") → Stage 1–2. Category-specific ("I need a CRM") → Stage 3. Brand-specific ("Does [product] integrate with [tool]?") → Stage 4. Insider ("the v2 release") → Stage 5.              | Maps directly        |
| **Their behavior on-site**                         | Bounce <10 sec from homepage → too cold for current copy (likely Stage 1–2 arriving at Stage 3–4 copy). Time-on-page >2 min on pricing → Stage 4. Repeat visits over 7+ days → Stage 4–5.                           | Diagnoses _mismatch_ |

### Sophistication Diagnostic — 4 Signal Tests

| Signal                                               | What to look at                                                                                                                                                                                            | Stage Reading                                   |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Number of competitors making similar claims**      | 0–1 competitors → Stage 1. A few, all claiming the same → Stage 2. Mechanism-led competitors emerging → Stage 3. Mechanism-comparison wars → Stage 4. Identity-led market leaders → Stage 5.               | Maps directly                                   |
| **Buyer skepticism in reviews / forums**             | "Sounds too good to be true" repeated → Stage 2 fatigue, move to Stage 3. "All of these tools are the same" → Stage 3 fatigue, move to Stage 4. "Just marketing fluff" → Stage 4 fatigue, move to Stage 5. | Diagnoses where the market is _moving_          |
| **Where category leaders compete**                   | On the claim → Stage 1–2. On the mechanism (named, branded process) → Stage 3. On variations of mechanism → Stage 4. On lifestyle, identity, brand-as-tribe → Stage 5.                                     | Read the leaders, mirror their stage            |
| **What advertising looks like in adjacent channels** | Direct-response ad copy still claim-heavy → Stage 2. Mechanism-led teardowns dominant → Stage 3. Identity-led brand films → Stage 5.                                                                       | Adjacent channels often reveal the market stage |

### Quick diagnostic decision tree

```
Is the prospect actively searching for a solution?
├── No (no search, didn't seek you out) → AWARENESS 1
└── Yes
    ├── Searching pain terms ("why does X happen") → AWARENESS 2
    ├── Searching category / "how to" / alternatives → AWARENESS 3
    ├── Searching your brand specifically → AWARENESS 4
    └── On your list / past customer / using product → AWARENESS 5

How crowded is your category with similar claims?
├── Empty / you are first → SOPHISTICATION 1
├── 2–5 competitors, all making the same claim → SOPHISTICATION 2
├── Competitors differentiating on named mechanisms → SOPHISTICATION 3
├── Mechanism variations / "ours is better because…" wars → SOPHISTICATION 4
└── Saturated; leaders sell lifestyle / identity / tribe → SOPHISTICATION 5
```

## Common Diagnostic Mistakes

| Mistake                                                             | What it looks like                                                                                                                            | The fix                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Diagnosing awareness from your perspective, not theirs**          | "Obviously they know they need [thing]" — when they don't. Founder bias.                                                                      | Read 30 cold-traffic comments / 10 sales call transcripts / 20 support tickets before writing. Use _their_ language as the awareness signal.    |
| **Treating awareness as a property of the segment, not the moment** | Assuming all readers from the same persona are at the same stage.                                                                             | Diagnose by _traffic source and message_, not by persona. Same CEO is Stage 1 on a cold LinkedIn ad and Stage 4 on a comparison page.           |
| **Confusing market sophistication with prospect awareness**         | "The market is sophisticated, so I'll write Stage 5 identity copy" — to a Stage 1 cold prospect who has no idea what the category is.         | Match BOTH scales simultaneously. Sophistication tells you the _style_ (identity vs mechanism vs claim); awareness tells you the _entry point_. |
| **Writing Stage 4 copy for Stage 2 traffic**                        | Landing page leads with offer + risk-reversal + urgency to readers who don't yet feel the problem. Pricing-page-as-homepage.                  | If traffic is cold, lead with story or problem agitation. Save the offer for the second screen and beyond.                                      |
| **Writing Stage 1 copy for Stage 4 traffic**                        | Re-explaining the category to readers who've been on your pricing page 3 times. "What is [your product]?" headline to a returning visitor.    | Use behavioral signals (return visits, referral source, list membership) to skip the basics. Personalize by stage.                              |
| **Mistaking a Stage-2 market for Stage-1**                          | Claiming you're "the first" in a category that already has 5 competitors. Reads as ignorant or dishonest.                                     | Audit the category honestly. If you can name 2+ competitors making similar claims, you are not Stage 1.                                         |
| **Mistaking a Stage-5 market for Stage-3**                          | Mechanism-stacking in a category where buyers have stopped caring about mechanism (e.g., cigarettes, bottled water, generic SaaS categories). | If category leaders sell identity, sell identity. Mechanism reads as desperate.                                                                 |
| **Trying to create mass desire instead of channeling it**           | Writing copy meant to _convince_ the reader they want something. Hours spent on a clever headline for a desire that isn't there.              | Pre-write step: name the mass desire (3-characteristic test). If you can't, kill the project or redefine the offer.                             |
| **Diagnosing once and never re-diagnosing**                         | Stage-3 copy that ran for 18 months and now under-performs because the market moved to Stage 4.                                               | Re-diagnose every quarter. Sophistication moves _up_ over time, never down. Awareness shifts as your marketing matures the market.              |
| **Letting persona override stage**                                  | "This is a CEO, so we'll go Stage 1 story-driven for everyone." Even Stage-4 CEOs exist (the CEO who's been to your demo).                    | Stage trumps persona. A Stage-4 CEO wants offer + proof, not a story they already read.                                                         |

### The single most expensive mistake

**Writing to Stage 4 because the founder is Stage 4.** Founders know their product cold. They forget that 80% of cold traffic doesn't. Most homepages read like Stage-4 product-aware copy because the writer is Stage 4. Cold traffic bounces. The fix: imagine the reader as a friend-of-a-friend who's heard the company name once and remembers nothing about it. Write for that person first; let warmer audiences self-route to deeper pages.

## Recap — The Pre-Write Checklist

Before writing a single headline, answer:

1. **Awareness stage of the typical reader (1–5)?** Diagnosed from traffic source, search terms, on-site behavior, sales-call language.
2. **Market sophistication stage (1–5)?** Diagnosed from competitor count, claim repetition, where category leaders compete, buyer skepticism in reviews.
3. **Primary mass desire being channeled?** Named in one sentence, with urgency × staying power × scope rated.
4. **Persona (role × life-stage)?** Used to refine voice, objections, proof formats — _after_ stage is locked.
5. **Which Schwartz technique applies?** Match sophistication stage → technique table above.
6. **What is the lead type?** Story / Problem-Solution / Promise / Big Secret / Proclamation / Offer — matched to awareness stage.

If you can't answer these crisply, you don't have enough research yet. Do not write. Go back to the source material. **Read 3× more than you'll write.**
