## 1. Martin Conroy — WSJ "Two Young Men" Letter (1975)

Estimated $2 billion in subscription revenue over 28 years. The single most successful direct-mail letter in publishing history. Ran nearly unchanged from 1975 to 2003.

### 1.1 Original Text (verbatim)

> Dear Reader:
>
> On a beautiful late spring afternoon, twenty-five years ago, two young men graduated from the same college. They were very much alike, these two young men. Both had been better than average students, both were personable and both — as young college graduates are — were filled with ambitious dreams for the future.
>
> Recently, these men returned to their college for their 25th reunion.
>
> They were still very much alike. Both were happily married. Both had three children. And both, it turned out, had gone to work for the same Midwestern manufacturing company after graduation, and were still there.
>
> But there was a difference. One of the men was manager of a small department of that company. The other was its president.
>
> **What Made The Difference**
>
> Have you ever wondered, as I have, what makes this kind of difference in people's lives? It isn't a native intelligence or talent or dedication. It isn't that one person wants success and the other doesn't.
>
> The difference lies in what each person knows and how he or she makes use of that knowledge.
>
> And that is why I am writing to you and to people like you about The Wall Street Journal.
>
> [...]
>
> About those two college classmates I mention at the beginning of this letter: they were graduated from college together and together got started in the business world. So what made their lives in business different?
>
> Knowledge. Useful knowledge. And its application.
>
> [...]
>
> I cannot promise you that success will be instantly yours if you start reading The Wall Street Journal. But I can guarantee that you will find The Journal always interesting, always reliable, and always useful.
>
> Sincerely, Publisher
>
> P.S. It's important to note that The Journal's subscription price may be tax deductible. Ask your tax advisor.

Source: Dow Jones PDF reproduction (skillshare s3) + https://swiped.co/file/wallstreet-letter-conroy/

### 1.2 Paragraph-by-Paragraph Dissection

**"On a beautiful late spring afternoon, twenty-five years ago…"** — Conroy buries the lede on purpose. No product, no offer, no benefit, no reader. He opens with fairy-tale cadence ("On a beautiful…") that triggers narrative mode before sales resistance can fire. Specificity ("twenty-five years," "late spring") creates a vivid mental movie. He is _bypassing the gatekeeper_. The reader is decoding a story, not a pitch.

**"They were very much alike, these two young men."** — Inverted syntax ("these two young men" coming last) is a literary marker. Loading the slot for the inevitable difference. Every "alike" raises the prediction error when the reveal hits.

**"Both had been better than average students, both were personable and both — as young college graduates are — were filled with ambitious dreams for the future."** — The mirror selection is the heaviest lift in the letter: he eliminates innate talent, charm, and ambition as the explanation. By the time the reveal lands, the only available causal slot left is _something they did with information_. The em-dashed parenthetical "as young college graduates are" is the _reader-induction line_. The reader nods, "yes, that's true," and now identifies with both men.

**"Recently, these men returned to their college for their 25th reunion."** — One sentence on its own line. White space as a beat. Twenty-five years compressed into a comma.

**"They were still very much alike. Both were happily married. Both had three children. And both, it turned out, had gone to work for the same Midwestern manufacturing company..."** — Triple "Both / Both / both." Rule of three used to lull. He's removed every variable. The reader's brain is now physically itching for the difference because Conroy has set up an A/B test in which only _one_ variable can be allowed to vary.

**"But there was a difference. One of the men was manager of a small department of that company. The other was its president."** — The seven most famous words of 20th-century direct mail: "But there was a difference." He doesn't say _what_ the difference is. He says _that_ there is one, then makes you wait one beat. "Manager of a small department" / "its president." Maximum contrast inside a sentence. Not "VP" or "head of marketing": the two roles an average reader can most viscerally rank.

**Subhead: "What Made The Difference"** — Conroy hands the reader's own question back to them as a subhead. _The_ model for the open-loop subhead.

**"Have you ever wondered, as I have…"** — First "you." First "I." The narrative voice zooms out and becomes a thoughtful peer. "As I have" is the rapport line. _We are the same kind of person who thinks about this._

**"It isn't a native intelligence or talent or dedication. It isn't that one person wants success…"** — Pattern: "It isn't… It isn't…" eliminates the reader's likely self-defeating explanations. This is _belief surgery_ before the offer. He's pre-clearing the objections "I'm just not as smart" or "I don't have the drive." If those weren't the variables, the reader has no excuse not to acquire what _was_.

**"The difference lies in what each person knows and how he or she makes use of that knowledge."** — The Big Idea, 18 words. "What each person knows" comes first. Knowledge as a _commodity you can acquire_. "How he or she makes use of that knowledge": the implementation half makes it credible.

**"And that is why I am writing to you and to people like you about The Wall Street Journal."** — Product name introduced exactly here. About 220 words in. Halbert called this _earning the right to make the offer_.

**"About those two college classmates I mention at the beginning of this letter…"** — Callback. Returns to the story to close the loop. The single most copied move in long-form copy.

**"Knowledge. Useful knowledge. And its application."** — Sentence fragments. Hammer-strike rhythm. The line a reader remembers a week later.

**"I cannot promise you that success will be instantly yours…"** — _The honesty pivot._ Refuses to promise the moon, and that disclaimer is what makes the rest believable. Halbert's "concession close" before Halbert formalized it.

**"P.S. It's important to note that The Journal's subscription price may be tax deductible."** — The most boring P.S. imaginable. It works because it's so functional. After a story, a literal tax tip is a _bid for serious adult intent_. Creates a tiny last-second rationalization.

### 1.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension                | Reading                                                                                                                                                    |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Schwartz awareness stage | 2–3 (problem-aware, somewhat solution-aware)                                                                                                               |
| Schwartz sophistication  | 3–4 (business news mature; differentiates via _new mechanism_ — knowledge as the variable)                                                                 |
| Primary framework        | Story Lead → Big Idea → Product as Embodiment → Proof → Callback                                                                                           |
| Cialdini principles      | Social proof (millions of readers), Authority (Publisher signature, "world's largest staff"), Consistency (reader nods at the story), Liking ("as I have") |
| Halbert specificity      | 7/10 (strong narrative specificity; weaker dollarized proof)                                                                                               |

### 1.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Construct a controlled experiment in story form, then sell your product as the controlled variable.** Conroy's letter is a 2×1 A/B test with a population of two. Same college, same company, same family. Only the outcome differs. The reader's brain runs the experiment and arrives at "knowledge" as the only available cause. By the time Conroy names the product, the reader has already concluded they need a knowledge instrument. Conroy just provides one.

Second timeless move: **delayed product mention**. 220 words of narrative before "Wall Street Journal." Modern direct-response writers panic at 220 words without the product. Conroy understood you cannot sell a product until the reader has accepted the _frame_ in which the product is the answer.

### 1.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 SaaS landing page)

**H1:** "Two engineers shipped on the same day. One got promoted. One got laid off."

**Subhead:** "They had the same experience, the same job, the same tickets. The difference was what they could see about their own code."

**Body:** "Most engineers we talk to think the difference between getting promoted and getting laid off is luck, hours worked, or what team you landed on. After looking at five years of engineering performance data from 200 companies, we know it isn't. The difference is signal: specifically, what each engineer knew about how their work was perceived, used, and connected to revenue. That's why we built [Product]…"

**P.S.:** "[Product] is an eligible engineering tool under most corporate L&D budgets. Check with your manager."

Same skeleton, modern surface.

## 2. Joe Karbo — "The Lazy Man's Way to Riches" (1973)

Full-page ad that ran for years, generated $10M+ in book sales, and made Karbo a cult figure among direct-response writers. Halbert and Sugarman both rate the headline in their top 10 of all time.

### 2.1 Original Text (canonical excerpts)

> **Headline:** The Lazy Man's Way to Riches
>
> **Deck:** Most People Are Too Busy Earning A Living To Make Any Money
>
> I used to work hard. The 18-hour days. The 7-day weeks. But I didn't start making big money until I did less — a lot less.
>
> For example, this ad took about two hours to write. With a little luck, it should earn me fifty, maybe a hundred thousand dollars.
>
> What's more, I'm going to ask you to send me $10 for something that'll cost me no more than 50 cents. […]
>
> Yet, I make this promise: Mail no money. […] I'll wait for my money until you've read my book.
>
> I have two boats and a Cadillac. All paid for. […]
>
> If, after reading the book for 30 days, you decide it's not worth the $10 you sent, just send it back. I'll return your check or money order — uncashed.

Sources: https://swiped.co/file/lazy-mans-way-to-riches-ad-by-joe-karbo/, http://www.copywriting1.com/2007/06/lazy-mans-way-to-riches.html

### 2.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**Headline: "The Lazy Man's Way to Riches."** — Three deliberate violations of period expectation: (1) the protestant work ethic was still cultural law in 1973, and "lazy" was an insult; (2) "lazy" + "riches" is a paradox the reader must resolve; (3) "Way" implies a single specific method. It weaponizes _shame inversion_. Every reader has felt lazy and felt guilty about it, and this headline tells them the guilt was misplaced.

**Deck: "Most People Are Too Busy Earning A Living To Make Any Money."** — Chiasmus (living/money inverted with money/living). The _Big Idea_ in one sentence: the path to wealth and the path to subsistence are _different paths_, not the same path traveled longer. It implicates the reader's current strategy as fundamentally wrong.

**"I used to work hard. The 18-hour days. The 7-day weeks."** — Sentence-fragment cadence. Not opening with a story arc; opening with proof of credentials _in the form of suffering_. Establishes "I am you" before "I am different from you."

**"But I didn't start making big money until I did less — a lot less."** — The conjunction "But" pivots the entire frame in five words. "A lot less" repeated as intensifier. The reader's brain demands the mechanism. _How do you make money by doing less?_ This is the open loop that powers the rest of the ad.

**"For example, this ad took about two hours to write. With a little luck, it should earn me fifty, maybe a hundred thousand dollars."** — The single most audacious move. He doesn't tell you that less effort produces more money; he _demonstrates it on the ad you're reading right now_. "Show, don't tell" applied to direct response. The ad becomes evidence of its own claim.

**"What's more, I'm going to ask you to send me $10 for something that'll cost me no more than 50 cents."** — Radical price transparency. By admitting his margins, Karbo defuses the #1 objection ("you're ripping me off"). This is _the candor close_: pre-empting suspicion by overstating the case against himself.

**"Yet, I make this promise: Mail no money."** — Third pivot in four sentences. Confession → admission → reversal → generosity.

**"I'll wait for my money until you've read my book."** — Risk reversal compressed into a single sentence. Not a money-back guarantee paragraph; the check itself won't be cashed.

**"I have two boats and a Cadillac. All paid for."** — Material proof in 8 words. "All paid for" is the second clause that gives it weight. Anyone can have toys; debt-free toys are a different claim. Halbert-grade specificity _before_ Halbert was famous for it.

### 2.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 1–2 (unaware that "working less" is a valid strategy)                                                                                 |
| Sophistication      | 4 (anti-positioning vs. saturated "work hard for riches" category)                                                                    |
| Primary framework   | Identity Reversal — existing identity ("hard worker") reframed as the obstacle                                                        |
| Cialdini            | Reciprocity (uncashed check), Authority (self-demonstration), Liking ("I am you"), Consistency, Scarcity (implicit special knowledge) |
| Halbert specificity | 9/10 (boats, Cadillac, $10, 50¢, 30 days, $100k, 2 hours, 18-hour days)                                                               |

### 2.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Use your offer as proof of your offer.** "This ad took two hours to write and will earn $100k" is the ad's own case study. The asset proves itself by existing. Apply this anywhere: a course on writing copy whose sales page is the best example of the course. A productivity tool whose onboarding completes in 90 seconds. A "less is more" brand whose homepage has 8 words.

### 2.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 productized service / course)

**H1:** "I quit my $400k engineering job two years ago. I now work 11 hours a week and make more."

**Subhead:** "Most engineers grind for the raise. The raise is the trap."

**First paragraph:** "This sales page took 90 minutes to write. If it does what the last one did, it'll sell about $120,000 worth of [course] this week, roughly what most engineers earn in a year of 50-hour weeks. The difference isn't talent. The difference is leverage…"

## 3. Gary Halbert — "Coat of Arms" Letter (1971)

Halbert's first big hit. Net revenue conservatively estimated at $7M+ in 1970s dollars. Built on a single insight: _people will pay for an artifact bearing their own name._

### 3.1 Original Text (canonical)

> Dear Mr. McDonald,
>
> Did you know that your family name was recorded with a coat-of-arms in ancient heraldic archives more than seven centuries ago?
>
> My husband and I discovered this while doing some research for some friends of ours who have the same last name as you do.
>
> We were so intrigued by what we learned that we sent away for a beautifully illustrated report on the history of the McDonald family, including a reproduction of the earliest McDonald coat-of-arms in full color.
>
> The report so delighted our friends that we have had a few extra copies made and we are now offering them to other people whose name is McDonald.
>
> If you are interested, please let us know right away as our supply is pretty slim.
>
> Just verify that we have your correct name and address and send the correct amount in cash or check for the number of reports you want.
>
> Sincerely, Nancy L. Halbert
>
> P.S. If you are ordering only one report, send two dollars ($2.00). Additional reports ordered at the same time and sent to the same address are one dollar each.

Sources: https://swiped.co/file/coat-of-arms-letter-by-gary-halbert/, https://hooshmand.net/the-nancy-l-halbert-heraldry-letter/

### 3.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**The envelope is the first line of copy.** Halbert hand-addressed envelopes, used real stamps (not metered postage), and used his wife's name. The letter starts being persuasive on the outside.

**"Dear Mr. McDonald,"** — The recipient's surname IS the product. The mail-merge here isn't a personalization trick. It's the entire creative concept.

**"Did you know that your family name was recorded with a coat-of-arms in ancient heraldic archives more than seven centuries ago?"** — One sentence, three persuasion devices: (1) Question lead engages answer-search; (2) "your" makes it about them; (3) "seven centuries" is temporal grandeur that pre-justifies the price. The reader's surname has just become a historical artifact.

**"My husband and I discovered this while doing some research for some friends of ours…"** — The casual "doing some research for some friends" is the _amateur disguise_. Critical: a professional heraldry company is suspect, but a couple who stumbled into research and made a few extra reports is harmless. Halbert engineered the persona before the offer.

**"…a beautifully illustrated report on the history of the McDonald family, including a reproduction of the earliest McDonald coat-of-arms in full color."** — "McDonald" appears twice in one sentence; the name as incantation. "Beautifully illustrated," "in full color": sensory specificity for a $2 product.

**"The report so delighted our friends that we have had a few extra copies made…"** — The offer, disguised as a favor. "A few extra copies" is scarcity without explicit scarcity language. Positioned as _something the couple did because their friends loved it_, not as a commercial enterprise.

**"…as our supply is pretty slim."** — "Pretty slim" is conversational. "Limited time only" would have shattered the amateur persona. _Registered_ scarcity rather than _staged_ scarcity.

**"Just verify that we have your correct name and address…"** — Subtly implies they already have it; that the recipient is already in the system, already validated.

**P.S. on price** — Halbert hid the price in the P.S. The body sells the romance; the P.S. handles transaction. Same separation as Conroy's tax-deduction P.S.

### 3.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                               |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 1 (no awareness of family-name reports as a category)                                                 |
| Sophistication      | 1 (virgin market — no competitors)                                                                    |
| Primary framework   | AIDA in purest form (Attention via name → Interest → Desire → $2 Action)                              |
| Cialdini            | Liking (homey voice), Scarcity ("slim supply"), Authority (heraldic archives), Identity (the surname) |
| Halbert specificity | 5/10 (the whole letter is one big specific: the name)                                                 |

### 3.4 What Makes It Timeless

**The recipient's identity, in their hands, is the ultimate selling proposition.** Halbert built a $300k/day business on the realization that people will pay for an artifact bearing their own name. Modern equivalents: personalized Spotify Wrapped, custom AI-generated case studies, year-in-review emails. The letter is also the founding document of _transactional intimacy_: making commercial mail look like a letter from a friend with a cool find.

### 3.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 personalized cold email)

> Subject: Did you know there's a $[YOUR_LAST_NAME] family business that opened in 1804?
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> I was researching family-business genealogy for a friend last week and stumbled into something for the [Last Name] surname I had to send you.
>
> The [Last Name] family appears in business records going back to the early 1800s: a forge in Pennsylvania, a printing press in Edinburgh, and a small spice trader in Naples, all under variants of your name.
>
> I had a beautiful one-page genealogical map drawn up for my friend and a few extras printed. Cost me about $4 to print one. If you want one, let me know in the next 48 hours. We have maybe a dozen left.
>
> [Real first name]

## 4. Gary Halbert — "Dollar Bill" Letter

Halbert's most-copied template after the Coat of Arms. The opener that put a real dollar bill at the top of the page and forced the prospect to read.

### 4.1 Original Text (opening)

> Dear Jim,
>
> As you can see, I have attached a nice, crisp dollar bill to the top of this letter. Why have I done this?
>
> Actually, there are two reasons:
>
> 1. I have something very important to tell you and I needed some way to make sure this letter would catch your attention.
> 2. Since what I have to say is about how to make a great deal of money, I figured that using a dollar bill to get your attention was rather appropriate.
>
> Now then, the important matter I have to tell you about is…

Source: https://swiped.co/file/famous-dollar-letter-by-gary-halbert/

### 4.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**The dollar bill itself is the first line of copy.** Before any text, the recipient sees real money stapled to a piece of mail. The most efficient pattern interrupt ever invented. Currency in unexpected context is impossible to throw away.

**"As you can see, I have attached a nice, crisp dollar bill to the top of this letter."** — Stating the obvious is the move. The reader is already looking at the bill, but the sentence forces _conscious acknowledgment_, which converts the visual interrupt into cognitive commitment. "Nice, crisp": Halbert's signature sensory adjective stack.

**"Why have I done this?"** — The single most generative question in the letter. The reader's brain locks onto answer-search; the letter cannot be put down until that question is resolved. Curiosity gap in pristine form.

**"Actually, there are two reasons:"** — "Actually" is the conversational gift, like the writer is leveling with the reader. "Two reasons" is strategic: one reason feels suspicious, three reasons feels like a list, two feels honest.

**"1) I have something very important to tell you and I needed some way to make sure this letter would catch your attention."** — _Meta-honesty._ He is telling the reader he is using a psychological technique on them, and this transparency disarms the resistance that would otherwise be activated.

**"2) Since what I have to say is about how to make a great deal of money, I figured that using a dollar bill to get your attention was rather appropriate."** — The dollar is now metaphor _and_ grabber. The grabber and the topic are _coherent_. A dollar bill on a letter about gardening would feel cheap. On a letter about money, it feels like a thesis statement.

**"Now then, the important matter I have to tell you about is…"** — The "bait and switch into the body." After the grabber, the curiosity, and the meta-honesty, the reader is committed.

### 4.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                             |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | Variable; the dollar bill works at every awareness level because it operates pre-cognitively                        |
| Sophistication      | 5 in mailbox-attention competition                                                                                  |
| Primary framework   | Grabber → Acknowledge → Explain → Tie to offer                                                                      |
| Cialdini            | Reciprocity (he gave you money), Consistency (you've taken the money, so you owe him a read), Liking (transparency) |
| Halbert specificity | N/A (the dollar IS the specificity)                                                                                 |

### 4.4 What Makes It Timeless

**A physical object in the wrong context will out-perform any headline.** The dollar bill is a _physical pattern interrupt_. Modern equivalents: a free Stripe credit applied to a stranger's account, a Loom video addressed to the recipient by name, a screenshot of an unfinished email "for your eyes only." The principle: place an object inside the recipient's normal flow that does not belong there, then explain why.

Second principle: **acknowledged manipulation**. Halbert tells you he is using a technique. The candor disarms suspicion.

### 4.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 cold outbound)

> **Subject:** I bought you a $20 Stripe credit
>
> Hey [First name],
>
> I added a $20 credit to a Stripe account I want you to use. Two reasons:
>
> 1. I have something I think is worth 30 minutes of your time, and bare cold outreach is dead.
> 2. What I want to talk about is how [SaaS] cut your payment failures by 18% last quarter at companies your size. Using a dollar to talk about dollars felt appropriate.
>
> Claim it here: [link]
>
> If you don't reply, I'll never bug you again. The credit's yours regardless.

## 5. John Caples — "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano" (1925)

Caples wrote this for the U.S. School of Music while a junior copywriter at Ruthrauff & Ryan. It ran for decades and became the most copied template in 20th-century direct response. Every "They Laughed When I Said I'd Quit My Job" headline is a direct lift.

### 5.1 Original Text (opening lead)

> **Headline:** They Laughed When I Sat Down At the Piano — But When I Started to Play!
>
> Arthur had just played "The Rosary." The room rang with applause. I decided that this would be a dramatic moment for me to make my debut. To the amazement of all my friends, I strode confidently over to the piano and sat down.
>
> "Jack is up to his old tricks," somebody chuckled. The crowd laughed. They were certain I couldn't play a single note.
>
> "Can he really play?" I heard a girl whisper to Arthur.
>
> "Heavens, no!" Arthur exclaimed. "He never played a note in all his life… But just you watch him. This is going to be good."
>
> I decided to make the most of the situation. With mock dignity I drew out a silk handkerchief and lightly dusted off the piano keys. Then I rose and gave the revolving piano stool a quarter of a turn, just as I had seen an imitator of Paderewski do in a vaudeville sketch.
>
> "What do you think of his execution?" called a voice from the rear.
>
> "We're in favor of it!" came back the answer, and the crowd rocked with laughter.

Source: https://swiped.co/file/they-laughed-when-i-sat-down-at-the-piano-by-john-caples/

### 5.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**Headline: "They Laughed When I Sat Down At the Piano — But When I Started to Play!"** — Two-act story compressed into a single line. The em-dash is the cliff. The reader is _forced_ to know what happened next.

**"Arthur had just played 'The Rosary.'"** — Cold open in medias res. No setup, no context. Caples drops the reader into a scene already in motion. "The Rosary" is a specific 1898 Ethelbert Nevin parlor piece; every 1925 reader recognized it. _Specificity earns trust before the pitch begins._

**"The room rang with applause."** — Six words. Sensory.

**"I decided that this would be a dramatic moment for me to make my debut."** — Conspiratorial first-person. The reader is now inside the protagonist's head. "Dramatic moment" promises payoff.

**"To the amazement of all my friends, I strode confidently over to the piano…"** — "Amazement" pre-loads the surprise reveal. "Strode confidently" makes us suspicious. Confidence in the absence of skill is the comedic engine.

**"'Jack is up to his old tricks,' somebody chuckled."** — Now we know the protagonist's name. Dialogue before description. The narrator has a reputation as a prankster.

**"Can he really play?" / "Heavens, no!"** — Caples uses dialogue like a screenwriter. Every line increases tension and credibility, _because real conversation feels real._

**"He never played a note in all his life… But just you watch him. This is going to be good."** — Arthur is the reader's surrogate. Caples set up the _most extreme possible expectation gap_ (_never played a note_), so the reveal lands harder.

**"With mock dignity I drew out a silk handkerchief and lightly dusted off the piano keys."** — "Mock dignity" is the comedy. "Silk handkerchief" is specificity. "Lightly dusted off" is sensory.

**"Just as I had seen an imitator of Paderewski do in a vaudeville sketch."** — Cultural anchor. Two of-the-moment references in one sentence make the scene feel real.

**"What do you think of his execution?" / "We're in favor of it!"** — A vaudeville-grade pun. Critical move: he establishes that he can make the reader laugh. A reader who laughs keeps reading.

### 5.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 2 (reader knows they want to be able to do something impressive)                                                                    |
| Sophistication      | 2–3 (mail-order music instruction existed but wasn't dominant)                                                                      |
| Primary framework   | Story Lead → Demonstration → Mechanism Reveal → Offer                                                                               |
| Cialdini            | Social proof (the crowd's reaction), Authority (Paderewski reference), Liking (underdog), Consistency (rooting for the protagonist) |
| Halbert specificity | 8/10 (real piano piece, real pianist, silk handkerchief, quarter-turn of the stool)                                                 |

### 5.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Dramatize the transformation before naming the mechanism.** Caples gives you 4 paragraphs of _experience_ (the laughter, the silk handkerchief, the punchline) before he names the U.S. School of Music. The reader has already lived the emotional payoff. Naming the product is then an act of _deliverance_, not a sale.

Deeper principle: **start the story past the boring part.** Caples doesn't open with "Last summer I decided to learn piano." He opens with "Arthur had just played 'The Rosary.'" _Start where the conflict already exists._

### 5.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 skills / fitness LP)

**H1:** "They Laughed When I Posted My First Deadlift Video — Then I Posted The One From This Morning."

**First paragraph:** "My CrossFit gym has a Slack channel where new members post their first lifts. Mine got 14 laughing emojis. One coach DM'd me, 'respect for trying, but maybe start with the goblet squats.' That was October. This morning I deadlifted 425. The Slack channel went quiet…"

The Caples template still ships every Tuesday on TikTok.

## 6. Maxwell Sackheim — "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?" (1919)

The Sherwin Cody School ad. Ran continuously for 40 years. The headline is on every direct-response writer's top 10 list.

### 6.1 Original Text

> **Headline:** Do You Make These Mistakes In English?
>
> Many persons use such expressions as "Leave them lay there" for "Let them lie there"; "Different than" for "Different from"; "Who are you going with" for "With whom are you going?" Still others say "between you and I" instead of "between you and me." It is astonishing how often "who" is used for "whom" […]
>
> Such mistakes make the ears of cultured people fairly ache. Yet, why is it some people seem to know instinctively how to use the right word at the right time, while others make mistakes which prevent their advancement both socially and in business? […]
>
> Sherwin Cody, the famous English authority, perfected an amazing invention that brings the correct use of English to your tongue's tip and pen-point. Only 15 minutes a day are required to master this method. […]
>
> A 32-page book describing his self-correcting method will be sent free upon request.

Source: https://swiped.co/file/do-you-make-these-mistakes-by-max-sackheim/

### 6.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**Headline: "Do You Make These Mistakes In English?"** — Three layers of torque in seven words:

1. _Direct question._ The reader cannot scan past without answering "I hope not" or "I might."
2. _"These mistakes"_ pre-supposes specific errors exist that the reader doesn't know about. The deictic "these" forces them to keep reading.
3. _Public stakes._ Language errors are publicly observable. Social reputation is implicated.

_Open-loop curiosity weaponized against status anxiety._ Halbert called it the perfect headline because it cannot be answered without continuing to read.

**"Many persons use such expressions as 'Leave them lay there'…"** — The lead doesn't explain the headline; it _demonstrates_ it. Three specific errors in three lines. The reader is immediately running an internal audit: _do I say "different than"? Have I said "between you and I"?_ The audit _is_ the agitation.

**"It is astonishing how often 'who' is used for 'whom'…"** — Stacking. Enough errors that it is statistically likely the reader has made at least one.

**"Such mistakes make the ears of cultured people fairly ache."** — Social consequence stated plainly. "Cultured people" is the deliberately status-loaded phrase.

**"Yet, why is it some people seem to know instinctively how to use the right word at the right time, while others make mistakes which prevent their advancement both socially and in business?"** — Two payoff arenas: _social_ and _business_. The mistake doesn't just embarrass; it costs raises. Monetizing the shame.

**"Sherwin Cody, the famous English authority, perfected an amazing invention…"** — Authority + mechanism. "Amazing invention" reframes language instruction as a _device_. "Tongue's tip and pen-point" is beautiful writing: sensory, alliterative, concrete.

**"Only 15 minutes a day are required to master this method."** — The micro-commitment that defuses the effort objection. The same job "Just 5 minutes a day with Duolingo" did 100 years later.

**"A 32-page book describing his self-correcting method will be sent free upon request."** — The two-step offer. He's selling the free book, not the course. Conversion drag minimized.

### 6.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                   |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 1 (the reader doesn't necessarily know they make these mistakes)                                                          |
| Sophistication      | 2 (differentiated by "self-correcting" mechanism)                                                                         |
| Primary framework   | PAS tightly compressed                                                                                                    |
| Cialdini            | Authority (Cody as authority), Social proof (implicit, cultured people), Reciprocity (free book), Consistency (the audit) |
| Halbert specificity | 7/10 (errors quoted verbatim, 32 pages, 15 minutes)                                                                       |

### 6.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Indict the reader with their own embarrassment, then offer the cure.** The reader is the prosecution witness against themselves, and the product is the only available defense. This template ports to any skill the reader is mildly insecure about: "Do You Make These Mistakes In Excel?" "Do You Use These Sales Phrases That Kill Deals?" "Do You Have These 5 Bad Posture Habits?"

### 6.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 SaaS / training LP)

**H1:** "Do you make these mistakes in cold email?"

**Subhead:** "87% of B2B reps still write opening lines that get them flagged as spam."

**First section:** "Did you write 'Hope this finds you well' last week? 'I wanted to reach out about'? 'Just circling back'? Each of those three phrases triples the chance your email lands in promotions. We pulled 41M sent emails and ran the analysis. […] Get the free audit. Paste your last 5 sent emails, get the score back in 60 seconds."

## 7. David Ogilvy — Rolls-Royce (1958)

Ogilvy reported he tested 26 (some sources say 104) headlines before settling on this one. He kept the ad in his pocket for the rest of his life and reproduced it in _Confessions of an Advertising Man_.

### 7.1 Original Text

> **Headline:** At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock.
>
> **Sub:** What makes Rolls-Royce the best car in the world? "There is really no magic about it — it is merely patient attention to detail," says an eminent Rolls-Royce engineer.
>
> 1. "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock," reports the Technical Editor of THE MOTOR. Three mufflers tune out sound frequencies — acoustically.
> 2. Every Rolls-Royce engine is run for seven hours at full throttle before installation, and each car is test-driven for hundreds of miles over varying road surfaces.
> 3. The Rolls-Royce is designed as an owner-driven car. It is eighteen inches shorter than the largest domestic cars.
> 4. The car has power steering, power brakes, and automatic gear-shift. It is very easy to drive and park. No chauffeur required.
> 5. The finished car spends a week in the final test-shop, being fine-tuned. Here it is subjected to 98 separate ordeals. For example, the engineers use a stethoscope to listen for axle-whine.
>    [...]
> 6. The coachwork is given five coats of primer paint, and hand rubbed between each coat, before nine coats of finishing paint go on.
>    [...]
> 7. The Bentley is made by Rolls-Royce. Except for the radiators, they are identical motor cars, manufactured by the same engineers in the same works. People who feel diffident about driving a Rolls-Royce can buy a Bentley.

Source: https://swiped.co/file/rolls-royce-ad-by-david-ogilvy/, https://adland.tv/rolls-royce-david-ogilvy-loudest-noise-comes-electric-clock-1958/

### 7.2 Sentence-by-Sentence Dissection

**Headline.** Demonstrative, sensory, specific. Doesn't say "quiet." Puts you in a car at 60 mph, where the only thing you hear is a clock. The headline IS the experience.

Critically: it is _attributed_ to the Technical Editor of _The Motor_. Ogilvy didn't make the claim; he reported it. _Journalistic borrowing of credibility._

**"There is really no magic about it — it is merely patient attention to detail."** — Sub-headline pre-frames the entire ad. The 13 points are not features; they are _examples of patient attention to detail_. Ogilvy is teaching you how to interpret the bullet list before you read it.

**Point 2: Engine run for seven hours at full throttle.** — Specificity is the entire argument. Not "engines are tested." _Seven hours. Full throttle._

**Point 4: "No chauffeur required."** — Status-anxiety reversal. In 1958, the perceived obstacle to buying a Rolls was needing a chauffeur. Ogilvy preempts the objection by name.

**Point 5: "98 separate ordeals" + "stethoscope to listen for axle-whine."** — The single most-quoted sentence in the ad. _Operatic specificity._ A doctor's tool used on a car. The image lodges permanently.

**Point 8: Five coats of primer, hand rubbed between each, before nine coats of finishing paint.** — Process specificity. The numbers do the work.

**Point 13: "People who feel diffident about driving a Rolls-Royce can buy a Bentley."** — The down-market peace offering. Ogilvy made room for the reader who wanted the substance without the social weight.

### 7.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                       |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 4–5 (brand-aware audience)                                                                                                    |
| Sophistication      | 4–5 (luxury car category saturated with adjectival claims; Ogilvy's mechanism is _itemized evidence instead of claims_)       |
| Primary framework   | Reason-Why (claim → proof with specifics)                                                                                     |
| Cialdini            | Authority (Technical Editor, engineers, heritage), Social proof (implicit), Scarcity (none; considered tacky for this market) |
| Halbert specificity | 10/10 (seven hours, 98 ordeals, stethoscope, 18 inches, three mufflers, five coats primer, nine coats finishing)              |

### 7.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Facts are more persuasive than adjectives, especially at the high end.** When you're selling premium, the language of advertising itself becomes suspect. Write like a feature reporter: attribute claims to authorities, list specific facts, let the reader connect them to value.

Second principle: **the headline is a single line of demonstrative proof, not a benefit statement.** "Luxury you can hear" would have been a benefit statement. "The loudest noise comes from the electric clock" is _the proof itself_.

### 7.5 Modern Adaptation (2026 premium B2B SaaS)

**H1:** "At 100,000 concurrent users the loudest noise in our infrastructure is a Slack notification."

**Subhead:** "There's really no magic. We just check everything." — Principal SRE, [Company]

**13 bullets:**

1. Each new release runs for 36 hours at 4× peak load before shipping.
2. Our p99.9 latency is 47ms, audited by Cloudflare's RUM data.
3. Every code change runs through a 412-step deploy pipeline.
4. Our SLA isn't "four nines." It's 99.999%: five minutes of downtime per year, max.
5. […]

This is the modern Stripe / Linear status-page-as-ad genre. Direct lineage to Ogilvy.

## 8. VW "Think Small" + "Lemon" (DDB, 1959–60)

The two ads that ended the era of hyperbole-driven car advertising. Voted #1 ad campaign of the 20th century by _Ad Age_. Bill Bernbach, Helmut Krone, Julian Koenig.

### 8.1 Original Text — "Think Small"

> **Headline:** Think small.
>
> Our little car isn't so much of a novelty any more.
>
> A couple of dozen college kids don't try to squeeze inside it. The guy at the gas station doesn't ask where the gas goes. Nobody even stares at our shape. In fact, some people who drive our little flivver don't even think 32 miles to the gallon is going any great guns.
>
> Or using five pints of oil instead of five quarts. Or never needing anti-freeze. Or racking up 40,000 miles on a set of tires.
>
> That's because once you get used to some of our economies, you don't even think about them any more.
>
> Except when you squeeze into a small parking spot. Or renew your small insurance. Or pay a small repair bill. Or trade in your old VW for a new one.
>
> Think it over.

### 8.1b Original Text — "Lemon"

> **Headline:** Lemon.
>
> This Volkswagen missed the boat. The chrome strip on the glove compartment is blemished and must be replaced. Chances are you wouldn't have noticed it; Inspector Kurt Kroner did.
>
> There are 3,389 men at our Wolfsburg factory with only one job: to inspect Volkswagens at each stage of production. (3000 Volkswagens are produced daily; there are more inspectors than cars.)
>
> Every shock absorber is tested (spot checking won't do), every windshield is scanned. VWs have been rejected for surface scratches barely visible to the eye.
>
> Final inspection is really something! VW inspectors run each car off the line onto the Funktionsprüfstand (car test stand), tote up 189 check points, gun ahead to the automatic brake stand, and say "no" to one VW out of fifty.
>
> This preoccupation with detail means the VW lasts longer and requires less maintenance, by and large, than other cars. (It also means a used VW depreciates less than any other car.)
>
> We pluck the lemons; you get the plums.

Source: https://www.manifestowriting.com/database/volkswagen-think-small/ and /volkswagen-lemon/

### 8.2 Dissection — "Think Small"

**Headline: "Think small."** — Two words. A period. In 1959 American advertising, no one used two-word headlines, no one used periods (every headline ended in exclamation), and no one _suggested smallness_ in a car ad. Three taboos in one beat.

The headline is _imperative voice_ aimed at a culture told to think _big_ (post-war abundance, V8s, tailfins). Bernbach's gambit: identify the cultural meta-pattern and command its opposite. A Zen koan disguised as a tagline.

**"Our little car isn't so much of a novelty any more."** — Anti-hype. Opens with a _demotion_ of the product. Inverse of every period car ad.

**"A couple of dozen college kids don't try to squeeze inside it. The guy at the gas station doesn't ask where the gas goes. Nobody even stares at our shape."** — Three _negative_ social proofs. Conveys market adoption without claiming popularity.

**"In fact, some people who drive our little flivver don't even think 32 miles to the gallon is going any great guns."** — "Flivver" was 1920s slang for a beat-up car. Self-deprecation. "32 miles to the gallon" drops the proof as afterthought.

**"Or using five pints of oil instead of five quarts. Or never needing anti-freeze. Or racking up 40,000 miles on a set of tires."** — Three more economies, conversationally listed. _Casual list_, not bullet-pointed sales talk.

**"Except when you squeeze into a small parking spot. Or renew your small insurance. Or pay a small repair bill."** — _Small_ flips from liability to benefit. The word goes from threat to virtue across one paragraph.

**"Think it over."** — Permission to take time. No CTA. No urgency. The opposite of direct response. The ad assumes the reader is intelligent enough to decide for themselves, and that flattery _is_ the conversion mechanism.

### 8.3 Dissection — "Lemon"

**Headline: "Lemon."** — One word. A period. For a car ad in 1960, this is closer to terrorism than copywriting. The reader looks at the photograph: a perfectly clean Beetle. The headline says "lemon." The mismatch _forces the read_.

**"The chrome strip on the glove compartment is blemished and must be replaced."** — A defect so minor it's almost comedic. _This is what they call a lemon?_

**"Chances are you wouldn't have noticed it; Inspector Kurt Kroner did."** — Naming the inspector. _Kurt Kroner._ The reader will remember Kurt Kroner. Specificity collapses the abstraction of "quality control" into a real German man with a name.

**"There are 3,389 men at our Wolfsburg factory with only one job…"** — More inspectors than cars. The parenthetical is the proof. The precise count: 3,389, not "more than 3,000."

**"Final inspection is really something! VW inspectors run each car off the line onto the Funktionsprüfstand…"** — The German word is the most important rhetorical move. _Funktionsprüfstand._ Four foreign syllables that _encode Teutonic seriousness_ into one paragraph. The translation in parens ("car test stand") makes it digestible. Two-for-one: exoticism + accessibility.

**"We pluck the lemons; you get the plums."** — A 10-word fruit metaphor that retroactively re-interprets the entire ad. The headline is no longer about a defective car. It's about VW's willingness to call one a lemon so the customer never sees it.

### 8.4 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 4–5 — Americans knew VW existed                                                       |
| Sophistication      | 5 — category saturated with hyperbole; Bernbach's mechanism is _style-as-positioning_ |
| Primary framework   | Anti-hype / Identity Lead                                                             |
| Cialdini            | Liking (humor), Authority (Kurt Kroner, Funktionsprüfstand), Social proof (implicit)  |
| Halbert specificity | 9/10 — 3,389 inspectors, 189 check points, 32 mpg, 40k miles, 1-in-50 rejection       |

### 8.5 What Makes It Timeless

**When your category is saturated with hyperbole, the absence of hyperbole is the loudest claim available.** The voice itself becomes the proof of quality. _Only a company actually confident in its product would write this way._

Second principle: **lead with your apparent weakness, then reframe it.** "Small" is the weakness; "small parking, small insurance, small repair" is the reframe. "Lemon" is the weakness; "we catch them so you don't" is the reframe.

### 8.6 Modern Adaptation (2026 DTC / SaaS)

**Think Small applied to SaaS:**

> H1: Small.
>
> Body: Our app does one thing. We don't add features. Our last update removed three things and added zero. That bothers a lot of product managers. It doesn't bother our users. They pay us $89/month and have for an average of 38 months. Small team, small feature set, small bills. Try it.

**Lemon applied to engineering:**

> H1: Rejected.
>
> Body: This commit was rejected. The state migration would have shaved 4ms off response time, but it added 0.2% chance of a deadlock at concurrency >50k. Senior engineer Devon Liu rejected it. There are 42 engineers at [Company] whose entire job is to reject commits like this one. We have more reviewers than authors. We reject 1 out of 8 commits at the final stage. We pluck the bugs; you get the uptime.

## 9. Apple "1984" + "Crazy Ones" Manifesto (1984 / 1997)

Two ads, thirteen years apart, both written when Apple was in commercial trouble, both refusing to show the product for nearly the entire run-time. Together they define identity advertising for the rest of the century.

### 9.1 "1984" Voiceover (full)

> Today we celebrate the first glorious anniversary of the Information Purification Directives. We have created for the first time in all history a garden of pure ideology, where each worker may bloom, secure from the pests of any contradictory true thoughts. Our Unification of Thoughts is more powerful a weapon than any fleet or army on earth. We are one people, with one will, one resolve, one cause. Our enemies shall talk themselves to death and we will bury them with their own confusion. We shall prevail!
>
> **Closing voiceover:** On January 24th, Apple Computer will introduce Macintosh. And you'll see why 1984 won't be like "1984."

### 9.1b "Crazy Ones" / "Think Different" (1997) — full text

> Here's to the crazy ones.
> The misfits.
> The rebels.
> The troublemakers.
> The round pegs in the square holes.
> The ones who see things differently.
> They're not fond of rules.
> And they have no respect for the status quo.
> You can quote them, disagree with them, glorify or vilify them.
> About the only thing you can't do is ignore them.
> Because they change things.
> They push the human race forward.
> And while some may see them as the crazy ones, we see genius.
> Because the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world, are the ones who do.

Source: https://fs.blog/steve-jobs-crazy-ones/

### 9.2 Dissection — "1984"

**"Today we celebrate the first glorious anniversary of the Information Purification Directives."** — The script is written in the voice of the _enemy_. Apple's ad doesn't promote Apple; it dramatizes the world _without_ Apple. Stage-5 sophistication: instead of arguing against the dominant alternative (IBM/PC), inhabit it and let the dominance itself be the horror.

**"A garden of pure ideology, where each worker may bloom, secure from the pests of any contradictory true thoughts."** — Doubled meaning: literal Orwellian propaganda + thinly veiled jab at IBM corporate culture.

**"We are one people, with one will, one resolve, one cause."** — Choric repetition. The tonal anchor: the literal opposite of what Macintosh's individual-user proposition would be.

**Closing card: "On January 24th, Apple Computer will introduce Macintosh. And you'll see why 1984 won't be like '1984.'"** — The product name appears in the _final_ sentence. For 60 seconds, the audience watches what is essentially a piece of dystopian filmmaking with no product visible. _The ad isn't about the product; it's about the world the product opposes._

### 9.3 Dissection — "Crazy Ones"

**"Here's to the crazy ones."** — Toast structure. _Here's to_ is a benediction. The ad opens not by selling, but by honoring. The viewer is positioned in a room where the toast is being made, and they have to decide if it's being made _to_ them.

**"The misfits. The rebels. The troublemakers."** — Asyndeton (no conjunctions). Each label loaded with simultaneous insult and badge. The reader has been called all three at some point, and the toast is converting the insult into a credit.

**"The round pegs in the square holes."** — The most concrete metaphor in the piece. Geometric, physical, unmistakable. The single line every reader can locate themselves inside.

**"The ones who see things differently."** — _The tagline appears inside the manifesto._ "Think Different" is grammatically wrong (should be "differently"), but the manifesto uses the correct adverbial form. The slogan is the imperative compression of this line.

**"They're not fond of rules. And they have no respect for the status quo."** — "Not fond of" is the deliberately mild phrasing; anyone can identify with mild antipathy. It admits the reader into the club at a low threshold.

**"You can quote them, disagree with them, glorify or vilify them. About the only thing you can't do is ignore them."** — Permission structure: joining the crazy ones doesn't require ideological purity. Makes the manifesto _non-cult_.

**"Because they change things. They push the human race forward."** — The civilizational claim. Big-frame argument: the crazy ones are the engine of progress. _Including the reader, if they choose to identify in._

**"And while some may see them as the crazy ones, we see genius."** — The _we_ is Apple. The brand declares its tribal allegiance.

**"Because the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world, are the ones who do."** — Tautological closer that lands because it _sounds_ true. Apple has converted a sentimental aphorism into a brand thesis.

### 9.4 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                                |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 5 — fully product-aware, deciding philosophical alignment                                                                              |
| Sophistication      | 5 — meta-position level, competing on meaning not features                                                                             |
| Primary framework   | Identity Lead / Manifesto                                                                                                              |
| Cialdini            | Liking (in-group), Social proof (the implied tribe of geniuses), Authority (historical figures lending their authority by association) |
| Halbert specificity | N/A — consciously above the specificity register                                                                                       |

### 9.5 What Makes It Timeless

**Sell the worldview the product makes possible, not the product.** Apple in both 1984 and 1997 was in commercial trouble making ads that didn't show the product for 55+ seconds. The bet: _if you align with the worldview, the product purchase follows automatically._

### 9.6 Modern Adaptation (2026 brand-level)

This template still ships. Patagonia ("Don't buy this jacket"). Apple's current work. Linear's "Issue tracking is dead." The 2026 risk is performative virtue. Modern audiences have a sharper filter for brand-philosophy ads than 1997 audiences did. The cure: tie the philosophy to a concrete operational practice on the very next scroll (otherwise it reads as posturing).

## 10. Linear Homepage (linear.app)

The canonical example of premium B2B copy in 2026. The reference every PMM uses when arguing for taste over feature lists.

### 10.1 Original Text (verbatim from live site)

> **H1:** The product development system for teams and agents
>
> **Subhead:** Purpose-built for planning and building products. Designed for the AI era.
>
> **Primary anchor link:** Issue tracking is dead

Source: https://linear.app

### 10.2 Dissection

**H1: "The product development system for teams and agents."** — Two moves in one line:

1. _Category recategorization._ Linear no longer calls itself "issue tracking" or "project management." It calls itself "product development system": a wider category. The Levitt move: "what business are we in?"
2. _"Teams and agents."_ The "and agents" is the 2026-specific signal. Linear has decided that AI agents are first-class users of the product. The H1 says so _before any feature is named_.

Note the absence of adjectives. No "fast," no "beautiful," no "modern." The H1 declares the category and moves on. Linear's confidence is communicated by the _absence_ of selling language: the same DDB principle, sixty years later.

**Subhead: "Purpose-built for planning and building products. Designed for the AI era."** — Two sentences, each one a proof beat:

- _"Purpose-built"_ denies the alternative ("we are not a generic tracker repurposed"). Specificity by negation.
- _"Designed for the AI era"_ is temporal placement. Linear is not "AI-powered"; it is _of the AI era_. Linear is positioned as the platform on which AI works, not as a feature wearing AI as costume.

**Anchor: "Issue tracking is dead."** — The CTA isn't "Get started." It's a position statement. Linear is using its CTA _to claim the death of a category_: the category Jira owns. The user who clicks is signaling agreement with the thesis. Identity-driven UX.

### 10.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                             |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 4–5 — audience is project-management-aware, often Linear-aware                                                      |
| Sophistication      | 5 — category maximally saturated; Linear competes on _taste and worldview_                                          |
| Primary framework   | Category-creation lead                                                                                              |
| Cialdini            | Authority (the design itself, customer logos below the fold), Liking (founder cult / aesthetic match), Social proof |
| Halbert specificity | 3/10 above the fold; rises sharply below                                                                            |

### 10.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Compete on the question, not the answer.** Jira competes on "how good is your issue tracker?" Linear competes on "should we still have an issue tracker at all?" When you can change the question the prospect is asking, you have already won.

### 10.5 How To Steal It

_The H1 should make a category-level claim, not a product-level claim._ Replace "the best X" with "X is dead." Replace adjectives with positioning verbs. Trust the reader to know the rest.

## 11. Stripe Homepage (stripe.com)

The cleanest example of restraint-as-positioning in modern B2B. Stripe writes like infrastructure: flat, durable, plain.

### 11.1 Original Text (verbatim from live site)

> **H1:** Financial infrastructure to grow your revenue.
>
> **Subhead:** Accept payments, offer financial services and implement custom revenue models — from your first transaction to your billionth.
>
> **Primary CTAs:** Get started / Sign up with Google
>
> **Stats:** 135+ currencies and payment methods supported / US$1.9tn in payments volume processed in 2025 / 99.999% historical uptime for Stripe services / 200M+ active subscriptions managed on Stripe Billing
>
> **Payments sub-page H1:** Unified, global payments to grow your revenue.

Source: https://stripe.com / https://stripe.com/payments

### 11.2 Dissection

**H1: "Financial infrastructure to grow your revenue."** — Three load-bearing words:

1. _Financial infrastructure._ Stripe is not "a payment processor." It is _infrastructure_. The category is wider than payments. (Capital, Atlas, Treasury, Issuing all live under this H1.)
2. _To grow._ Purpose-clause. The infrastructure is in service of an outcome.
3. _Your revenue._ The customer's revenue, not Stripe's volume.

A single declarative sentence with no exclamation, no adverb, no metaphor. _Infrastructural in its own diction._

**Subhead: "Accept payments, offer financial services and implement custom revenue models — from your first transaction to your billionth."** — Two operations:

- Triple-verb list (Accept, offer, implement) as a _product surface area indicator_.
- "From your first transaction to your billionth" is _temporal scale_. Stripe addresses both the indie developer and the enterprise CFO in one sentence. Stripe's worldview: payments don't get harder, you get bigger.

**Stats: 135+ / $1.9tn / 99.999% / 200M+.** — Ogilvy specificity. Each stat is category-level proof a competitor cannot match.

### 11.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                              |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 4–5 — most prospects are Stripe-aware                                                                                |
| Sophistication      | 5 — differentiation via developer-friendliness, scale, category expansion                                            |
| Primary framework   | Category-frame + Proof-stat tile                                                                                     |
| Cialdini            | Authority (stats, logos, developer docs), Social proof (volume), Consistency (design radiates adult professionalism) |
| Halbert specificity | 9/10                                                                                                                 |

### 11.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Restraint communicates premium.** No bullet pyramid. No urgency. No "trusted by world-class teams." Just one declarative H1 and four statistics. The restraint _is_ the positioning.

**Specificity scales upward.** Stripe addresses both the side-project developer and the public-company CFO with the _same_ H1. The trick: the H1 is so plain it works at both ends. _Plain language scales; clever language doesn't._

### 11.5 How To Steal It

Write the H1 that works for the smallest customer AND the largest customer with no rewriting. If you have to write two different H1s for two audiences, you don't yet know what your category is.

## 12. Notion Homepage (notion.com)

The canonical example of _metaphor as positioning_ in the AI era. One four-word headline doing the work that competitors do with paragraphs of jargon.

### 12.1 Original Text (verbatim from live site)

> **H1:** Meet the night shift.
>
> **Subhead:** Notion agents keep work moving 24/7. They capture knowledge, answer questions, and push projects forward — all while you sleep.
>
> **CTAs:** Get Notion free / Request a demo
>
> **Trust:** Trusted by 98% of the Forbes Cloud 100
>
> **Stats:** Over 100M users worldwide / #1 knowledge base 3 years running (G2) / #1 AI enterprise search (G2) / 62% of Fortune 100

Source: https://www.notion.com/

### 12.2 Dissection

**H1: "Meet the night shift."** — Four words. The move:

- It does not say "Notion AI."
- It does not say "Agents."
- It says _night shift_: a workplace metaphor that converts an abstract AI capability into a familiar human role.

The **metaphorical reframe**. The reader who has been told 47 times this year that "AI is a co-pilot" or "AI is your assistant" now encounters "AI is the team that works when you don't." The image is concrete. The reader can see it.

**Subhead: "Notion agents keep work moving 24/7. They capture knowledge, answer questions, and push projects forward — all while you sleep."** — Three concrete verbs (capture, answer, push) bookended by the temporal claim (24/7, while you sleep). The subhead refuses jargon. No "AI-powered," no "leveraging," no "intelligent." There is _capture, answer, push_. Verbs only.

**"All while you sleep."** — Sleep as the proof of autonomy. The reader's literal absence is the test of the agent's value.

**Trust: "Trusted by 98% of the Forbes Cloud 100."** — Specific social proof. Not "trusted by leading companies" but a _percentage of a named index_. Fact-checkable, and that's the point.

**Stats: 100M users / 3 years #1 on G2 / 62% of Fortune 100.** — Three different scales (mass-market, expert ranking, enterprise penetration). Bottom-up + expert validation + enterprise depth.

### 12.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                             |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 4–5                                                                                 |
| Sophistication      | 5 — AI productivity tools maximally saturated; Notion needed a fresh frame          |
| Primary framework   | Metaphor + Proof-tile                                                               |
| Cialdini            | Authority (Forbes, Fortune, G2), Social proof (100M), Liking (playful humanization) |
| Halbert specificity | 8/10                                                                                |

### 12.4 What Makes It Timeless

**A working metaphor is worth a hundred features.** "Night shift" is a Trojan horse: it imports an entire mental model (workers, shifts, hand-offs, autonomy) without naming any of those concepts. The reader does the cognitive work for free. Same move as Apple's "1,000 songs in your pocket" or Stripe's "infrastructure": _one metaphor, the entire positioning argument._

### 12.5 How To Steal It

If your category has been described the same way for two years, find a metaphor from a different domain entirely (industrial, biological, geographical, theatrical, military) and import it. Test it by trying to describe the product without using any category-standard vocabulary.

## 13. Liquid Death (liquiddeath.com)

The single most-cited DTC case study of the 2020s. Water sold like a metal band. CEO Mike Cessario's stated strategy: "Take an unhealthy category aesthetic and apply it to a healthy product."

### 13.1 Original Text (verbatim, live site)

> **Brand tagline:** Murder Your Thirst
>
> **Energy drink positioning:** We Made a Better-For-You Energy Drink
>
> **Soda alternative:** Less Sugar.\* Huge Flavor.
>
> **Iced tea:** Less Sugar.\* Greater Tea.
>
> **Water:** Deadly Mountains. Delicious Water.
>
> **Sustainability:** Plastic Recycling Is a Myth. Most Plastic Is Sent to Landfills. Aluminum Is Infinitely Recyclable.

Source: https://liquiddeath.com

### 13.2 Dissection

**"Murder Your Thirst."** — Two words. Brand positioning compressed to a verb. "Murder" turns drinking water into a confrontational, violent verb: the entire brand thesis. Liquid Death is selling _attitude packaged with water_. The product is the attitude; the water is the delivery vehicle.

The line is also a _pun on the brand name_. "Liquid Death" → "kill" → "murder" → "thirst." Brand name and tagline are recursive.

**"Deadly Mountains. Delicious Water."** — Four words, two periods, parallel structure. The contradiction (deadly/delicious) is the brand voice in miniature.

**"Less Sugar.\* Huge Flavor."** — Mainstream nutrition copy in a punk register. The asterisk is honest (links to a comparative-claim footnote); Liquid Death respects the FDA, even though its brand reads like it doesn't.

**"Plastic Recycling Is a Myth. Most Plastic Is Sent to Landfills. Aluminum Is Infinitely Recyclable."** — Three sentences. Each one a fact. Each fact builds an argument for the can format. Liquid Death is taking an environmental position that _is also a product feature_: aluminum cans are their packaging. The sustainability copy IS the product copy.

**Product names:** Scary Strawberry, Dead Billionaire, Rest in Peach. Every SKU extends the brand voice. The voice is the asset; the product is the vehicle.

### 13.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                  |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Awareness stage     | 3–4                                                                                                                      |
| Sophistication      | 5 — bottled water is the most commoditized category on earth; Liquid Death wins by being the only brand with an attitude |
| Primary framework   | Identity Lead / Brand-as-Cult                                                                                            |
| Cialdini            | Liking (humor), Social proof (merch economy — people wear LD shirts), Consistency (cross-SKU commitment)                 |
| Halbert specificity | N/A — operating at the Apple register                                                                                    |

### 13.4 What Makes It Timeless

**In a commoditized category, the only available differentiator is voice.** Find the most emotionally charged adjacent category and import its tone.

Second principle: **make merch.** Liquid Death t-shirts are walking ads. The brand voice is so strong that consumers pay to advertise the brand. This is the modern Coat-of-Arms principle: the brand becomes part of the consumer's identity, which they then display.

### 13.5 How To Steal It

If your product is commoditized, pick a voice from a different cultural register entirely (metal, horror, religious, military, hyper-corporate, monastic) and apply it consistently across every touchpoint. The cardinal rule: voice must be enforced across product naming, packaging, copy, and customer service. _One inconsistent surface and the spell breaks._

## 14. Magic Spoon (magicspoon.com)

The DTC cereal case study. The line on the homepage is the entire positioning, in seven words.

### 14.1 Original Text

> **H1:** Childhood Classics. Grown-Up Ingredients.
>
> **Secondary H1:** High Protein, Keto-Friendly, 0g Sugar Cereal
>
> **Bullets:** High Protein: 12-14g complete protein in every bowl / 0-2g Sugar: No cane sugar, corn syrup, or sugar alcohol / Sweet & Delicious: Tastes just like you remember, only better
>
> **Pricing:** Current price $54 was $59 (Variety 6-Pack) / Subscribe & Save 20%
>
> **CTAs:** Shop Cereal / Build your own bundle / Try Now

Source: https://magicspoon.com

### 14.2 Dissection

**H1: "Childhood Classics. Grown-Up Ingredients."** — Two-phrase parallelism. The entire positioning in seven words:

- "Childhood Classics": emotional appeal, nostalgia.
- "Grown-Up Ingredients": rational appeal, health.

Schwartz-grade positioning: the product is the _resolution of a contradiction_ the consumer felt. They miss Frosted Flakes; they can't have Frosted Flakes anymore. Magic Spoon is the synthesis.

**Secondary H1: "High Protein, Keto-Friendly, 0g Sugar Cereal."** — Three diet-trend keywords stacked. SEO + comp-frame stacking. Each one matches a specific search intent.

**Bullets:** Specificity (grams), comparison (three named exclusions), benefit ("tastes just like you remember, only better"). Each bullet hits a different value axis: protein, sugar, taste.

**Pricing: "$54 was $59. Subscribe & Save 20%."** — Anchoring + commitment ladder. The reader sees a $5 discount and then a 20% subscription discount. Two price psychologies in one line.

**"Variety 6-Pack"** — Bundle-as-default. The unit Magic Spoon optimizes for is the 6-pack, not the single box. Reduces shipping cost per unit and ladders the AOV.

### 14.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                       |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 2–3 — audience knows they want healthier cereal; may not know Magic Spoon                     |
| Sophistication      | 3–4 — "healthy cereal" is saturated; differentiates via _nostalgia-meets-macros_ synthesis    |
| Primary framework   | Contradiction-resolution + macro proof + price ladder                                         |
| Cialdini            | Social proof (reviews), Authority (macros), Liking (nostalgia), Commitment (subscribe & save) |
| Halbert specificity | 8/10                                                                                          |

### 14.4 What Makes It Timeless

**The best positioning resolves a contradiction the consumer feels.** The H1 isn't "healthy cereal." It is "childhood classics + grown-up ingredients," because the consumer _wants both_ and currently has to pick one. The product is the synthesis.

**Sell the bundle, not the unit.** Magic Spoon's homepage anchor is the 6-pack. The reader's first encounter is a higher-AOV unit.

### 14.5 How To Steal It

For any consumer category: find the trade-off your customer currently has to make and write the H1 as "[thing they miss] + [thing they need]." Examples:

- "Boxed wine quality. Restaurant-bottle dignity."
- "Tax software speed. Accountant accuracy."
- "Therapist depth. Coach pace."
- "Editorial photography. iPhone speed."

## 15. Ramit Sethi — iwillteachyoutoberich.com / Earnable

The canonical info-product positioning case study of the 2010s–2020s. Ramit owns the phrase "Rich Life" the way Apple owns "Think Different."

### 15.1 Original Text (verbatim from live site)

> **Homepage H1:** Your Rich Life Starts Here
>
> **Subhead:** Step-by-step Programs for Creating Your Rich Life
>
> **Supporting:** Word-for-word scripts and exact tactics, all rigorously tested by thousands of students.
>
> **CTA:** Get Coaching from Ramit
>
> **Earnable H1:** Break the shackles of your job, your calendar, and your 9-to-5 income.
>
> **Earnable subhead:** WHAT IF YOU COULD START A BUSINESS THAT PAYS YOU TO LIVE THE LIFE YOU'VE ALWAYS WANTED?
>
> **Earnable hook:** I went from a college student with no experience to founder and CEO of a successful, automated, multimillion-dollar business that's helped millions of people.

Source: https://www.iwillteachyoutoberich.com/ and /earnable/

### 15.2 Dissection

**Homepage H1: "Your Rich Life Starts Here."** — Ramit's entire empire compressed. "Rich Life" is his proprietary phrase. The audience is implicated by the second word ("your") and the action is implicated by the fourth ("starts").

**Subhead: "Step-by-step Programs for Creating Your Rich Life."** — The mechanism word is _step-by-step_. The audience for this product is people who've consumed enough finance content to know they want _prescriptive_ instruction, not theory.

**"Word-for-word scripts and exact tactics, all rigorously tested by thousands of students."** — Four modifiers each carrying a benefit:

- "Word-for-word": no creative work required.
- "Exact tactics": no judgment calls required.
- "Rigorously tested": risk mitigated.
- "Thousands of students": social proof.

One of the most-copied lines in the productized-service space. Every word load-bearing.

**Earnable H1: "Break the shackles of your job, your calendar, and your 9-to-5 income."** — Triple-object structure. "Shackles" is the violence word. "Job, calendar, income": three different prison metaphors. The reader is now visualizing release.

**"WHAT IF YOU COULD START A BUSINESS THAT PAYS YOU TO LIVE THE LIFE YOU'VE ALWAYS WANTED?"** — All caps signals the conditional fantasy. "What if you could" is the dream-state opener; the reader fills in their own answer.

**Hook: "I went from a college student with no experience to founder and CEO of a successful, automated, multimillion-dollar business that's helped millions of people."** — Transformation story compressed. Six load-bearing claims in 30 words: "college student" (relatable starting point), "no experience" (no advantage), "automated" (the mechanism), "multimillion-dollar" (proof of size), "millions of people" (proof of help).

### 15.3 Framework Mapping

| Dimension           | Reading                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness stage     | 3–4 — audience has consumed his book, podcast, Netflix show                                                                      |
| Sophistication      | 5 — personal-finance / online-business maximally saturated; wins on _proprietary vocabulary_ + _rigorous specificity_            |
| Primary framework   | Identity-led ("Rich Life") + Mechanism ("step-by-step") + Proof (thousands of students)                                          |
| Cialdini            | Authority (Netflix, NYT bestseller), Social proof (42,000+ students), Liking (founder voice), Consistency (book → course ladder) |
| Halbert specificity | 9/10                                                                                                                             |

### 15.4 What Makes It Timeless

**Own a proprietary phrase.** "Rich Life" is Ramit's. He uses it on every page, every podcast episode, every email. It becomes a _cognitive shortcut_: the reader hears the phrase and immediately routes to Ramit's universe. _Find a 2–3 word phrase that compresses your worldview and use it 10,000 times._

**Sell the specificity of the asset, not the promise of the outcome.** Ramit promises less than competitors. He doesn't say "make $1M in a year." He says "word-for-word scripts." The reader who trusts the asset trusts the outcome.

### 15.5 How To Steal It

This is the dominant template for high-ticket info products in 2026. The principle: _coin your own phrase and use it everywhere._ Earn the right to sell the outcome by over-delivering on the specificity of the asset.

## Cross-Teardown Patterns

Six patterns recurred at every level: classic and modern, long and short, B2B and DTC. These are the universal moves.

### 1. The first sentence does not sell

Conroy, Caples, Karbo, Halbert, Throssell, Settle, Bernbach: none of them open with the product, the price, or the offer. They open with story, demonstration, mystery, fiction, anecdote, taboo. **The first sentence's job is to make the second sentence harder not to read.** The product introduction has to be _earned_.

Modern landing pages compress this, but Linear, Notion, and Stripe still accomplish it in the H1 itself by setting up the worldview before the product is named.

**Operational test:** Could your first sentence appear in a novel? In a magazine feature? In a private email to a friend? If not, rewrite.

### 2. Specificity beats adjectives at every level

Ogilvy's stethoscope. Bernbach's 3,389 inspectors. Halbert's recipient by name. Karbo's two boats and a Cadillac. Stripe's $1.9tn. Allbirds's wool, tree fiber, sugarcane. The specific noun out-performs the qualitative adjective at every awareness stage and every sophistication level.

The discipline: refuse "premium," "best-in-class," "world-class," "innovative," "cutting-edge," and **name the thing**. A stethoscope. A surname. A check that won't be cashed.

**Operational test:** For every adjective in your copy, write the specific fact that justifies it. If you can't write the fact, cut the adjective. If you can, replace the adjective with the fact.

### 3. The product is named only after the frame is accepted

Every long-form classic delays the product introduction until the reader has accepted the _worldview in which the product is the answer_. Conroy: 220 words. Karbo: a complete philosophical reframe before he says "book." Caples: four paragraphs of comedy before "U.S. School of Music."

Modern equivalents accomplish this _inside the H1_: Linear's "Issue tracking is dead." Notion's "Meet the night shift." Apple's "Think Different." The frame is the headline.

**Operational test:** Strip the product name from your hero section. Does the remaining copy still make a coherent argument? If yes, your frame is doing real work. If no, your copy is just a feature list.

### 4. Identity is the deepest possible appeal

Halbert (your surname). Apple (the kind of person you are). VW (the kind of car-buyer you are). Liquid Death (the kind of drinker you are). Sethi (your Rich Life). Belgray (getting paid to be you).

The most durable copy doesn't sell what the reader will _have_; it sells what they will _be_. Identity appeals scale across all five Schwartz awareness stages because identity is upstream of every other purchase motivation.

**Operational test:** Complete the sentence "After they buy this, they will be the kind of person who \_\_\_\_." If you can finish it in one specific noun phrase, you have an identity-led product. If not, you're selling features.

### 5. Mechanism reveal earns the right to make a promise

Schwartz's Stage-4 sophistication move: when the market has heard every claim, the writer's only edge is _naming a new mechanism for why the claim is true._ Conroy: "knowledge as the variable." Karbo: "lazy is smart." Sackheim: "self-correcting method." Stefan Georgi VSLs: "30-second metabolic switch." Notion: "night shift."

The mechanism is a _handle_: a phrase the buyer can grasp and remember. It does not have to be technically accurate. It has to be _ownable, memorable, and coherent_ with the proof.

**Operational test:** In one sentence, name the mechanism by which your product works. Does it sound like something a competitor could say verbatim? If yes, your mechanism is generic. Replace it with one you can own.

### 6. Restraint signals premium

Stripe, Linear, Notion, and the modern brand-restraint canon all refuse cheap moves (exclamation points, urgency timers, social-proof bombardment) precisely because their audience has been over-marketed to. The absence of selling becomes the strongest sell.

This is DDB's 1959 thesis ("Think Small.") applied to 2026 SaaS. The same principle: _only a brand actually confident in its product would write this way._ Restraint is a costly signal; only brands with margin to spare can afford it.

**Operational test:** Read your hero section aloud. Count exclamation points, count emoji, count adjectives, count instances of "the best / leading / world-class / innovative." If any of those counts is above zero, ask whether removing them makes the page more or less confident-sounding.

### Bonus: The closer is a callback

Conroy returns to the two classmates. Caples returns to "they laughed." VW Lemon returns to "pluck the lemons." Karbo's guarantee returns to the dollar amount. Linear's footer link returns to "Issue tracking is dead." Modern equivalent: **pin the second-to-last paragraph back to the opening image.** Symmetry is satisfying. Satisfied readers click.

## Build Your Swipe File

A teardown library is the single most under-leveraged asset in a copywriter's career. The masters all had one. Halbert kept binders. Sugarman kept a wall. Joanna Wiebe runs Copyhackers with a teardown machine attached. Here's how to build yours and keep it useful.

### Step 1 — Collect (passive)

- **Set up a "Swipe" folder** in your bookmarks, Notion, or a dedicated Raindrop.io / Workona collection.
- **Subscribe to teardown newsletters:** Marketing Examples (Harry Dry), Very Good Copy (Eddie Shleyner), Copywriting Examples (Harry Dry alt). Each one delivers 1–3 mini-teardowns weekly.
- **Save every piece of marketing that converts you personally.** If you almost bought it, save it. Your emotional reaction is the data.
- **Photograph direct mail.** Physical mail still works in 2026 and gets thrown away within a week. Snap a photo, file it.
- **Screen-record VSLs and ads.** Loom or QuickTime + a folder per vertical.

### Step 2 — Curate (semi-active, weekly)

Every Friday, spend 30 minutes reviewing the week's haul. For each piece you saved, decide:

- **Keep:** has at least one move worth stealing. Move to "Annotated" folder.
- **Toss:** interesting but not generative. Delete.
- **File for later:** interesting context, no current use.

The discipline: _most of what you save is bad_. Curation is what makes the file useful.

### Step 3 — Annotate (active, monthly)

Pick one piece per week. Sit down with a notebook (paper or markdown) and do the five-part teardown from this file:

1. **Original text:** paste verbatim with source link.
2. **Sentence-by-sentence dissection:** what is each line _doing_?
3. **Framework mapping:** awareness stage, sophistication, primary framework, Cialdini, Halbert specificity 0–10.
4. **What makes it timeless:** the principle a copywriter could steal in 2026.
5. **Modern adaptation:** how _you_ would write this for your current project.

Halbert's discipline: handwrite the copy. Yes, the whole thing. The friction forces you to feel the rhythm. Modern adaptation: copy-paste into a markdown file, then _re-type the headline and lead by hand_ before annotating.

### Step 4 — Re-read (passive, quarterly)

Every 90 days, re-read your annotated file from top to bottom. Two things will happen:

- You will see your own annotations through new eyes. Last quarter's "what makes it timeless" reads as obvious now. That's growth.
- You will spot patterns across pieces you didn't notice before. The Conroy callback move and the Caples opening move are _the same move at different scales_. You won't see that until you've read 30 teardowns.

### Step 5 — Force application (active, every project)

The swipe file is worthless if it sits on a shelf. The discipline:

- Before writing any new piece, open the swipe file and pick **two annotated pieces** with similar awareness/sophistication targeting.
- Don't copy. Adapt. Identify which moves apply to your project. Borrow the _structure_, replace the _content_.
- Note in your draft margins which swipe pieces you borrowed from. (This is also useful when reviewing — you can ask "did this actually do the move I borrowed from?")

### What to put in your starter file (priority order)

If you build only one, build it in this order:

1. **The WSJ Conroy letter** (the long-form skeleton)
2. **Ogilvy Rolls-Royce** (the proof-by-specificity skeleton)
3. **VW Think Small + Lemon** (the anti-hype + reframe skeleton)
4. **Apple Crazy Ones** (the identity manifesto skeleton)
5. **Linear, Stripe, Notion homepages** (the modern restraint canon)
6. **Liquid Death** (the voice-as-moat canon)
7. **Magic Spoon** (the contradiction-resolution canon)
8. **Ramit Earnable** (the modern info-product canon)
9. **Sackheim "Do You Make These Mistakes…"** (the indictment headline)
10. **Caples Piano** (the dramatization template)
11. **Halbert Coat of Arms + Dollar Bill** (the personalization + grabber)
12. **Karbo Lazy Man** (the identity reversal)

Then add: the best welcome email you've received this year. The best cold email. The best landing page from a competitor. The best ad you've seen on YouTube. Tag everything with awareness stage and sophistication level, so when you sit down to write Stage-5 sophistication copy at 11pm, you can pull the relevant references in 30 seconds.

### One last discipline

**Re-write the masters by hand, in their voice, for your product, once a month.** Pick a piece (Conroy, say) and write the equivalent piece for whatever you're currently selling. Not as a final draft. As an exercise. You will discover where your own product breaks the structure (which usually means your product needs a stronger frame, not that the structure is wrong). You will discover which moves you can't yet execute. You will accumulate, slowly, the muscle memory of the masters.

A swipe file you collect and never re-read is a museum. A swipe file you re-read and re-write is a gym.
