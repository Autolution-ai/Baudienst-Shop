## Why Headlines Matter

**Ogilvy** (1963): "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar." Your headline is the ad. Everything else is a footnote that 80% of readers will never reach. Spend the time accordingly — Ogilvy wrote 26 versions of the Rolls-Royce headline before settling on the electric-clock line that raised sales 50% in a year.

**Caples** (1932) proved it numerically: a change in the headline alone has multiplied response 19x in tested mail-order campaigns. Same body copy. Same offer. Same product. Different headline. **Halbert** put it most plainly: a headline's job is to make the reader say _"Hey, that's me / I want that / I need to know more — now."_ And **Sugarman** narrowed the brief further still: the headline's only job is to get the reader to read the first sentence. The first sentence's only job is to get them to read the second. Don't try to sell in the headline — try to get them inside.

## Caples' 35 Headline Formulas

From _Tested Advertising Methods_ (1932), Chapter 5. The canonical reference. Most modern headlines are a remix of one of these.

### Keyword-opener formulas (1–10)

1. "How to \_\_\_"
2. "How \_\_\_"
3. "Why \_\_\_"
4. "Which \_\_\_"
5. "Who else wants \_\_\_"
6. "Wanted — \_\_\_"
7. "This \_\_\_"
8. "Because \_\_\_"
9. "If **_, then _**"
10. "Advice to \_\_\_"

### Benefit-focused (11–19)

11. Testimonial format (quote-style headline)
12. Offer the reader a free test
13. Offer information of value
14. Story-based headline
15. Warning against delay/loss
16. First-person, advertiser-to-reader
17. Address a specific person or group
18. Question headline
19. Headline with facts and figures

### News-style (20–27)

20. "Introducing \_\_\_"
21. "Announcing \_\_\_"
22. Announcement words ("Finally," "Presenting," "Just Released," "At Last")
23. "New \_\_\_"
24. "Now \_\_\_"
25. "At Last \_\_\_"
26. Headline with a date
27. News-style report

### Price/offer (28–32)

28. Feature the price
29. Feature the reduced price
30. Feature a special offer
31. Feature an easy payment plan
32. Feature a free offer

### Length-based (33–35)

33. One-word headline
34. Two-word headline
35. Three-word headline

> **Use it like this:** when stuck, scan all 35. The right formula is almost always already on the list. Then move to the templates below for the worked structure.

## Email Subject Line Templates

Mobile previews cut at 35–45 characters. Test short. Lowercase often outperforms title case (looks like a personal note, not a broadcast). Numbers and questions raise opens. Mystery + relevance > clarity for cold lists; clarity > mystery for warm lists.

## CTA Microcopy Library

Generic CTAs are a tax. "Submit," "Learn more," and "Get started" leak conversion every day because they describe the form, not the user's reward. Match the CTA to the value the user is about to receive.

### Sign Up / Try / Start

1. **"Get started free"**
2. **"Start free — no card required"**
3. **"Try it (no card required)"**
4. **"Start my 14-day trial"**
5. **"Create my account"**
6. **"Join 4,127 founders"** (proof-laden)
7. **"Claim my seat"** (scarcity-laden)

### Demo / Request

8. **"Show me the demo"**
9. **"Book a 15-min walkthrough"**
10. **"See it in action"**
11. **"Talk to a human"**
12. **"Get a quote in 2 minutes"**

### Download / Lead Magnet

13. **"Send me the playbook"**
14. **"Get my [specific deliverable]"**
    → "Get my pricing template"
15. **"Email me the PDF"**
16. **"Download the 2026 report"**
17. **"Save my spot"** (webinar/event)

### Learn / Explore

18. **"Tell me more"**
19. **"See how it works"**
20. **"Walk me through it"**
21. **"Read the case study"**

### Purchase / Commitment

22. **"I want this"** (first-person; high-conversion in tested e-com)
23. **"Buy now — [$X]"**
24. **"Add to cart"** (don't overthink this one — it's the standard)
25. **"Upgrade to Pro"**
26. **"Lock in founder pricing"**
27. **"Get instant access"**

### Bonus — Negative-space CTAs (use with confidence)

28. **"No thanks, I'd rather lose customers"** (negative confirm-shame on a modal — controversial, test before deploying)
29. **"Maybe later"** (low-pressure dismiss; reduces back-button churn)

**Rules:**

- First person ("Get **my** report") tests +20–90% against second-person ("Get **your** report") in repeat studies — confirm in your data.
- Verb + reward beats verb alone. "Send me the playbook" > "Submit."
- Specificity beats generality. "Get my 30-day audit" > "Get started."

## Halbert's Specificity Conversions

The brain accepts specific numbers as evidence and rejects round numbers as marketing. Anywhere you see a generality in your draft, replace it with a specific. Steal this conversion table:

| Generic                 | Halbert-Specific                                            |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Lose weight             | Lose 23 pounds by Easter                                    |
| Saved money             | Saved $4,318.27 on his last vacation                        |
| Many customers          | 11,247 customers since 2019                                 |
| Years of experience     | 14 years and 3 months building B2B SaaS                     |
| Thousands of dollars    | $25,397 in 14 months                                        |
| Fast results            | First reply by Thursday at 4:12pm                           |
| A lot of subscribers    | 8,431 subscribers, 92% open rate                            |
| High conversion         | 4.7% of cold visitors book a demo                           |
| Most founders           | 73% of founders we surveyed                                 |
| Long-time customer      | Customer since March 2018                                   |
| Significant growth      | Revenue went from $4k MRR to $47k MRR in 11 months          |
| Improved deliverability | Inbox placement went from 41% to 94%                        |
| A small team            | Three of us in a Brooklyn apartment                         |
| A few minutes           | 4 minutes 12 seconds                                        |
| Better sleep            | Falls asleep in 7 minutes, wakes once instead of four times |
| Saved time              | Saved 11 hours/week, every week for 8 months                |
| Many integrations       | 87 integrations including Stripe, HubSpot, and Slack        |
| Increased traffic       | Organic traffic 8,400 → 71,200 in 9 months                  |

## Sugarman's First-Sentence Examples

The first sentence of long-form copy has exactly one job: get the reader to read the second sentence. Therefore: short, intriguing, _opens a loop_, doesn't sell. The classics:

1. **"I lied."** — Sugarman, BluBlocker
2. **"It was raining."** — Sugarman; pulls the reader into a scene before any pitch.
3. **"I was wrong."**
4. **"Yes, that headline is correct."** — confirms a wild claim from the H1, then forces the reader to continue for the proof.
5. **"Forget everything you've read about [topic]."**
   → "Forget everything you've read about cold email."
6. **"This may be the most important letter you read this year."** (Halbert classic — high-stakes, use with content that can pay it off)
7. **"There are two kinds of [type of people] in the world."**
   → "There are two kinds of founders in the world."
8. **"Most people don't believe me when I tell them this."**
9. **"Let me tell you a quick story."** (Permission-asking; lowers the bar to keep reading)
10. **"I'm going to make you an offer you can't refuse — but not yet."** (Open loop)
11. **"Three years ago, I made a decision I almost regretted."**
12. **"At 3am on a Tuesday in March, I got an email that changed how I price everything."**
13. **"Dear friend,"** (Halbert — instantly A-pile)
14. **"I almost didn't write this."**
15. **"This isn't for everyone."** (Disqualification = perceived value)

## Generating 30 Headline Variations from a Seed

The cardinal rule (see [ai-workflow.md](ai-workflow.md) for the full discipline): **write one strong human headline first.** It must already pass the diagnostic below. Then, and only then, use AI to generate 30 variations _in that direction_. Never ask AI for headlines from scratch — you'll get median output every time.

### The full prompt template

Paste into Claude / GPT exactly as written. Replace bracketed inputs.

```text
This is my strongest headline: [paste seed headline]

The hook is: [pain / promise / identity / curiosity / contrarian]
The mechanism is: [the specific differentiator that makes the claim believable —
  the new method, ingredient, sequence, system, named research, named person]
The audience: [role × company size × awareness stage × sophistication stage]
The promise: [the single most important outcome — in one sentence]

Generate 30 variations that preserve the underlying promise but vary:
- Structure (question, command, declarative, story, list)
- Opening word
- Specificity (number, named entity, time-bound detail)
- Rhythm and sentence length
- Emotional register (urgent, calm, contrarian, intimate, authoritative)

Constraints:
- Do NOT use em-dashes
- Do NOT use the words "unlock," "discover," "transform," "revolutionary,"
  "supercharge," "elevate," "seamless," "effortless," "leverage"
- Do NOT use three-item lists ("faster, cheaper, smarter")
- Do NOT use vague intensifiers ("absolutely," "truly," "incredibly")
- Every variation must include either a specific number, a named entity,
  or a verb a competitor wouldn't use

Output: numbered list 1–30, one headline per line, no commentary.
```

### Selection criteria

Of the 30 outputs, expect 6–10 to be usable. Score each against:

1. **Taste filter** — would a real person say this aloud without cringing?
2. **Variation filter** — does it bring a structural angle the seed didn't?
3. **Specificity filter** — number, named entity, or unique verb present?
4. **Awareness-stage match** — does it speak to the prospect's _current_ awareness, not where you wish they were?
5. **Could a competitor steal it word-for-word?** — if yes, kill it.

Keep your top 3–5 candidates. Run them against the Diagnostic below. Test the survivors.

## Headline Diagnostic — The 5-Question Test

Run every finalist through these five questions. If it fails any one, rewrite. If it fails two or more, scrap and start over.

### 1. Does it pass the 4 Us? (Mark Ford)

- **Useful** — does it offer the reader a clear benefit?
- **Urgent** — is there a reason to act now (real, not manufactured)?
- **Unique** — does it sound like you, or like everyone in the category?
- **Ultra-specific** — does it include a number, a named entity, or a time-bound detail?

Most weak headlines hit only "Useful." Elite headlines hit all four.

### 2. Does it match the awareness stage?

- **Stage 1 (Unaware):** story / news / identity hook. Never lead with product.
- **Stage 2 (Problem-Aware):** name the pain, then tease that a solution exists.
- **Stage 3 (Solution-Aware):** introduce the _mechanism_ — what makes you different.
- **Stage 4 (Product-Aware):** proof, offer stack, risk reversal, urgency.
- **Stage 5 (Most Aware):** direct offer, named price, deadline.

A Stage-1 audience reading Stage-4 copy bounces in 3 seconds.

### 3. Could a competitor steal it word-for-word?

If yes, it's too generic. Strip the company name and read it aloud — does it still sound like _you_, or could it run on any landing page in your category? Elite headlines are unstealable because the specificity (named research, founder voice, contrarian POV, mechanism) only fits one company.

### 4. Does it imply a mechanism, or just claim?

Stage-3+ markets reject claims without mechanisms. "Lose weight" is dead; "Lose weight with intermittent ketosis" survives. "Get more leads" is dead; "Get more leads with the 4-email reverse-funnel sequence" survives. The mechanism is the new lever. If your headline only claims, layer a mechanism into the subhead or H2 before publishing.

### 5. Does it pass the "would a real person say this aloud?" test?

Read it out loud, the way you'd say it to a friend at a coffee shop. If you'd be embarrassed to say it aloud, your audience will be embarrassed to read it. Cut every word that sounds like it was written by a marketer trying to sound like a marketer.

> **Final test (Sugarman):** does the headline make you want to read the first sentence? If not, you haven't earned the rest of the page. Rewrite.
