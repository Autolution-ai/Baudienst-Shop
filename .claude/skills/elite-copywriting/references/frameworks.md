# Copy Frameworks

The structural skeletons that good copy is hung on. Pick the framework AFTER you've diagnosed awareness stage, sophistication level, channel, and length — never before. The framework is a scaffold; the Big Idea is the soul.

## Framework Selection Guide

The wrong framework with the right words still loses. Diagnose first, then pick.

### Master decision table

| Awareness Stage   | Sophistication | Channel / Length              | Default Framework            | Backup               |
| ----------------- | -------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------- | -------------------- |
| 1. Unaware        | Any            | Long-form (sales letter, VSL) | **Story lead → PASTOR**      | Slippery Slide       |
| 1. Unaware        | Any            | Short (ad, social)            | **Story / News headline**    | 4 Ps                 |
| 2. Problem-Aware  | 1–2 (low)      | Landing page, ad              | **PAS**                      | BAB                  |
| 2. Problem-Aware  | 3–4 (high)     | Long-form                     | **PASTOR**                   | QUEST                |
| 3. Solution-Aware | 1–2            | Landing page                  | **BAB**                      | FAB                  |
| 3. Solution-Aware | 3–4            | Long-form, VSL                | **PASTOR + mechanism**       | AICPBSAWN            |
| 3. Solution-Aware | Any            | B2B website                   | **StoryBrand SB7**           | CAB hero + FAB body  |
| 4. Product-Aware  | Any            | Email, retargeting            | **4 Ps**                     | AIDA (compressed)    |
| 4. Product-Aware  | Any            | Long-form pitch               | **AICPBSAWN**                | PASTOR (offer-heavy) |
| 5. Most-Aware     | Any            | Email, SMS, push              | **SLAP**                     | 4 Ps (offer-only)    |
| Any               | 5 (identity)   | Brand site, manifesto         | **Identity story**           | SB7 with stakes      |
| Any               | Any            | Long-form anything            | **Slippery Slide** (overlay) | —                    |

### Channel × length cheat sheet

| Channel                  | Length         | Pick                                         |
| ------------------------ | -------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Cold email               | 50–100 words   | 4 Ps                                         |
| LinkedIn post            | 100–300 words  | BAB or PAS                                   |
| Paid social ad           | <50 words      | SLAP or PAS-compressed                       |
| Landing page hero        | 1 line + sub   | CAB (Clarity/Authority/Benefit) + 4 Us check |
| Landing page body        | 600–2,000 wd   | PAS → FAB → social proof → CTA               |
| B2B SaaS homepage        | 800–1,500 wd   | StoryBrand SB7                               |
| Long-form sales letter   | 2,500–8,000 wd | PASTOR or AICPBSAWN                          |
| Video sales letter (VSL) | 15–45 min      | PASTOR with Hook-Story-Offer                 |
| Product description      | 100–300 wd     | FAB                                          |
| Welcome email sequence   | 5–7 emails     | BAB across the sequence; PAS per email       |
| Abandoned cart email     | 50–200 wd      | PAS (loss-framed) or SLAP                    |
| Pricing page             | 300–800 wd     | FAB grid + objection answers                 |
| Case study               | 800–1,500 wd   | BAB (Before / After / Bridge = your method)  |

**Rule of thumb:** If you can't name the framework before you draft, you're going to ramble.

## Core Frameworks

### 1. AIDA — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action

**Origin:** E. St. Elmo Lewis, ~1898. The granddaddy.

**Structure**

| Stage     | What it does                          | Copy job                                      |
| --------- | ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Attention | Stop the scroll                       | Headline, hero visual, first sentence         |
| Interest  | Earn the next 30 seconds              | Hook, story, surprising fact, identity mirror |
| Desire    | Make them want it                     | Benefit + proof + mechanism + visualization   |
| Action    | Make the next step obvious + low-risk | Clear CTA + risk reversal + urgency           |

**When to use**

- Default skeleton for almost any short-to-mid format
- Single-page landing, ad creative, video script outline
- When in doubt, start here and add depth later

**When NOT to use**

- Stage-1 Unaware audiences needing extensive education (use story lead → PASTOR)
- Stage-5 Most-Aware (skip to SLAP)
- High-ticket considered purchases (use PASTOR / AICPBSAWN)

**Failure mode:** Treating AIDA as four paragraphs instead of four **outcomes the reader must experience**. The "Attention" step keeps working until they reach the CTA — it's not a section you finish.

**Worked example — paid social ad for a 1:1 productivity coaching offer:**

> **[A]** Your calendar is full and you're still behind.
>
> **[I]** Most "productivity" advice is for people whose problem is laziness. Yours isn't. The problem is that you're the bottleneck for 6 people, every system in your business routes through your inbox, and you can't think past the next Slack ping.
>
> **[D]** I coach exactly 4 founders at a time. We rebuild your week around the 3 decisions that actually move revenue, kill the meetings that don't, and install a Friday review that makes Monday calm. Average client recovers 11 hours/week in the first 30 days.
>
> **[A]** Apply for the May cohort (4 spots, closes Friday). 15-minute screening call. No pitch. → [link]

Each beat does one job. The CTA explicitly disqualifies (Kennedy's takeaway) to raise perceived value.

### 2. PAS — Problem → Agitate → Solution

**Origin:** Dan Kennedy / direct-response folklore. The workhorse of pain-driven copy.

**Structure**

| Stage    | What it does                       | Copy job                                       |
| -------- | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Problem  | Name the specific pain             | Concrete, observable, verbatim from VoC        |
| Agitate  | Make the pain present and personal | Consequences, future-pace the cost of inaction |
| Solution | Resolve the tension                | Your offer as the relief, with mechanism + CTA |

**When to use**

- Pain-driven offers: B2B services, health, financial, legal, IT
- Problem-Aware audiences (Stage 2)
- Anywhere the prospect has a felt, current, expensive problem

**When NOT to use**

- Stage-1 Unaware (they don't yet feel the problem — you need a story lead first)
- Aspirational/luxury offers (use BAB or identity copy)
- Categories where the pain is taboo (use indirection)

**Failure mode:** Under-agitating. The Problem is named but feels theoretical — the reader nods politely and bounces. The fix: get specific. Use the customer's exact phrasing. Quantify the cost. Make them feel it in their chest.

**Worked example — landing page for a tax-resolution service:**

> **[P]** You opened a letter from the IRS this week. Maybe last month. You haven't told your spouse. The number on the line that says "balance due" doesn't feel real.
>
> **[A]** Here's what isn't on that letter: in 90 days the IRS can levy your bank account without warning. They can garnish 25% of your paycheck before you see it. Your "Notice CP504" — the one buried under junk mail right now — is the legal countdown. Most people open the next one only after a paycheck disappears. By then the cheapest path is closed.
>
> **[S]** I'm an Enrolled Agent (the IRS's own credential). I've negotiated settlements for 1,400+ clients in 11 years — average reduction 47%. Free 20-minute call. We review the letter, tell you exactly which path you're on, and what it costs to fix. If you decide to hire us after, payment plans start at $250/mo. If not, you leave with a written game plan. → [link]

Notice the agitation does the heavy lifting: it adds the consequence the IRS letter omits.

### 3. BAB — Before → After → Bridge

**Origin:** Direct-response folklore; popularized in modern info-product copy.

**Structure**

| Stage  | What it does                       | Copy job                               |
| ------ | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Before | Where they are now (vividly)       | Current state, frustrations, frictions |
| After  | Where they want to be (visualized) | Future state, sensory and specific     |
| Bridge | How your offer gets them across    | Your mechanism, plan, and CTA          |

**When to use**

- Transformation offers (fitness, dating, coaching, courses)
- Solution-Aware audiences (Stage 3) who want a clear contrast
- Case studies and testimonials (their Before → After → your Bridge)

**When NOT to use**

- Audiences whose Before isn't shared (B2B with diverse pains)
- Mature markets (Sophistication 4–5) where the After is overclaimed
- High-ticket where proof matters more than vision (use PASTOR)

**Failure mode:** The After is vague. "Feel confident." "Have freedom." "Wake up energized." Readers can't see themselves there, so they don't desire it. Fix: borrow the After from real customers' words. Sensory detail. Specific Tuesday morning.

**Worked example — homepage for a sleep-coaching offer for new parents:**

> **[Before]** It's 3:47am. Your six-month-old has been awake since 2. Your spouse is on the floor pretending to sleep. You've Googled "is it normal for a baby to never sleep" so many times Chrome autocompletes it. You're a different person at work now and you can feel everyone noticing.
>
> **[After]** Two Sundays from now, you and your partner share an actual conversation over coffee at 7am. Your baby is in their crib, already awake but content. By 8pm tonight, you'll both be in bed with a show queued up — and you'll get six uninterrupted hours. Not "better." Solved.
>
> **[Bridge]** Our 14-night sleep plan rebuilds your child's night around three sleep cues that work for 91% of babies 4–18 months. You get a custom plan in 24 hours, daily check-ins with a certified pediatric sleep consultant, and a 14-night guarantee — if your baby isn't sleeping through, the next 14 are free. → [Get the plan]

The After is _sensory and specific_, not abstract. The Bridge is a mechanism, not a brochure.

### 4. FAB — Features → Advantages → Benefits

**Origin:** Bob Bly's _Copywriter's Handbook_. The "which means…" framework.

**Structure**

| Layer     | What it is                          | Question it answers    |
| --------- | ----------------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Feature   | What the product _is_ / has         | "What is it?"          |
| Advantage | What the feature _does_ differently | "So what?"             |
| Benefit   | What the user _gets_ in real life   | "What's in it for me?" |

**The mechanic:** Bridge every feature with **"which means…"** until you hit a lived benefit.

> _10-hour battery_ → which means _doesn't die on a transcontinental flight_ → which means _you arrive ready to present, not panicking at the gate_.

**When to use**

- Tech products, SaaS, hardware, anywhere features dominate
- Product pages and pricing pages (paired with comparison grids)
- B2B where multiple decision-makers want different layers

**When NOT to use**

- Identity/lifestyle products (use Schwartz Stage-5 identification)
- Stage-1 Unaware audiences (they don't care about features yet)
- Service offers where the "feature" is intangible (use BAB or PAS)

**Failure mode:** Stopping at "advantage" without translating to lived benefit. Half of all B2B SaaS copy lives in advantage-land ("real-time sync," "AI-powered") and never crosses the bridge.

**Worked example — feature block on a noise-cancelling headphone product page:**

| Feature                           | Advantage                                        | Benefit (lived)                                                        |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Adaptive ANC with 8 mics          | Cancels engine drone _and_ sudden voices         | You hear your kids when they call — but not the seat-kicker behind you |
| 40-hour battery (ANC on)          | Survives a round-trip JFK→Tokyo without charging | Stop packing the charger. Stop the airport-floor outlet-hunt.          |
| Multi-point Bluetooth (2 devices) | Phone + laptop simultaneously                    | Take the call. Don't re-pair. Don't miss the standup.                  |
| 4Hz–40kHz response                | Reproduces sub-bass + cymbal shimmer cleanly     | Music actually feels the way it did in your old studio monitors        |

**As prose (for the hero block):**

> A 40-hour battery means you fly to Tokyo, work the trip, and fly home — without packing a charger. Adaptive ANC means the engine drone disappears, but the gate agent's "we're boarding now" doesn't. Multi-point Bluetooth means your phone and laptop are both connected at once: take the standup, then the song picks up where it left off.

Every feature has crossed the bridge to a real moment.

### 5. PASTOR — Problem → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation → Offer → Response

**Origin:** Ray Edwards (_How to Write Copy That Sells_, 2016). The long-form workhorse.

**Structure**

| Stage          | What it does                                      |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Problem        | Name the pain precisely (PAS-style)               |
| Amplify        | Twist the knife — consequences, cost of inaction  |
| Story/Solution | Origin story of the solution; introduce mechanism |
| Transformation | Show what life becomes — vivid, sensory, specific |
| Offer          | What they get, in what order, at what price       |
| Response       | The ask — explicit CTA, urgency, risk reversal    |

**When to use**

- Long-form sales letters (2,500–10,000 words)
- Video sales letters (VSLs)
- Info-product launches, high-ticket coaching, supplements
- Problem-Aware and Solution-Aware audiences at sophistication 3–4

**When NOT to use**

- Short formats (<800 words — too many beats; collapse to PAS)
- Stage-5 Most-Aware (they don't need the story, they need the offer)
- Mature B2B where the "transformation" sounds infomercial-ish

**Failure mode:** Skipping the **Transformation** step. The copy goes Problem → Story → Offer, but the reader never _feels_ what becomes possible. The offer feels transactional and the close fails.

**Worked example — sales page for a $2,400 6-week course on AI for solo lawyers (abbreviated to one paragraph per beat):**

> **[P]** You're a solo attorney billing 1,800 hours a year and the work that pays you the most — drafting, research, client memos — is the work that costs you the most. Saturdays. Anniversaries. The bedtime stories you didn't read.
>
> **[A]** Meanwhile, your competitors using AI are quoting fixed fees on work you still hourly-bill, winning RFPs you don't see, and _answering client emails at 6:47pm on a Wednesday_ with a finished memo attached. The gap is widening every month. In 24 months, "lawyer who uses AI well" will be a baseline credential. The window to be early is closing.
>
> **[S]** I'm Maya. Solo practice 11 years, 60% commercial litigation. Two years ago I burned out, taught myself prompt engineering for legal work, and rebuilt my practice. Now I bill the same revenue in 22 hours/week. Last cohort, 47 solo attorneys went through this exact system. Eight of them sent me before/after time logs.
>
> **[T]** Six weeks from today: your standard memo (the kind that takes 4 hours) is done in 35 minutes with a first draft you'd actually sign your name to. Discovery review on a 12,000-page production is done in a day, not a week. You quote fixed fees on three engagement types and your average matter margin doubles. You log off at 5pm because the work is done, not because you gave up.
>
> **[O]** _AI for the Solo Attorney_ — 6 weekly cohort sessions (live, recorded), the 47-template prompt library specifically for U.S. legal work (motions, memos, discovery, client comms), a 4-hour deep-dive on ethics + privilege + confidentiality, and lifetime access to the community of past cohorts. Tuition $2,400 (or 3 × $850). 47 alumni testimonials with redacted before/after time logs at [link].
>
> **[R]** Cohort starts June 3. We cap at 24 attorneys (you get face-time during live calls; this is the entire point). 90-day money-back guarantee: complete weeks 1–4, do the assignments, and if you haven't saved 8 hours that month, full refund. → [Apply to join — 4 spots left]

The Transformation paragraph is doing the work that closes the sale. Without it the Offer feels like a pitch; with it the Offer feels like the obvious next step.

### 6. QUEST — Qualify → Understand → Educate → Stimulate → Transition

**Origin:** Michel Fortin. Designed for audiences that need filtering before the pitch.

**Structure**

| Stage      | What it does                                             |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Qualify    | Opens by speaking only to the right reader (self-select) |
| Understand | Demonstrates you know them better than they expected     |
| Educate    | Teaches them the new lens / mechanism / category         |
| Stimulate  | Builds desire by showing what becomes possible           |
| Transition | Hands them the CTA as the next step they already wanted  |

**When to use**

- Long-form sales letters with broad traffic where you need to filter
- High-ticket where the wrong client costs you money
- Niche professional/identity-driven offers ("This is for [specific person]")

**When NOT to use**

- Cold audiences where filtering reads as gatekeeping
- Short formats — there's no room to Understand + Educate

**Failure mode:** "Qualify" too narrow — you scare off warm prospects who'd convert. Fix: qualify by _self-description_ (what they're working on, what they want) not by demographics or revenue thresholds.

**Worked example — opening of a sales letter for a $15K-per-engagement positioning consultant:**

> **[Q]** This is for B2B founders between $2M and $20M ARR who feel like the company has stopped compounding. Sales cycles are getting longer. Your "value prop" is the third version this year. Marketing keeps changing the homepage. If that's not you, this won't land — and the rest of the page is 4,000 words of why.
>
> **[U]** I've worked with 31 companies in this band over the last seven years. The pattern is almost always the same: you outgrew the founder-led positioning that got you to $2M, hired a marketing leader who tried to professionalize it, and now nobody — including your sales team — can describe in one sentence who you're for and what you replace. Reps default to feature dumps. Your win rate against your closest competitor has slipped 15 points. You suspect the issue isn't sales execution; you don't know how to prove it.
>
> **[E]** Most positioning advice is for marketers — "find a category," "write a manifesto." That's downstream. The real lever is upstream: Dunford's 5+1 components, run as a working session with sales, marketing, product, and the CEO in one room, with a structured input format from your last 20 lost deals. The output isn't a tagline. It's a one-page decision document that names who you're for, who you're not, what alternative you replace, and the unique value only you provide. Everything else — homepage, pitch deck, sales script — gets rewritten downstream of _that_.
>
> **[S]** Companies that get this right see win rate against their main competitor rise 10–25 points within two quarters. Sales cycles compress. Marketing finally has a brief. The founder stops being the only person who can tell the story.
>
> **[T]** I take 4 engagements at a time. Each is a 6-week sprint: 2 weeks of customer + lost-deal interviews, a 2-day working session with your team, and 3 weeks of artifact production (positioning doc, homepage rewrite, pitch deck rewrite, sales script). $15,000 fixed fee. If you want to know whether your situation fits, the next step is a 30-minute call where I ask 8 questions. No pitch. → [Book the call]

The Qualify is permissive (a wide self-description), not exclusive. The Understand wins trust. The Educate teaches a new lens. The Stimulate is restrained. The Transition is low-friction.

### 7. 4 Ps — Promise → Picture → Proof → Push

**Origin:** Henry Hoke, _The Hoke Report_ / classic direct response.

**Structure**

| P       | What it does                               |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Promise | A big, specific, beneficial claim          |
| Picture | Make them see themselves with it           |
| Proof   | Evidence the promise is real (data, names) |
| Push    | Clear, low-friction CTA                    |

**When to use**

- Cold email
- Short ads, social posts, LinkedIn DMs
- Anywhere you have <300 words to land the punch

**When NOT to use**

- Long-form (use PASTOR / AICPBSAWN instead)
- Stage-1 Unaware (the Promise sounds like puffery; you need a story lead)

**Failure mode:** Promise too big, Proof too weak. The reader's skepticism alarm fires at the Promise and the Proof can't recover. Fix: shrink the Promise until it's believable _given the Proof you actually have_.

**Worked example — 78-word cold email to a VP of Engineering:**

> Subject: **6 fewer outages last quarter — without rewriting**
>
> **[Promise]** We help platform teams at 200–800-person SaaS cut Sev-1 frequency 40–70% without changing your stack.
>
> **[Picture]** Your on-call rotation actually sleeps. Your engineers stop pager-driven context-switching. Postmortems shrink.
>
> **[Proof]** Last 4 customers (Linear, Mercury, Vanta, one stealth): average Sev-1 reduction 53% in Q1 after install. 3 references on a call.
>
> **[Push]** Worth a 20-minute look? Reply with a yes and I'll send three time options. — Sam

Nothing extra. Every sentence carries a beat.

### 8. 4 Us — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific

**Origin:** Mark Ford / Michael Masterson, AWAI. A **headline checklist**, not a full framework.

**Use it to score every headline / subject line / hero line on four dimensions:**

| Dimension      | Question                                    | Score 1–4 |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| Useful         | Is there a benefit / promise to the reader? |           |
| Urgent         | Is there a reason to read NOW (not later)?  |           |
| Unique         | Is the claim new, fresh, or differentiated? |           |
| Ultra-specific | Is the language concrete (numbers, names)?  |           |

**Pass score:** 3+ on at least three of the four. Most weak headlines hit only "Useful."

**Worked examples — scoring real headlines:**

| Headline                                                              | U   | Ur  | Un  | Us  | Verdict      |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------- | --- | --- | --- | --- | ------------ |
| "Get more leads"                                                      | 2   | 1   | 1   | 1   | Fail (5/16)  |
| "Improve your sales pipeline"                                         | 2   | 1   | 1   | 1   | Fail         |
| "47% more qualified leads in 6 weeks — without paid ads"              | 4   | 3   | 3   | 4   | Pass (14/16) |
| "How a Boise dentist tripled new-patient bookings using a $40 letter" | 3   | 2   | 4   | 4   | Pass (13/16) |
| "Final 48 hours — Q1 cohort closes Friday"                            | 3   | 4   | 2   | 4   | Pass (13/16) |

**Failure mode:** Hitting 1–2 of 4 and calling it done. Most "vague but professional" copy lives here.

### 9. StoryBrand SB7 — Donald Miller

**Structure**

| Element                     | The role                                                                |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. A Character              | The **customer** is the hero (not you)                                  |
| 2. Has a Problem            | External (the thing), Internal (how they feel), Philosophical (the why) |
| 3. And Meets a Guide        | You — empathy + authority (NOT the hero)                                |
| 4. Who Gives Them a Plan    | 3 steps. Clear. Process or agreement.                                   |
| 5. And Calls to Action      | Direct (buy) + Transitional (free thing for the not-ready)              |
| 6. That Helps Avoid Failure | What's at stake if they don't act                                       |
| 7. And Ends in Success      | The transformation, vividly                                             |

**When to use**

- B2B websites and homepages
- Brand messaging across a company
- Anywhere clarity matters more than emotion
- Service businesses with longer consideration

**When NOT to use**

- Direct-response sales letters (use PASTOR. SB7 is too compressed for $5K+ offers on a single page)
- Identity / lifestyle brands at Sophistication 5 (use identity copy)
- B2C impulse / commodity (use SLAP)

**Failure mode:** Making the **brand the hero** instead of the customer. "We're the leading…" "Our award-winning team…" Every "we" sentence is the brand stealing the hero role. Fix: count "you" vs "we" in the first 200 words. "You" should win 3:1.

**Worked example — homepage for a fictional B2B SaaS called "Ledgerline" (close-the-books software for mid-market controllers):**

> **[Hero — Character + Problem]**
> _H1:_ You shouldn't close the books at 11pm on the 5th of the month.
> _Sub:_ Ledgerline reconciles 14 accounting systems, flags the 6 entries that need your judgment, and turns your monthly close from a 9-day grind into a 2-day review.
> _CTA:_ [See a 4-minute demo] [Talk to a controller who uses it]
>
> **[Problem — three layers]**
> External: "It's the 5th. We're still chasing intercompany variances."
> Internal: "Every close I feel one mistake away from losing credibility with the CFO."
> Philosophical: "Controllers shouldn't be the bottleneck. Judgment should be the bottleneck — not data wrangling."
>
> **[Guide — empathy + authority]**
> We built Ledgerline because our founder closed the books at three mid-market companies and watched the same 9-day cycle every month. We've now processed 18,000+ closes across 240 controllers. The Big 4 alums on our team review every workflow before release.
>
> **[Plan — 3 clear steps]**
>
> 1. Connect your ERPs, banks, and sub-ledgers (45 minutes, with us).
> 2. Run your first close in parallel with your current process (one month).
> 3. Cut your close to 2 days. Cancel the parallel run when you're ready.
>
> **[CTA]**
> Direct: [Start a 30-day pilot] Transitional: [Download "The 9-Day Close Audit" — free]
>
> **[Avoiding failure — the stakes]**
> Without an automated close: monthly burnout cycles, audit anxiety, controllers leaving for jobs with better tooling. Most companies wait until their controller resigns to fix this.
>
> **[Success]**
> 90 days in, you spend the 5th reviewing exceptions, not chasing variances. Your CFO has clean numbers by noon. Your team books a 2pm finish on close day. You start to actually trust the close — which means you start to act on what it tells you.

Note: the brand is the **guide**. The customer (the controller) is the hero throughout.

### 10. AICPBSAWN — Robert Collier / Dan Kennedy lineage

**Attention → Interest → Credibility → Proof → Benefits → Scarcity → Action → Warn → Now**

**Structure**

| Letter | Stage       | Job                                         |
| ------ | ----------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| A      | Attention   | Stop the scroll                             |
| I      | Interest    | Earn the next 30 seconds                    |
| C      | Credibility | Establish authority (yours + the offer's)   |
| P      | Proof       | Evidence — data, testimonials, before/after |
| B      | Benefits    | What they actually get                      |
| S      | Scarcity    | Why they can't wait                         |
| A      | Action      | The explicit ask                            |
| W      | Warn        | What happens if they don't                  |
| N      | Now         | Final urgency push                          |

**When to use**

- Long-form sales letters (1,500+ words)
- High-ticket info-products, supplements, financial services
- Promotions to Product-Aware audiences (Stage 4)

**When NOT to use**

- Anything under 1,500 words (too many steps; collapse to PASTOR or PAS)
- Brand sites / SaaS homepages (feels like infomercial)
- Markets fatigued by direct-response cadence

**Failure mode:** Trying to hit all 9 beats in 600 words — the copy becomes a checklist instead of a flow. Fix: if you can't give each beat 100+ words of weight, switch frameworks.

**Worked example — the spine of a 4,000-word financial newsletter sales letter (one line per beat):**

> **A** — "Why the next 14 months will be the worst on record for U.S. retirees holding bonds."
> **I** — The 1981 parallel that almost nobody is publishing about.
> **C** — Why I (former Fed economist, 23 years) am the analyst writing this.
> **P** — The five charts that show the pattern is already underway.
> **B** — How my $97/year newsletter walks subscribers through monthly rebalancing — the same model that produced 8.2% real return for our subscribers 2008–2024.
> **S** — Charter membership at $47 closes in 72 hours, then it's $97.
> **A** — Join here.
> **W** — If you wait, you pay double _and_ miss the May allocation update.
> **N** — Click below now; you'll receive the May update within 5 minutes.

### 11. SLAP — Stop → Look → Act → Purchase

**Origin:** Direct-response folklore for impulse offers.

**Structure**

| Stage    | Job                                               |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Stop     | Pattern interrupt — scroll-stopping visual / hook |
| Look     | One-line benefit promise that makes them lean in  |
| Act      | Clear, single-action CTA — frictionless           |
| Purchase | One-click checkout / single tap                   |

**When to use**

- Impulse offers, low-ticket ($1–$50)
- Paid social ads for established products
- Stage-5 Most-Aware audiences (existing customers, retargeting)
- Restocks, flash sales, abandoned-cart recovery

**When NOT to use**

- Considered purchases (B2B, high-ticket, services)
- Stages 1–3 (audiences who need education first)
- Anywhere the buyer needs to convince a second person

**Failure mode:** Using SLAP for a considered purchase. The reader stops, looks, then asks the questions you didn't answer — and leaves.

**Worked example — Instagram ad for a restocked $32 product:**

> **[Stop]** _Visual: the product on a clean surface, the one detail people screenshot._
> **[Look]** Back in stock. (Sold out in 4 days last drop.)
> **[Act]** [Shop now →]
> **[Purchase]** Apple Pay enabled. Free shipping over $25. Three sizes in stock.

That's it. No story. No PASTOR. No hero's journey. Stop, look, act, purchase.

### 12. Slippery Slide — Joe Sugarman

**Origin:** _The Adweek Copywriting Handbook_. Not a structural framework — a **discipline overlay** for any long-form copy.

**The core rule:**

> The only purpose of the first sentence is to get the reader to read the second sentence. The only purpose of the second sentence is to get them to read the third. And so on, all the way to the CTA.

**Mechanics**

- First sentence: short (5–10 words), intriguing, opens a loop. **Never** sells.
- Use connectors that pull the eye forward: _"But there's more." "Let me explain." "Here's the strange part." "What happened next surprised me."_
- Curiosity gaps at the end of paragraphs.
- No paragraph longer than 3 lines on mobile. Visual descent matters.

**When to use**

- Long-form sales letters
- VSL scripts
- Founder stories, email newsletters, longer LinkedIn posts
- Any time scroll-depth is the bottleneck

**When NOT to use**

- Short-form / headlines (no "slide" to engineer)
- Reference content (docs, FAQs) where readers want answers, not flow

**Failure mode:** Front-loading the value prop in the first paragraph. The reader gets what they need and leaves before the offer. Fix: hide value behind curiosity until the reader has descended at least 60% of the page.

**Worked example — first 200 words of a long-form course sales page using Slippery Slide:**

> I almost didn't write this letter.
>
> Three months ago a former student emailed me. He'd been through my course in 2023. The email had one line.
>
> "It worked again."
>
> I had to scroll down to figure out what he meant. What I read in that thread is the reason this page exists — and the reason I'm reopening enrollment after saying I wouldn't.
>
> Let me back up.
>
> In 2021, I was running a coaching business that was, by every measurable signal, working. Six-figure revenue. Repeat clients. Testimonials.
>
> And I was miserable.
>
> The reason had nothing to do with the work — and everything to do with how I'd structured the offer. (I'll get to that in a moment. It's the entire mechanism of what I'm about to show you.)
>
> So I tore the business down to the studs. Built it back up around three constraints I'd never seen anyone else use. Within four months, revenue had doubled, hours had halved, and — for the first time in two years — I was sleeping past 6am.
>
> Then I taught it to 47 people. Here's what happened to them.

Notice: no offer mentioned. Every sentence pulls forward. Curiosity loops opened ("It worked again" — at what?), mechanism teased ("three constraints I'd never seen anyone else use"), proof previewed ("47 people"). The reader is _down the slide_ — and the actual offer is still 2,000 words away.

## Mark Ford's 6 Lead Types

From _Great Leads_ (Mark Ford / Michael Masterson + John Forde). The first 100–500 words of a sales letter or sales page — the lead — has one job: hook the right reader at their awareness stage and pull them into the body.

**Map lead type to awareness stage. This decision bends every subsequent choice on the page.**

| Lead Type               | Hook                                        | Best Awareness Stage | Use When                                                               |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Offer**            | The deal itself — price, bonus, guarantee   | 4–5 Most-Aware       | Existing customers, retargeting, restocks, end-of-cohort closes        |
| **2. Promise**          | A specific, big benefit claim               | 3–4                  | Solution-Aware audiences shopping mechanisms; product-launch headlines |
| **3. Problem-Solution** | Name the pain → tease the fix               | 2 Problem-Aware      | Pain-driven audiences, services, health, financial                     |
| **4. Big Secret**       | Curiosity gap around a withheld mechanism   | 2–3                  | Sophistication 3–4 markets — the _mechanism_ IS the news               |
| **5. Proclamation**     | Bold contrarian claim / news / prediction   | 1–2                  | Unaware or Problem-Aware — break their model of the world              |
| **6. Story**            | Narrative hook — character, scene, conflict | 1 Unaware            | Cold traffic that doesn't yet feel the problem                         |

### Worked snippets for each lead type

**1. Offer lead** (Most-Aware)

> Final 36 hours. The Spring cohort closes Friday at midnight. Tuition $1,200 off through tonight. → [Enroll]

**2. Promise lead** (Solution-Aware)

> 47% more qualified leads in 6 weeks — without paid ads, cold outreach, or hiring an SDR. Here's the system 31 B2B founders ran last quarter.

**3. Problem-Solution lead** (Problem-Aware)

> If you've ever stared at a sales pipeline that looked healthy in March and empty in June — and you can't explain why — this is for you. The pattern repeats because of a single forecasting error almost every founder makes.

**4. Big Secret lead** (Sophistication 3+)

> There's a single line of code most platform teams have never heard of that cut Sev-1 incident frequency by 53% across four mid-market SaaS companies last quarter. It isn't a tool. It isn't a vendor. And I'm about to show you exactly what it is — and why nobody's talking about it.

**5. Proclamation lead** (Unaware → Problem-Aware)

> Most "productivity advice" is for the wrong person. If you're a founder, the entire genre is actively making you worse at your job. Here's why — and what to do instead.

**6. Story lead** (Unaware)

> The day I knew I had to leave Goldman wasn't the day my bonus was cut. It was a Tuesday in November, on the 28th floor, when I watched a senior partner cry quietly in the men's room and pretend he hadn't. I'd been telling myself I'd give it three more years. That night I called my wife from the cab and told her I was done. What I built in the 18 months after that conversation — and the one decision that made it possible — is the entire reason this letter exists.

### Schwartz × Lead Type quick map

| Awareness Stage   | Lead types that work                       | Don't use           |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------- |
| 1. Unaware        | Story, Proclamation                        | Offer, Promise      |
| 2. Problem-Aware  | Problem-Solution, Proclamation, Big Secret | Offer               |
| 3. Solution-Aware | Big Secret, Promise                        | Story (too slow)    |
| 4. Product-Aware  | Promise, Offer                             | Story, Proclamation |
| 5. Most-Aware     | Offer                                      | Everything else     |

## Bonus Frameworks

### PAPA — Problem, Advantages (of solving), Proof, Action

A leaner cousin of PAS that emphasizes **proof before ask**. Useful when the audience is skeptical and Stage 3+.

| Stage      | Job                                                     |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Problem    | Name the pain                                           |
| Advantages | What changes when it's solved (not features — outcomes) |
| Proof      | Evidence — case studies, data, named references         |
| Action     | The ask                                                 |

**Use when:** B2B mid-market, RFP responses, proposal cover letters, anywhere the buyer has been burned before.
**Don't use when:** Unaware audiences (no Problem felt yet).

---

### ACCA — Awareness, Comprehension, Conviction, Action

A psychological progression framework — closer to the cognitive journey than the narrative one.

| Stage         | Job                                                      |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness     | Make the problem/opportunity visible                     |
| Comprehension | Explain the mechanism — how the solution works           |
| Conviction    | Build belief through proof, demonstration, risk reversal |
| Action        | The ask                                                  |

**Use when:** Education-heavy categories (insurance, financial services, complex SaaS), B2B with multiple stakeholders.
**Don't use when:** Stage-5 Most-Aware audiences who already have all four — they just need the offer.

---

### OATH — Oblivious, Apathetic, Thinking, Hurting

Not a structural framework — a **buyer-state diagnostic**, similar to Schwartz's awareness stages. Match copy intensity to state:

| State     | Reader thinks…                      | Match with                              |
| --------- | ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Oblivious | "I don't have this problem"         | Story lead, proclamation, identity hook |
| Apathetic | "I have it but I don't care enough" | Agitate, future-pace consequences       |
| Thinking  | "I might solve this; I'm comparing" | Mechanism, differentiation, proof       |
| Hurting   | "I need this now"                   | Offer, scarcity, risk reversal, action  |

**Use when:** Diagnosing the _intensity_ of the prospect's state, not just their awareness.

---

### CAB — Clarity, Authority, Benefit

A **hero-section** framework. Three things every landing-page hero must do in 6 seconds:

| Letter    | Question                                 |
| --------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Clarity   | What is this, in one sentence?           |
| Authority | Why should I trust the people behind it? |
| Benefit   | What changes for me?                     |

**Hero structure that hits all three:**

> **H1 (Benefit):** Close your books in 2 days, not 9.
> **Sub (Clarity):** Ledgerline automates reconciliation across 14 accounting systems for mid-market controllers.
> **Authority signals (logos / testimonial / press):** Used by 240 controllers at companies $50M–$500M.

**Use when:** Drafting/reviewing any landing-page hero. Run it as a checklist.

---

### Danny Iny's 6+1 — Context, Attention, Desire, Gap, Solution, Call to Action + Credibility throughout

Iny's argument: AIDA misses the first step (**Context** — why is the reader here at all?) and forgets that **Credibility** isn't a stage, it's an overlay running through every beat.

| Stage             | Job                                                                  |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Context        | Why are they here? What brought them to this page?                   |
| 2. Attention      | Grab the focus                                                       |
| 3. Desire         | Why this matters to them now                                         |
| 4. Gap            | The chasm between where they are and where they want to be           |
| 5. Solution       | Your offer as the bridge                                             |
| 6. Call to Action | The ask                                                              |
| +1 Credibility    | Trust signals woven through every beat (not concentrated at the end) |

**Use when:** Long-form content marketing, blog-to-conversion pages, anywhere the traffic arrives with mixed intent and you need to acknowledge the visit itself before pitching.

## Operating Principles

Frameworks are scaffolds. These principles apply _across_ all of them:

1. **Diagnose stage before picking framework.** Most underperforming copy targets a Stage-4 audience with Stage-1 copy or vice versa. The framework is downstream of awareness.
2. **One framework per piece.** Pick one and commit. Mixed frameworks read as confused.
3. **Slippery Slide overlays everything.** Whatever framework you pick, every sentence's job is the next sentence.
4. **The Big Idea is the soul; the framework is the skeleton.** Frameworks structure; they don't substitute for a Big Idea.
5. **Specificity beats template adherence.** A specific PAS beats a generic PASTOR. Specificity is the variable, not the framework choice.
6. **Read aloud.** The framework should be invisible to the reader. If they can feel the scaffolding, you're showing your work.
7. **Match length to format, not framework to ambition.** PASTOR on a 200-word ad fails. PAS on a 6,000-word VSL fails. Pick the framework the length supports.
8. **Frameworks fail silently.** Wrong framework = polite bounce. Diagnose the bounce by checking awareness × sophistication × framework alignment before rewriting.

**The single test:** After picking a framework, ask — _"Could I name what each section is doing, out loud, to a stranger?"_ If yes, you've structured the piece. If not, you're decorating.
