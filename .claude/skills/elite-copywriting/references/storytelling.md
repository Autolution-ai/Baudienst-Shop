## Why Storytelling Works

Story is not decoration. It's the most efficient way to move a human being from one belief to another.

### The Neuroscience

**Mirror neurons** (Giacomo Rizzolatti, Parma 1992). When you watch someone reach for a cup, the same motor neurons fire in your brain as fire in theirs. When you read a sentence about someone reaching for a cup, the _same thing happens_. Narrative literally simulates experience inside the reader's body. Bullet points don't.

**Dopamine peaks in anticipation** (Robert Sapolsky). The dopamine spike isn't at the reward — it's just before. This is why open loops and cliffhangers work: an unresolved question keeps a reader in a heightened, leaned-in state. A bullet list is fully resolved on arrival; a story isn't.

**System 2 bypass** (Kahneman). System 2 is your skeptical, effortful, logical mind — the part that argues with marketing copy. System 1 is fast, associative, narrative. Story rides into System 1 under the cover of "I'm just hearing a story" and lands the claim before System 2 wakes up. Argue with a customer and they argue back. Tell them a story and they listen.

**The simulation chamber** (Heath Brothers, _Made to Stick_). Stories are mental flight simulators. The reader rehearses the outcome in their head — using your product, achieving the result, getting the win. That rehearsal is the closest thing copy has to a free trial.

### What This Means Operationally

- Every product page can open with a story ("here's how we got here…")
- Every benefit can be illustrated with a 1-2 sentence vignette
- Every case study should be told as a narrative arc, not a feature list
- Every founder has an origin story — even if they haven't written it yet

## The Hero's Journey

Joseph Campbell, _The Hero with a Thousand Faces_ (1949). The monomyth underneath every great story from Gilgamesh to Star Wars. For copy, we collapse the 17-stage Campbell version into 7 beats.

### The 7 Beats

| Beat                       | Description                            | Maps to copy element                           |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **Ordinary World**         | Hero's normal, dissatisfying life      | Opening that mirrors the reader's status quo   |
| **Call to Adventure**      | Something disrupts the routine         | The triggering pain or possibility             |
| **Refusal**                | Hero hesitates, doubts they can        | The objections (cost, risk, "is this for me?") |
| **Meeting the Mentor**     | A guide appears with wisdom + tools    | Your brand enters the story (this is you)      |
| **Crossing the Threshold** | Hero commits, leaves the familiar      | The purchase / signup / first action           |
| **Tests**                  | Struggles, setbacks, learning          | Onboarding, early wins, plateaus               |
| **Return with the Elixir** | Hero returns transformed, helps others | Outcome + testimonial + referral               |

### Worked Example: A Fitness Coach's Origin Story

> **Ordinary World:** Three years ago I was 47 pounds overweight, working 70-hour weeks as a corporate lawyer in Toronto. I had a closet full of suits that didn't fit and a daughter who'd stopped asking me to play.
>
> **Call to Adventure:** Then my dad — 62, in better shape than me — was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. I sat in the doctor's office and watched my future.
>
> **Refusal:** I'd tried every diet. Keto, paleo, intermittent fasting, the one where you only eat potatoes. I'd hire a trainer for six weeks, quit, gain it all back. The thought of starting again exhausted me before I started.
>
> **Meeting the Mentor:** A former Olympic weightlifter I'd met at a charity dinner sat me down and drew six circles on a napkin. He said: "Forget diets. Six habits. Twelve weeks. That's it."
>
> **Crossing the Threshold:** I quit the gym. I bought a $40 kettlebell. I started.
>
> **Tests:** Week three I almost quit. Week six I noticed my wedding ring was loose. Week nine my daughter said "Daddy you look strong."
>
> **Return with the Elixir:** Forty-seven pounds gone. Off two medications. Now I teach this system to fathers over 40 who don't have time to be in the gym four days a week. The six habits are in the free guide below.

That's a coaching landing page hero section. Every beat earns its place. Every detail is concrete (47 pounds, 70 hours, $40 kettlebell, 6 circles on a napkin).

## StoryBrand SB7

Donald Miller, _Building a StoryBrand_ (2017). The most-used B2B narrative framework in the world. Treat this in depth — most brands violate the central rule.

### The 7 Parts

> A **Character** with a **Problem** meets a **Guide** who gives them a **Plan** and calls them to **Action**, helping them avoid **Failure** and reach **Success**.

| Part          | Question it answers                   | Common failure                                                                                                           |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Character** | Who is the hero?                      | Brands cast themselves as the hero. The customer is the hero.                                                            |
| **Problem**   | What pain are they in?                | Vague problem ("inefficiency") instead of specific ("your sales team spends 14 hours/week updating a CRM nobody trusts") |
| **Guide**     | Who appears with empathy + authority? | Authority without empathy = arrogant. Empathy without authority = unqualified. Need both.                                |
| **Plan**      | What's the simple path forward?       | Too many steps. 3-step plans convert best.                                                                               |
| **Action**    | What's the next click?                | Hidden, weak, or multiple competing CTAs                                                                                 |
| **Failure**   | What happens if they don't act?       | Skipping stakes — no urgency, no consequence                                                                             |
| **Success**   | What does winning look like?          | Generic ("grow your business") instead of specific ("close 30% more deals with the team you already have")               |

### The Single Most-Violated Rule

**The customer is Luke. You are Yoda.**

In Star Wars, Yoda is older, wiser, more powerful — and still not the hero. He doesn't lift the X-wing to win the day. He teaches Luke to. The story is Luke's transformation, not Yoda's wisdom.

Most B2B sites get this wrong. They open with their story ("Founded in 2017 by ex-Google engineers, we set out to revolutionize…"). Wrong hero. The reader didn't come to read your bio. They came because they have a problem and they're trying to figure out if you can help.

### The Three Problem Layers

Miller's hidden power move: every problem has three layers, and great copy addresses all three.

| Layer             | What it is                               | Example (CRM software)                                                  |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **External**      | The tangible, surface problem            | "Our CRM is a mess and reps don't update it"                            |
| **Internal**      | How the external problem makes them feel | "I feel like I have no visibility into the pipeline. I'm flying blind." |
| **Philosophical** | Why this problem is just wrong           | "Sales teams shouldn't have to choose between selling and reporting."   |

Address all three and you're not selling features — you're selling resolution at every level a human cares about.

### The Two CTA Types

- **Direct CTA:** "Start free trial" / "Book a demo" / "Buy now"
- **Transitional CTA:** "Download the guide" / "Watch the 4-minute demo"

Most pages need both. Direct for ready buyers. Transitional for the 95% who aren't ready yet but want to keep learning.

### Worked Example: SB7 on a SaaS Landing Page

A B2B sales-intelligence tool:

> **(Character + Problem — hero)**
> Your sales team is drowning in dashboards and still missing quota.
>
> **(Internal problem)**
> You can't tell which deals are real until they slip. Your VP wants a forecast you don't believe. Mondays feel like guessing.
>
> **(Guide — empathy + authority)**
> We've spent eight years inside 200+ sales orgs. We've seen the spreadsheets. We've watched the slipped quarters. We built [Product] because nobody should have to forecast on vibes.
>
> **(Plan — 3 steps)**
>
> 1. Connect your CRM in 4 minutes.
> 2. We score every open deal against 47 win/loss signals from your own history.
> 3. Your forecast updates every Sunday night — before the Monday meeting.
>
> **(Direct CTA)** Start your 14-day trial → **(Transitional CTA)** See the 4-minute product tour
>
> **(Failure stakes)**
> Another quarter of "we'll know by Thursday." Another rep PIP'd because the deal you both believed in vanished. Another forecast meeting you'd rather skip.
>
> **(Success)**
> A pipeline your VP trusts. Forecasts that hit within 5%. Mondays where you walk in already knowing.

Notice: the customer is the hero of every sentence. The brand only appears as the guide with the napkin-drawing.

### Worked Example: SB7 on an Agency Homepage

A design studio (notice the restraint — agency sites tend toward bragging):

> **(Hero)** Most B2B SaaS sites look like they were built in 2018. Yours doesn't have to.
>
> **(Internal problem)** You know it's costing you. The CFO calls before the demo because the site didn't earn the question. The candidate ghosts because the careers page reads like a 1990s job board.
>
> **(Guide)** We've redesigned sites for 40+ Series-B-and-beyond SaaS companies. Two of them IPO'd with the site we built. We work in pods of three for six weeks. Then we ship.
>
> **(Plan)**
>
> 1. Two-week strategy + messaging sprint
> 2. Three-week design + build sprint
> 3. One-week QA, launch, and 30-day support
>
> **(CTA)** Book a 20-minute fit call → **(Transitional)** See the last 6 projects
>
> **(Stakes implied through case studies, not stated):** Four case studies, each with a before/after revenue or pipeline number.

## Pixar's 22 Rules

Emma Coats (former Pixar story artist) tweeted these in 2011. They're the operating system behind films that gross a billion dollars apiece. Most of them apply directly to copy.

### The 22 Rules

1. You admire a character for _trying_ more than for their successes.
2. Keep in mind what's interesting to the audience, not what's fun to do as a writer.
3. Trying for theme is important, but you won't see what the story is actually about till you're at the end of it. Now rewrite.
4. **The Story Spine:** Once upon a time there was **_. Every day, _**. One day **_. Because of that, _**. Because of that, **_. Until finally _**.
5. Simplify. Focus. Combine characters. Hop over detours. You'll feel like you're losing valuable stuff but it sets you free.
6. What is your character good at, comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them. Challenge them. How do they deal?
7. Come up with your ending before you figure out your middle. Endings are hard; get yours working up front.
8. Finish your story, let go even if it's not perfect. In an ideal world you have both, but move on. Do better next time.
9. When you're stuck, make a list of what _wouldn't_ happen next. Lots of times the material to get you unstuck will show up.
10. Pull apart the stories you like. What you like in them is part of you; you've got to recognise it before you can use it.
11. Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you'll never share it with anyone.
12. Discount the 1st thing that comes to mind. And the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th — get the obvious out of the way. Surprise yourself.
13. Give your characters opinions. Passive/malleable might seem likable to you as you write, but it's poison to the audience.
14. **Why must you tell THIS story?** What's the belief burning within you that your story feeds off of? That's the heart of it.
15. If you were your character, in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty lends credibility to unbelievable situations.
16. What are the stakes? Give us reason to root for the character. What happens if they don't succeed? Stack the odds against.
17. No work is ever wasted. If it's not working, let go and move on — it'll come back around to be useful later.
18. You have to know yourself: the difference between doing your best and fussing. Story is testing, not refining.
19. **Coincidences to get characters into trouble are great; coincidences to get them out of it are cheating.**
20. Exercise: take the building blocks of a movie you dislike. How do you rearrange them into what you _do_ like?
21. You gotta identify with your situation/characters; can't just write 'cool'. What would make YOU act that way?
22. What's the essence of your story? Most economical telling of it? If you know that, you can build out from there.

### The Four Most Copy-Relevant

**#1 — Admire characters for trying.** The customer case study where they immediately win is unconvincing. The case study where they tried, failed, almost gave up, and kept going — that one earns the testimonial. Show the trying.

**#4 — The Story Spine.** Memorize this. It's the single best template for a 4-sentence customer story:

> Once upon a time there was a **[customer archetype]** who **[did the old thing]**.
> Every day they **[suffered the consequence]**.
> One day **[they tried your product]**.
> Because of that, **[first-order change]**.
> Because of that, **[second-order change]**.
> Until finally **[new normal]**.

Example:

> Once upon a time there was a 14-person engineering team that deployed to production on Fridays. Every day they lost an hour to flaky tests, and every Friday somebody stayed late fixing what broke in prod. One day they switched to our CI. Because of that, the flaky tests surfaced as flaky instead of as failures. Because of that, the team trusted green builds again. Until finally, they shipped 3x more often and nobody worked Saturdays.

**#14 — Why must you tell THIS story?** If you can't answer this, don't tell the story. Most "brand storytelling" fails here. There's no conviction underneath, no belief the story feeds off. Conviction is the burn rate. Readers feel its absence instantly.

**#19 — Coincidences to get out are cheating.** This is the rule case studies violate constantly. "We were struggling, then we found [product], and now everything is amazing!" That's a coincidence-out. It feels false because it is false. The honest version: "We tried [product]. The first two weeks were rough — onboarding took longer than promised. Then in week three we caught a churn signal we'd have missed. That saved a $40k account. Now we trust it." The friction makes the resolution believable.

## Story Types in Copy

Seven types, when to use each, and a worked example for every one.

### 1. The Origin / Founder Story

**When to use:** About pages, brand pillar pages, first email of a welcome sequence, pitch decks. Earns trust by showing the human underneath.

**Structure:**

1. Ordinary life (relatable)
2. Triggering event (catalyst)
3. Struggle (the trying — Pixar #1)
4. Breakthrough (the epiphany)
5. Mission (why you do this now)

**Example (skincare brand):**

> I'm a chemist. Twelve years ago I had cystic acne so bad I'd cancel dinners with friends. I tried every prescription. One of them landed me in the ER with liver enzymes off the charts. I spent the next four years in a lab figuring out why every drugstore moisturizer made it worse. Turns out: 89% of "gentle" cleansers had pH above 7. Your skin's pH is 5.5. We launched in 2021 with one product — a cleanser that respects skin chemistry. Today it's six products. None of them will land you in the ER.

### 2. The Customer Transformation Story

**When to use:** Case studies, testimonials, mid-funnel emails, sales decks. Case study as narrative arc, never as feature list.

**Structure:**

1. Context — who they are, specifically
2. Problem — specific, quantified, painful
3. Approach — what you actually did
4. Outcome — specific, quantified, on a date
5. Testimonial in their voice

**Example (B2B agency case study):**

> **Context:** Mercer Logistics, a 40-person freight brokerage in Indianapolis. $14M ARR. Sales team of nine. Founded 2014.
>
> **Problem:** Their close rate had dropped from 22% to 11% over eighteen months. Reps blamed the market. Leadership blamed the reps. Nobody could see the pipeline clearly because deals lived in three places — HubSpot, a shared Google Sheet, and Robert's head.
>
> **Approach:** We spent the first week shadowing reps on calls. We rebuilt the pipeline in HubSpot around three stages instead of seven. We killed 11 of 14 fields nobody filled in. We set up one Slack channel: deals slipping past 30 days.
>
> **Outcome:** Within 90 days, close rate was back to 19%. Within 180, 24%. Forecast accuracy went from "off by 30%" to "off by 4%" for two consecutive quarters.
>
> **Testimonial:** _"I thought we needed more reps. We needed less pipeline noise. We added $2.1M in ARR with the team we already had." — Robert M., CEO_

Every number is specific. Every name is real. No "increased revenue significantly."

### 3. The Discovery Story

**When to use:** Info-products, courses, lead magnets, sales letters for problem-aware audiences. Powerful when the audience has tried the conventional solutions and failed.

**Structure:** "I was struggling with X… I tried the obvious things… they didn't work… then I discovered Y… and everything changed."

**Example (productivity course):**

> I tried every productivity system. Bullet journals. GTD. The pomodoro app with the little tomato. I had Notion templates that took longer to maintain than the work they were tracking. Then I read a research paper from a Cornell lab on "attention residue" — the cognitive cost of context-switching is 23 minutes per switch. I did the math on my own week: 31 context switches a day. That's 11.8 hours of pure residue. There wasn't a system that could fix that. There was only one move: redesign the day to switch four times instead of thirty-one. I tested it for six weeks. My output doubled. I now teach this in a 4-week course.

### 4. The Contrarian Story

**When to use:** When the market is saturated with the same advice and you need to stand out. When you have lived counter-evidence. Don't fake this — readers smell manufactured contrarianism instantly.

**Structure:** "Everyone says X. I tried X for [time]. It didn't work — here's exactly how. Then I tried the opposite. Here's what happened."

**Example (sales coach):**

> Every sales trainer tells you to "follow up 7-12 times." I did. I sent the breakup email. The "circling back." The Gary V "just one more touch." For two years. My response rate was 3%. So I tried the opposite — I send exactly one follow-up, four days after the first email, and then I disappear. Response rate now: 17%. Turns out the people you want to work with hate being chased. The ones who don't mind being chased aren't the ones you want.

### 5. The Cautionary Story

**When to use:** Educational content, top-of-funnel posts, opening of a sales letter. Pulls the reader in with "don't make my mistake."

**Structure:** "If I'd known X five years ago, I'd have avoided Y. Here's the story."

**Example (founder lessons post):**

> If I'd known one thing five years ago, I'd have my Saturdays back. We raised our seed round in 2019. I spent the next eighteen months hiring "for where we're going" instead of "for where we are." I hired a VP of Marketing when I needed a content writer. A Head of Sales when I needed a closer who'd run discovery. By month 14 I had a $190k/month burn and revenue of $34k. The senior people I'd hired weren't bad — they were wrong. They needed teams underneath them. I'd hired the apex of pyramids that didn't exist. Here's the four-question filter I now use before every hire.

### 6. The 1-2 Sentence Story (Eddie Shleyner)

**When to use:** Social media, ad copy, email openings, tweet hooks. Where attention is paid for and every word costs.

**Structure:** Setup. Tension. Question left unanswered. Often three sentences max.

**Example (Shleyner's own canonical version):**

> On the train, a man sat down across from me. He looked exactly like my father. I had not seen my father in twelve years.

Three sentences. Complete arc. Stakes. Question. You'd read whatever comes next. _Train this with daily practice_ — Shleyner writes one of these every morning before breakfast.

**Copy applications:**

> Last March, my biggest client emailed at 11pm to say they were leaving. I replied at 11:03pm with one question. They stayed — and tripled the contract.

> She'd been a designer for nineteen years. She quit the day she was asked to make the button bigger for the fourth time.

> Our worst client became our best testimonial. It took thirteen months and one uncomfortable conversation.

Each is three sentences. Each opens a loop. Each is the lead of a post or a landing page.

### 7. The "Day in the Life" Story

**When to use:** When you need to show the outcome instead of telling it. When abstract benefits aren't landing.

**Structure:** Walk the reader through a specific day in their post-product life. Concrete, sensory, hour-by-hour.

**Example (B2B sales SaaS):**

> It's 7:42 Monday morning. You open your laptop. Your forecast is already there — built overnight from this weekend's deal motion. Three deals moved up in confidence. One dropped. The one that dropped has a note: "champion left the company Friday." You write two emails before coffee. By the 9am pipeline meeting, you already know what you're going to say. The meeting ends 22 minutes early.

Show — don't tell — the outcome.

## The Epiphany Bridge

Russell Brunson's frame, evolved from Michael Hauge. The spine of every modern info-product page, every webinar, every long-form sales VSL.

### The Idea

You don't sell the prospect on your product. You sell them on the **belief change** that makes your product the obvious answer. You do that by telling them the story of the moment _you_ had that belief change — so they have the same epiphany themselves.

### The 8 Parts

1. **Backstory** — Who you were before, in their world.
2. **Desire** — What you wanted (and they want).
3. **The Wall** — What stopped you. The old way that doesn't work.
4. **The Epiphany** — The moment of realization. The new belief.
5. **The Plan** — What you decided to do because of the epiphany.
6. **The Conflict** — The struggle of executing the plan.
7. **The Achievement** — The concrete result.
8. **The Transformation** — Who you are now. Often: now you teach this.

### Worked Example: An Info-Product Sales Page

> **Backstory:** Three years ago I ran a marketing agency. Six employees, $740k/year revenue, profit margins under 8%. I worked 65-hour weeks. My wife had started calling herself a single mother.
>
> **Desire:** I wanted to break $2M without working more hours.
>
> **Wall:** I tried hiring. Every new hire added overhead before they added output. I tried raising prices. We lost two clients. I tried "going niche" — I picked dentists. I had no idea what dentists actually needed.
>
> **Epiphany:** I was on a flight back from a conference, reading a book about productized services. It hit me at 33,000 feet: I'd been selling time. Every client was custom. Every project rebuilt the wheel. The agency that scales doesn't sell time — it sells a _product_ that happens to be a service.
>
> **Plan:** I stopped taking new agency clients. I took our single most repeatable service — programmatic SEO setups — and turned it into a fixed-scope, fixed-price, fixed-timeline product. $14,500. Six weeks. Same deliverable every time.
>
> **Conflict:** The first eight months I doubted every week. Old clients churned. New clients didn't trust the model. My CFO told me I was insane.
>
> **Achievement:** Month 9 we hit our old monthly revenue with two employees instead of six. Month 18 we crossed $2M. Year 2 we sold to a holdco.
>
> **Transformation:** I don't run an agency anymore. I teach agency owners how to productize before they burn out. The framework is in the course below.

Every product page can use this skeleton. The opening doesn't say "here's our product." It says "here's how we got here." The reader bridges to the same belief on their own.

## Soap Opera Sequences

Andre Chaperon's framework, evolved by Russell Brunson. The architecture of email sequences that get opened day after day.

### The Mechanism

Each email ends on an **open loop** — an unresolved question, a cliffhanger, a "tomorrow I'll tell you what happened next." The dopamine-anticipation spike (Sapolsky again) keeps the reader looking for tomorrow's email. Open rates compound instead of decay.

### The Typical 5-Email Arc

| Email | Function                            | Open loop ending                                                  |
| ----- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Set the scene + drop into mid-story | "What happened next changed everything — I'll tell you tomorrow." |
| 2     | Backstory + first turning point     | "Then I found something I shouldn't have. More tomorrow."         |
| 3     | The wall / crisis                   | "I almost gave up. What saved it is in email 4."                  |
| 4     | The epiphany + early proof          | "There's one thing I haven't told you yet — the catch."           |
| 5     | Resolution + offer                  | (Pitch lands here, when trust is fully built.)                    |

### Sample Subject Lines (Fictional 5-Email Sequence)

A coaching offer to indie founders:

1. _Subject:_ The 11pm email that almost killed my business
2. _Subject:_ I read the email three times before I replied
3. _Subject:_ My co-founder thought I'd lost my mind
4. _Subject:_ The four words that fixed it
5. _Subject:_ Why I'm telling you this (and what to do)

Each subject line is a hook into the next chapter. No subject line says "10% off" or "Last chance." The story is the product until email five.

### Why It Works

The first email of a welcome sequence has a ~50% open rate. Email five usually has ~12%. A soap opera sequence can hold ~35% open rates through email five because the reader is mid-story. That's the difference between a 4% campaign and a 15% campaign on the same offer.

## Specificity as Truth-Signal

Hemingway's principle, journalism's house rule, Harry Dry's entire brand. The single most-impactful edit in copy.

### The Principle

**Concrete details ring true. Abstractions ring marketing.**

This isn't decoration. It's a credibility test the reader runs unconsciously. When you write "we help businesses grow," the reader's System 1 flags it: _this could be said by anyone, therefore it tells me nothing, therefore I don't believe it._ When you write "we helped Mercer Logistics in Indianapolis add $2.1M in ARR over nine months with the same nine reps," the same System 1 says: _this is too specific to be invented, therefore it's probably true._

Caples documented this in 1932 (_Tested Advertising Methods_): "Pulls were 19.5% greater for the ad that specified '$25,397.50 saved' than for the one that said 'thousands saved.'" One hundred years later it still holds.

### 12 Before / After Examples

| Vague (delete)                      | Specific (keep)                                            |
| ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Trusted by thousands of customers" | "Used by 3,847 teams including Notion, Linear, and Vercel" |
| "Increase your productivity"        | "Save 6.5 hours a week — back by the end of month one"     |
| "Industry-leading uptime"           | "99.97% uptime over the last 14 months. SOC2 Type II."     |
| "Productive routines"               | "Wake up at 6am to write"                                  |
| "She arrived late"                  | "She arrived at 3:47am with a single brown leather bag"    |
| "Significant growth"                | "From $14k MRR to $112k MRR in eleven months"              |
| "Many of our clients say…"          | "Robert M., CEO of Mercer Logistics, said:…"               |
| "Our team has deep expertise"       | "We've spent eight years inside 200+ sales orgs"           |
| "Saves you money"                   | "Replaces $4,200/yr of software with one $390/yr tool"     |
| "Improves close rates"              | "Closes 22% more deals on the same pipeline"               |
| "Fast onboarding"                   | "Live in your CRM in 4 minutes — no engineer required"     |
| "World-class support"               | "First reply in under 11 minutes, 24/7, by a human"        |

The vague version sounds like a brochure. The specific version sounds like a witness statement.

### The Rule

**Every abstract claim deserves at least one concrete proof.** If you write "we save you time," the very next sentence is a number. If you write "our clients love us," the very next sentence is a name and a quote. Specificity isn't a stylistic choice; it's how trust gets transmitted at scale.

## Show vs Tell, Scene vs Summary

Fiction techniques applied to copy. The single most important rhythm decision in narrative writing.

### Tell / Summary

- **Tell:** "He was nervous."
- **Summary:** "Over the next six months, the team grew from 4 to 14."

Used for: background context, time-passes moments, anything where speed matters more than texture.

### Show / Scene

- **Show:** "His hand shook as he reached for the doorknob."
- **Scene:** "It's Monday, 7:42am. You open the laptop. The forecast is already there."

Used for: high-emotion moments, the moment of transformation, testimonials, the first impression of using the product.

### When to Use Which in Copy

| Moment in copy                            | Use                                                           |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Founder's catalyzing event                | **Scene** (you want the reader inside it)                     |
| Years of grinding before the breakthrough | **Summary** (compress; nobody wants the daily diary)          |
| The epiphany itself                       | **Scene** (the single highest-impact moment)                  |
| Onboarding journey                        | **Summary** (1-2 sentences; nobody buys for the setup)        |
| First win                                 | **Scene** (this is the simulation chamber — let them feel it) |
| Years of compounding outcomes             | **Summary** (a table or a number line)                        |

### The 30-Second Scene Technique

For your highest-impact moment, slow time down. Write it like a film. Where are they? What's the light like? What do they see? What's the sound? You don't need all of this in the final draft — but writing it expanded forces the concrete details to surface. Then cut to the bones.

**Pre-edit (writer's draft):**

> She refreshed the dashboard at 8:42pm Friday from the kitchen counter. The lasagna was cooling. Her daughter was practicing piano in the next room — the same four bars over and over. The number was 312. She'd never closed 312 deals in a month. She'd never closed 200. She put the laptop down and sat down on the floor of the kitchen.

**Post-edit (in copy):**

> She refreshed the dashboard from her kitchen counter at 8:42pm Friday. The number was 312. She put the laptop down and sat down on the floor.

The unused details still inform the kept ones. That's why writers' drafts matter even when 80% gets cut.

## The Big Idea Inside the Story

Every story in copy carries a Big Idea (see [canon.md](./canon.md)). The narrative is the vehicle; the Big Idea is the cargo.

### The Through-Line Test

Ask: if you delete the story but keep one sentence, what's the sentence? That's your Big Idea. The story exists to make that sentence land.

- **Bad:** Story about a founder's journey with no clear belief underneath.
- **Good:** Story about a founder's journey where the belief is "agencies that sell time will be replaced by agencies that sell products."

The story can survive editing — paragraphs come and go — but the Big Idea is the spine. If the spine breaks, the story collapses into anecdote.

### Mark Ford's Four Tests for a Big Idea

When evaluating whether the story is carrying a real Big Idea or just being entertaining, run Ford's four tests (covered in detail in [canon.md](./canon.md)):

1. Is it new (or freshly framed)?
2. Does it speak to a deep, existing desire?
3. Does it provoke immediate, specific belief?
4. Does it suggest action?

If the story passes all four, ship it. If it only entertains, it's an anecdote, not a story.

## Narrative Failure Modes

The six ways stories in copy go wrong. Most published "brand storytelling" commits at least three of these.

### 1. Story for Story's Sake

The story doesn't earn the sale. It's interesting but doesn't move the reader toward the action.

> _Symptom:_ Reader finishes the story and thinks "huh, neat" instead of "I need this."
> _Fix:_ Pixar #14 — why must you tell THIS story? Cut any story that doesn't carry the Big Idea.

### 2. Hero Confusion

The brand is the hero instead of the customer.

> _Symptom:_ The page is about the company's journey, awards, history. The customer doesn't appear until paragraph eight.
> _Fix:_ StoryBrand. Yoda, not Luke. Rewrite with the customer as the protagonist of every sentence.

### 3. Saccharine / Manufactured Emotion

"We believe in helping people…" "Our mission is to make the world a better place through…"

> _Symptom:_ The reader feels nothing because the writer wasn't moved either.
> _Fix:_ Concrete + earned. Show one specific person who was helped, in one specific way, on one specific date. Drop the manifesto.

### 4. Specificity Vacuum

The story is full of "she" and "him" and "a client" with no names, no places, no numbers.

> _Symptom:_ It reads like a parable. Could be about anyone. Therefore: probably about no one.
> _Fix:_ Specificity audit. Replace every pronoun-only character with a name. Replace every "many" with a number. Replace every "recently" with a date.

### 5. Tense Inconsistency

Bouncing between past tense and present tense without intent.

> _Symptom:_ "She walked into the office. She sees the email." Reader's eyes glaze.
> _Fix:_ Pick a tense. Past for retrospective narration. Present for in-scene immediacy. Don't mix without intent.

### 6. Pacing Failure

Too slow to open, too fast to resolve.

> _Symptom:_ Three paragraphs of setup before anything happens. Then the resolution happens in one sentence.
> _Fix:_ Start in the middle of the action (Pixar #4 maps "One day" to your second sentence, not your eighth). Spend the most time on the moment of transformation, not the build-up.

## Storytelling in 2026

The AI-era angle. Why story is more valuable now than it has ever been.

### The AI Sameness Problem

When everyone has access to the same generation models, copy converges to a mean. "Transform your business with our innovative solution" — that sentence is generated 8,000 times an hour somewhere on the internet right now. The cost of producing generic copy has dropped to zero. The reader's tolerance for it has dropped to zero too.

### Why AI-Generated Stories Ring Fake

LLMs are trained on a million stories. They produce a story that is structurally correct and emotionally hollow. Eddie Shleyner: _"AI can't feel the gravity of a profound moment. It can describe one. It can't feel one."_

The tells:

- **Statistical-mean specificity.** The AI picks the "typical" detail (a coffee shop, a Tuesday morning) instead of the lived detail (a Tim Hortons in Mississauga, 6:47am).
- **Resolved tension.** The AI defaults to closure. Real stories have residue.
- **Universal voice.** The AI smoothes voice to the average. Real voice has friction.
- **No contrarian POV.** The AI hedges. Real founders have positions other founders disagree with.

### The Human Premium

What humans can do that models can't (yet):

1. **Lived specificity.** The detail nobody would invent because nobody else was there.
2. **Contrarian POV with skin in the game.** A take you'd defend in public because you've lived through its opposite.
3. **The unique mechanism.** Not "we help you grow your business" but "we found that 89% of churn happens between days 4 and 11, so we redesigned the entire trial around days 4-11."
4. **The off-ramp moment.** The detail that doesn't fit, that wouldn't be in the marketing-ready version. Including it signals truth.

### Unique-Mechanism Story (vs Generic Transformation Story)

**Generic transformation story (AI-shaped):**

> Sarah was struggling to grow her business. She tried many things but nothing worked. Then she found our platform. Now her business is thriving.

That sentence was generated four times while you read it. It's worthless.

**Unique-mechanism story (human-shaped):**

> Sarah ran a $40k/month bookkeeping firm in Halifax. She'd hired three times and lost margin every time. The fourth hire — a virtual assistant in Manila — finally worked. Not because of where the VA was. Because of the _handoff document_. Sarah had spent two months building a 47-page SOP that any human could execute. The VA wasn't replacing Sarah. The SOP was. We help bookkeepers build that SOP in eleven days. Sarah's firm cleared $112k in March. The 47 pages are why.

Same outcome. Specific people, specific places, specific mechanism, specific numbers. The mechanism — the SOP, not the hire — is what no model could have invented, because it came from sitting with Sarah for an afternoon and watching her work.

In 2026, the question isn't _can you tell a story?_ It's _can you tell a story only you could tell?_

That's the bar. The work earns the right to be called elite when no model could have written it — because no model was in the room.
