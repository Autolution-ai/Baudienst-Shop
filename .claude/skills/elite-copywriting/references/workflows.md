## The Canonical 8-Phase Copy-Development Workflow

The default end-to-end process. Each phase is a **gate** — do not proceed until the previous phase has produced its named output. Treat the phases as cyclic for ongoing programs (testing in Phase 8 feeds back into Phase 1 / Phase 2 on the next iteration).

| Phase | Name                   | Output                                         |
| ----- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| 0     | Brief Discovery        | One-page project brief                         |
| 1     | Positioning            | One-page positioning doc                       |
| 2     | Audience Synthesis     | Persona doc + Voice Constraint Card + JTBD doc |
| 3     | Objection Prediction   | Objection Map                                  |
| 4     | Big Idea Hunt          | One-sentence Big Idea                          |
| 5     | Outline + Framework    | Outline with framework mapped                  |
| 6     | Draft                  | Complete first draft                           |
| 7     | Edit Pass              | Polished draft                                 |
| 8     | Objection Audit + Test | Published copy + test plan                     |

---

### Phase 0 — Brief Discovery (Pre-Project)

Run this **before** any phase begins. If you don't have these inputs you do not yet have a project — you have a request.

**Inputs to collect:**

- [ ] **Project** — what specifically are we writing? (a single artifact, named: "the homepage hero," "the welcome email," "the cold outreach template")
- [ ] **Format / channel** — landing page / VSL / cold email / abandoned-cart / LinkedIn post / proposal / ad / etc.
- [ ] **Audience sketch** — initial 1–2 sentences. Will be refined in Phase 2. Don't skip this even if rough.
- [ ] **Deadline + success metric** — what date does this need to ship, and how will we know it worked? (sign-ups / replies / sales / open-rate / specific number)
- [ ] **Research budget** — time and money for interviews, Wynter tests, customer panels. Often zero. That's fine; just know it.
- [ ] **AI use policy** — is the channel one where AI-detectable copy will hurt (Daniel Throssell-style audiences, founder voice, premium B2B), or one where AI assistance is fine for connective tissue?
- [ ] **Constraints** — word counts, brand guidelines, legal/compliance, mandatory claims, forbidden claims

**Output:** a single-page project brief containing the above. Use the [Creative Brief Template](#creative-brief-template-paste-and-fill) below.

### Phase 1 — Positioning

You cannot write words until you know what mental category you're claiming, what alternative you're displacing, and what stage of awareness the reader is at. **Skipping this is the most common cause of "I have good copy but it isn't converting."**

**Steps:**

1. **Run Dunford 5+1** (cross-reference: [positioning.md](positioning.md))
   - Competitive alternatives — what does the reader currently use / consider instead of us?
   - Unique attributes — what do we have that those alternatives don't?
   - Value (so what?) — what does that uniqueness enable the reader to do or feel?
   - Best-fit customer — who cares most about that value?
   - Market category — the preferred mental shelf we want to live on
   - +1 (trends) — relevant market shifts that make us inevitable now

2. **Diagnose Schwartz awareness stage (1–5)** for the typical reader (cross-reference: [awareness-sophistication.md](awareness-sophistication.md))
   - Stage 1 Unaware → no problem awareness
   - Stage 2 Problem-Aware → feels pain, doesn't know solutions exist
   - Stage 3 Solution-Aware → knows solutions exist, doesn't know us
   - Stage 4 Product-Aware → knows us, evaluating
   - Stage 5 Most-Aware → ready to buy, needs the offer

3. **Diagnose market sophistication stage (1–5)** — how many times has the market heard this kind of claim before? Each level requires a more sophisticated hook:
   - L1 First to market → state the claim directly
   - L2 Competitors emerging → bigger claim
   - L3 Claims tiring → new mechanism
   - L4 Mechanism tiring → elaborated mechanism / contrarian angle
   - L5 Skeptical / fatigued → identification, story, or new market entirely

4. **(B2B) Draft strategic narrative** — Andy Raskin's 5-step:
   - Name the **shift** in the world
   - Show the **stakes** (winners and losers)
   - Paint the **Promised Land**
   - Identify the **obstacles** preventing the buyer from reaching it
   - Position the product as the **bridge**

5. **Name the preferred category** — one sentence: "We are the [category] for [best-fit customer] who want [value]."

**Output:** a single-page positioning doc with Dunford 5+1 filled in, awareness stage diagnosed, sophistication stage diagnosed, (optional) strategic narrative drafted, and the preferred category named.

### Phase 2 — Audience Synthesis

Words have to fit the exact phrases the reader uses in their own head. If you're guessing those phrases, your copy is decorative.

**Steps:**

1. **Mom Test interviews** (cross-reference: [audience-research.md](audience-research.md))
   - Target 5–10 customer interviews ideally; 3 if budget is tight; 0 is unacceptable unless the founder _is_ the customer
   - Ask about life, not opinions of the product
   - Capture exact phrases verbatim — this is the single most valuable raw material in the whole workflow

2. **VoC mining** — gather 50+ data points from:
   - 3-star reviews (the most honest)
   - Support tickets (especially first 14 days post-purchase)
   - Sales-call transcripts (especially "what was your hesitation?")
   - Reddit threads in the customer's vertical
   - G2 / Capterra / TrustRadius / Amazon reviews
   - Exit surveys and cancellation forms
   - Win/loss interview transcripts

3. **Identify primary persona** — role × life-stage × awareness stage × sophistication level (cross-reference: [decision-makers.md](decision-makers.md))
   - Name them mentally; describe a typical Tuesday for them
   - Identify the moment they go searching for a solution like ours

4. **Build JTBD doc** (cross-reference: [audience-research.md](audience-research.md))
   - Functional job — what task are they trying to complete?
   - Emotional job — how do they want to feel?
   - Social job — how do they want to be perceived?
   - 4 Forces (Christensen) — push of current situation, pull of new solution, anxiety of switching, habit of status quo

5. **Build Voice Constraint Card** (cross-reference: [voice-tone.md](voice-tone.md))
   - NN/g 4 dimensions — formal/casual, serious/funny, respectful/irreverent, matter-of-fact/enthusiastic
   - 10 always-use words
   - 10 never-use words
   - 5 favored rhetorical moves
   - 5 avoided moves
   - One rhythm rule (e.g., "Sentences vary 4 to 20 words; never two long sentences in a row")

**Output:** Persona doc + Voice Constraint Card + JTBD doc.

### Phase 3 — Objection Prediction

The most-skipped phase, and the one that most distinguishes copy that converts from copy that earns "let me think about it." Cross-reference: [objection-prediction.md](objection-prediction.md).

**Steps:**

1. **Run the 6-layer objection-prediction stack:**
   - **Layer 1 — Universal 8:** no time / no money / no need / no trust / no urgency / wrong priority / wrong fit / no authority
   - **Layer 2 — Role-specific:** distinct objections per role (CEO / CFO / CTO / champion / end user — see [decision-makers.md](decision-makers.md))
   - **Layer 3 — Life-stage / demographic:** women in their 20s vs 40s have different objection clusters; founders vs employees diverge sharply
   - **Layer 4 — Industry-specific:** SaaS / eCom / agency / courses / local — each has stereotyped objections
   - **Layer 5 — Awareness-stage-specific:** Stage 2 objections differ entirely from Stage 4
   - **Layer 6 — Embarrassing objections:** ego, status, distrust, "what if I look stupid" — VoC rarely surfaces these because people don't say them aloud

2. **Generate 20 objections via the AI ladder prompt** (cross-reference: prompts in [ai-workflow.md](ai-workflow.md))
   - Force ranking by likelihood
   - Force inclusion of price, ego, distrust, status-risk objections

3. **For each predicted objection, define four things:**
   - Surface objection (what they'd actually say or think)
   - Underlying fear (what they actually feel)
   - Proof or reframe that addresses it
   - **Exact page element** that addresses it (hero subline / FAQ entry / case-study card / risk-reversal clause / pricing-page footnote / testimonial placement)

4. **Score the 4 Buyer Concerns provisionally (0–10 each):**
   - Will it actually work?
   - Will it work for ME specifically?
   - How fast will I see results?
   - How risky is this purchase?
   - Any score below 7 = "objection lurking unresolved"

5. **Define risk reversal architecture** (cross-reference: [pricing-offer.md](pricing-offer.md))
   - The explicit promise that absorbs perceived risk (money-back, results guarantee, free trial, pilot scope)
   - Where on the page it appears (above the CTA, in the FAQ, in the offer block)

**Output:** Objection Map — a table where each row is one predicted objection mapped to one specific page element.

---

### Phase 4 — Big Idea Hunt

The Big Idea is the one sentence that, if it lands, makes the rest of the copy almost incidental. Most copy fails because there is no Big Idea — only well-arranged features.

**Steps:**

1. **Apply the Schwartz reading discipline** — read **3× more about the market than you'll write** (cross-reference: [Schwartz "Read Before Writing" Discipline](#schwartz-read-before-writing-discipline) below)

2. **Hunt for the contradiction** — what is true that the prospect doesn't realize, or believes that isn't true?

3. **Hunt for the surprising mechanism** — why does this work, specifically? What's the named reason the alternative fails?

4. **Draft the Big Idea as one sentence.** If you cannot get it to one sentence, you don't have it yet. Keep digging.

5. **Test against Ford's 4** (cross-reference: [canon.md](canon.md)):
   - Instantly comprehensible — a 12-year-old gets it
   - Exciting — it makes the right reader lean in
   - Believable — proof is available somewhere
   - Leads to the inevitable conclusion — the reader can already see why buying makes sense

If it fails any of the four, iterate.

**Output:** A single-sentence Big Idea that passes Ford's 4 tests.

---

### Phase 5 — Outline + Framework Selection

Structure precedes prose. If you start drafting paragraphs before you have a skeleton, you'll rewrite three times.

**Steps:**

1. **Select framework based on awareness × sophistication** (cross-reference: [frameworks.md](frameworks.md), [awareness-sophistication.md](awareness-sophistication.md)):
   - Stage 1 Unaware → Story lead / news lead / identity hook → AIDA or PAS variant
   - Stage 2 Problem-Aware → PAS / PASTOR with heavy agitation
   - Stage 3 Solution-Aware → Mechanism reveal / category differentiation → 4Ps or FAB
   - Stage 4 Product-Aware → Offer + Proof + Risk Reversal → AICPBSAWN or SB7
   - Stage 5 Most-Aware → Direct offer / restock / first-name → minimal framework

2. **Generate 40+ headline candidates** using the templates library (cross-reference: examples in [canon.md](canon.md))
   - Force variety: news headlines, story headlines, question headlines, command headlines, "how to" headlines, secret-reveal headlines, contrarian headlines, identity headlines
   - Most will be bad. That's the point. The 41st is often the one.

3. **Generate 20+ hook candidates** for the first sentence (Sugarman's slippery slide — the first sentence's only job is to make you read the second)
   - Mix: question, story opener, statistic, quote, contrarian claim, scene-setter

4. **Wireframe / structural outline** before any prose:
   - Section labels (Hero → Problem → Mechanism → Proof → Offer → Risk Reversal → FAQ → Final CTA)
   - One-sentence intent for each section
   - Each predicted objection from Phase 3 mapped to the section that addresses it

**Output:** outline with framework selected, headline + hook candidates generated, and structural skeleton with intent-per-section.

---

### Phase 6 — Draft

**Now** you write. Not before.

**Steps:**

1. **Write to one person** — name them mentally. The specific reader you're addressing. Use their job, their first name, what they wore on their last Zoom call. Vagueness in audience produces vagueness in voice.

2. **Write fast.** Bad words on the page beat blank page. You will edit ruthlessly in Phase 7 — your job here is to externalize, not to polish.

3. **Use AI for connective tissue** (cross-reference: [ai-workflow.md](ai-workflow.md))
   - FAQ filler text
   - "What's included" / feature explanations
   - Transitional sentences between sections
   - Variations on a sentence you already wrote

4. **Human-only — never delegate to AI:**
   - Hooks (the first sentence)
   - The Big Idea statement
   - Contrarian POV / opinion paragraphs
   - Proof statements (you must verify these are true)
   - Voice signature moves
   - The close (the last paragraph before the CTA)

5. **Layer in stack components** in the order they appear on the page:
   - Hero (Big Idea + promise + immediate proof)
   - Problem agitation (only if Stage 2 awareness)
   - Mechanism (why this works)
   - Testimonials and social proof
   - Offer (what they get)
   - Risk reversal (guarantee / pilot)
   - FAQ (objection pre-emption from Phase 3)
   - CTA (single primary; clear, specific)

**Output:** complete first draft. Imperfect on purpose.

---

### Phase 7 — Edit Pass

This is where good copy becomes great copy. **Expect to cut 30–50% of the words.**

**Steps:**

1. **Read aloud** — every rhythm break, every place you'd stumble, every place the breath runs out. Mark them. Fix them.

2. **Cut 30–50% on the second pass.** Every word earns its place. Default to deletion.

3. **Specificity pass:**
   - Replace every generic adjective with a number ("fast" → "in 6 seconds")
   - Replace every "many" / "some" / "a lot" with the exact count
   - Replace every abstraction with a sensory specific ("scalable" → "ran 4M requests/day on a $50/mo box")

4. **"You" vs "We" count** — "you" should win 3:1 or better. If "we" is winning, the copy is about you, not them.

5. **Anti-slop pass** (cross-reference: [ai-workflow.md](ai-workflow.md))
   - Em-dash overuse (AI tell)
   - "delve," "tapestry," "leverage," "crucial," "pivotal," "vibrant," "nestled," "boasts," "in today's [adjective] landscape"
   - Three-item-list reflex ("fast, simple, and intuitive")
   - "It's not X — it's Y" reflex
   - Default passive voice — convert to active

6. **Add one truly contrarian point** that AI couldn't have written — a take that risks disagreement.

7. **Add one made-from-life specific detail** — the kind of detail that signals "a human wrote this": a named customer, a specific time of day, a real number, an admitted flaw.

8. **Headline test** — does the final headline pass the 4 Us (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific)?

9. **First-sentence test** — does it pass Sugarman's slippery slide (does it compel reading the next one)?

**Output:** polished draft ready for objection audit.

---

### Phase 8 — Objection Audit + Test

Last gate before publish. Then publish and learn.

**Steps:**

1. **Run the Pre-Publish Objection Audit Checklist** (cross-reference: [objection-prediction.md](objection-prediction.md)):
   - [ ] All 8 universal objections addressed somewhere?
   - [ ] Role-specific objections for the primary persona addressed?
   - [ ] Awareness-stage-specific objections addressed?
   - [ ] Top 3 embarrassing objections pre-empted?
   - [ ] Each VoC-discovered objection mapped to a page element?
   - [ ] 4 Buyer Concerns each score ≥ 7?
   - [ ] Risk reversal explicit and visible above the CTA?
   - [ ] FAQ contains the toughest objection, not the convenient one?

2. **Score the 4 Buyer Concerns final** (target ≥ 7 each). Below 6 = killing conversion. Fix before publishing.

3. **Validate** via one of:
   - Wynter message test (B2B)
   - Peer review by a fellow copywriter
   - Customer panel of 5
   - Small-cell A/B test before full rollout
   - Founder gut-check pass (low-rigor, low-cost)

4. **Define the test hypothesis** in the form: **"If we change X, we expect Y, because Z."**

5. **Ship.** Measure against the success metric defined in Phase 0.

6. **After 30 / 60 / 90 days:** diagnose, refine, iterate. Feed learnings back into Phase 1 (positioning) and Phase 2 (audience) for the next cycle.

**Output:** published copy + written test plan + measurement schedule.

## Stefan Georgi RMBC Workflow (for Direct Response)

A specialization of the 8-phase workflow for long-form direct response (VSLs, sales letters, advertorials). Cross-reference: [canon.md](canon.md).

| Step          | Maps to 8-Phase | What you do                                                                                                   |
| ------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **R**esearch  | Phase 0 + 1 + 2 | Exhaustive market + product + audience + competitor research. Read everything. Mine VoC. Interview customers. |
| **M**echanism | Phase 4         | Identify the unique reason your thing works, or works differently. The named mechanism is the Big Idea.       |
| **B**rief     | Phase 5         | Bullet-by-bullet outline. Every section, every claim, every proof. No prose yet.                              |
| **C**opy      | Phase 6 + 7     | Write fast from the brief; edit slow.                                                                         |

**When to use RMBC over generic 8-phase:**

- Long-form copy (VSL, sales letter, advertorial)
- Direct-response economics (paid traffic, immediate purchase)
- Markets at Schwartz sophistication L3+ where mechanism matters more than benefit list

**Key principle:** Research time often exceeds writing time 4:1. Most failed direct response is under-researched, not under-written.

## Joanna Wiebe Conversion Copywriting Workflow

A specialization for SaaS / digital products where A/B testing infrastructure exists.

| Step             | Maps to 8-Phase | What you do                                                                               |
| ---------------- | --------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **VoC research** | Phase 2         | Review mining, transcript synthesis, customer interviews. Capture exact phrases verbatim. |
| **Wireframe**    | Phase 5         | Structure before words. Sections, hierarchy, scroll depth, F-pattern.                     |
| **Write copy**   | Phase 6         | Pour copy into the wireframe. VoC quotes appear as section headlines wherever possible.   |
| **Diagnose**     | Phase 7 + 8     | The "and so what?" test on every line. Clarity, scannability, hierarchy check.            |
| **Test**         | Phase 8         | A/B, multivariate, Wynter message test. Hypothesis-driven.                                |

**When to use Wiebe over generic 8-phase:**

- You have measurable traffic and conversion infrastructure
- You're optimizing an existing page, not launching one
- The product is SaaS / digital / subscription
- The audience is sophisticated and skeptical (the diagnosis-and-test loop is more valuable than the Big Idea swing)

**Key principle:** Customer-Voice copy outperforms creative-genius copy. The best lines on the page already exist — in your customers' reviews.

## Schwartz "Read Before Writing" Discipline

Eugene Schwartz reportedly read for **a full week** before writing a sales letter. The discipline applies to every project, scaled to its size.

**The rule:** Read 3× more about the market than you'll write.

**The practice:**

1. **Read widely in the market.** Forums, subreddits, customer service tickets, support transcripts, competitor pages, review sites, industry publications, related books.

2. **Hunt for surprising facts.** The detail that surprises you will surprise the reader. The contrarian claim. The counterintuitive cause. The number that doesn't match the conventional wisdom.

3. **Mark every quote-able sentence.** Verbatim. From customers, from articles, from research papers, from competitor copy you wish you'd written. These become headlines, subheads, FAQ questions.

4. **Marinate.** Let the material sit for a day. Take a walk. Sleep on it. The Big Idea often arrives during the gap, not during reading and not during writing.

5. **Write fast in one sitting.** After full marination, the draft should pour out. If it doesn't, you haven't marinated enough — read more.

**Failure mode:** Treating research as a chore to get through, not as the source of the Big Idea. If your research feels efficient, you're doing it wrong.

## Halbert Priority Order (List > Offer > Copy)

Gary Halbert's most famous teaching: if you could only fix one thing, fix the **list** (the market). The words are last.

**Order of priority:**

1. **Get a great LIST (market)** — the right people. No copy fixes a wrong audience. A mediocre letter to a starving market beats brilliance to indifference.
2. **Get a great OFFER** — the right deal. Risk reversal, pricing structure, bonuses, payment terms. The offer is 80% of conversion when the list is right.
3. **Then worry about the COPY** — the words. Important, but multiplicative on top of (1) and (2).

**How to use this in the 8-phase workflow:**

- Phase 1 (Positioning) → defines who the list is and where to find them
- Phase 3 (Objection Prediction) + risk reversal → defines the offer architecture
- Phase 6 (Draft) → finally, the copy

If conversion is poor and the writer's instinct is "improve the words," check the list and offer first.

## Short-Project Workflow Collapse

For ads, single emails, LinkedIn posts, tweet threads, microcopy — running the full 8 phases is overkill. Use the **15-minute collapsed workflow**:

| Step | Action                                                                | Time  |
| ---- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----- |
| 1    | Positioning check — read the existing positioning doc (don't redo it) | 2 min |
| 2    | Awareness diagnosis — what stage is this reader at?                   | 1 min |
| 3    | Framework selection — pick from [frameworks.md](frameworks.md)        | 1 min |
| 4    | Draft — write fast                                                    | 6 min |
| 5    | Edit pass — read aloud, cut 30%, specificity pass, anti-slop scan     | 5 min |

**When to use the full 8-phase vs the collapsed workflow:**

| Project type                           | Workflow                                             |
| -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Landing page / hero / sales page / VSL | Full 8-phase                                         |
| Email sequence (5+ emails)             | Full 8-phase (sequence-level), collapsed (per email) |
| Single transactional email             | Collapsed                                            |
| Cold outreach template                 | Collapsed, with deeper Phase 2                       |
| LinkedIn post / tweet thread           | Collapsed                                            |
| Ad (Meta / Google / display)           | Collapsed, but with full Phase 3 (objections)        |
| Microcopy / UI strings                 | Collapsed                                            |
| Proposal / pitch deck                  | Full 8-phase                                         |
| Brand tagline / category-defining line | Full 8-phase + extended Phase 4                      |

**Minimum viable workflow** for anything: positioning check → awareness diagnosis → framework selection → draft → edit. Even when in a hurry, do not skip any of these five.

## Per-Channel Workflow Variations

Cross-reference: [channels.md](channels.md).

### Landing Page Workflow

Run the **full 8-phase workflow**. Specific variations:

- Phase 5: Wireframe sections explicitly before prose. Section labels first, intent second, copy third.
- Phase 6: Draft hero last, not first. Internal sections inform what claim the hero should make.
- Phase 7: Read the page aloud start-to-finish. Time it. If a section drags, cut it.
- Phase 8: Score 4 Buyer Concerns scroll-by-scroll, not page-overall. Above-the-fold must satisfy all 4 to some degree.

### Email Sequence Workflow

Two passes:

**Sequence-level (full 8-phase):**

- Phase 0–4: usual
- Phase 5: define the **concept arc** — the through-line across all emails. What does Email 1 set up that Email 5 pays off?
- Phase 6: outline each email's role in the arc (hook / problem / story / proof / offer / urgency / close)

**Per-email (collapsed workflow):**

- Each email gets: framework selection → draft → edit
- Subject line: generate 10+ candidates per email
- Preview text: written intentionally, never auto-generated

**Sequence-level QA:**

- Read all emails back-to-back
- Each email must have its own value if it's the only one opened
- Together they must build pressure / value across the sequence

### VSL (Video Sales Letter) Workflow

Specialized 8-phase variation:

- Phase 5: outline with **time markers** (0:00–0:30 hook, 0:30–2:00 problem, etc.)
- Phase 6: **script aloud** — write to be spoken, not read. Test by speaking each sentence.
- Phase 6 add-ons: **visual-pacing pass** — what is on screen during each beat? **Proof-stack pass** — where do testimonials, charts, screenshots appear?
- Phase 7: read aloud the entire script. Time it. Most VSLs run long; expect to cut 25%.
- Phase 8: pilot with 100–500 paid views before scaling spend.

### Cold Outreach Workflow

The shortest workflow, but with the deepest Phase 2:

1. **Research the individual** — LinkedIn, recent posts, company news, mutual connections
2. **Identify a trigger event** — funding, hire, product launch, podcast appearance, recent tweet
3. **Single question** — one specific question that proves you did the research and creates a low-stakes reply
4. **Single CTA** — "Worth a 15-min call?" or "Want me to send a teardown?" — never both

Voice rule: write as if writing to one person, because you are. Never use templated openers ("I hope this email finds you well").

### LinkedIn Post Workflow

| Component                       | What it does                 | Examples                                          |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Hook** (line 1)               | Earn the click on "see more" | Contrarian claim / specific number / story opener |
| **Story / context** (lines 2–6) | Earn the read                | Specific, sensory, personal                       |
| **Insight** (lines 7–9)         | Earn the save / repost       | The named lesson                                  |
| **Soft CTA** (final line)       | Earn engagement              | Question / "DM me if X" / "agree?"                |

Run collapsed workflow. Generate 5 hooks before drafting body.

### Twitter Thread Workflow

| Component         | Tweet position                                                       |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hook tweet        | T1 — must promise specific payoff                                    |
| Setup             | T2–T3 — establish stakes                                             |
| Structure markers | T4 onward — "Here's how:" / numbered list / "First / Second / Third" |
| Payoff            | T-2 — the named insight or specific result                           |
| CTA               | T-1 — bookmark / RT / "follow for more X"                            |

Threads succeed or fail on T1. Spend 50% of total time on the hook tweet alone.

## Team Collaboration Workflow

When copywriter + designer + founder are all involved:

### 1. Brief alignment meeting (before Phase 1)

Attendees: copywriter, designer, founder (and PM if applicable).

Agenda:

- Read the Phase 0 brief aloud together
- Resolve any conflict on objective / audience / success metric
- Confirm who owns each phase output
- Confirm deadlines for each phase output

### 2. Strategic narrative review (after Phase 1)

Founder reviews positioning doc. Specifically:

- Does the Dunford 5+1 match how founder thinks about the company?
- Does the awareness diagnosis match founder's intuition?
- Are there proof points / claims the founder knows about that copywriter doesn't?

This is the single best time to surface "we never told you about X" — capture them.

### 3. Draft review with structured feedback (after Phase 6 and Phase 7)

**Replace "I like / I don't like" with structured feedback:**

- ❌ "I like this line" → ✅ "This line works because it pre-empts the [specific objection] objection"
- ❌ "I don't like this paragraph" → ✅ "This paragraph creates a [specific] objection that we don't pre-empt; can we either delete it or add a proof statement?"
- ❌ "Make it stronger" → ✅ "The 4 Buyer Concerns score for 'will it work for me' is 4/10; here are 3 fit-specific data points we could add"

Every comment must be: **(a) a specific objection it triggers**, **(b) a specific suggestion**, or **(c) a question that surfaces missing info**.

### 4. Final QA pass (before Phase 8)

Run the Pre-Publish Objection Audit Checklist (from [objection-prediction.md](objection-prediction.md)) as a group:

- Copywriter reads each objection aloud
- Designer confirms it's addressed visibly on the page
- Founder confirms the proof is true
- All three sign off before publishing

## AI-Augmented Workflow Variations

Cross-reference: [ai-workflow.md](ai-workflow.md).

The core principle: **AI is an edit-pass tool, not a draft-pass tool, for the parts that matter.** Use AI to multiply human judgment, not to replace it.

### When to use AI in each phase

| Phase           | AI use                                                                                            | Why                                                  |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| 0 — Brief       | Yes — summarize source docs                                                                       | No voice risk                                        |
| 1 — Positioning | Yes — Dunford 5+1 brainstorming, competitive analysis                                             | Output is structured, not prose                      |
| 2 — Audience    | **Heavy** — VoC mining, review synthesis, transcript clustering                                   | This is AI's superpower; manual mining is 10× slower |
| 3 — Objections  | **Heavy** — generate 20 objections via ladder prompt                                              | AI is excellent at predicting objections             |
| 4 — Big Idea    | **No.** AI cannot generate Big Ideas. It can stress-test one you've drafted.                      | Big Ideas come from humans noticing contradictions.  |
| 5 — Outline     | Yes — outline scaffolding, framework selection, headline variations                               | Generate many candidates fast; human picks           |
| 6 — Draft       | **Selective.** AI for connective tissue; humans for hooks, Big Idea, contrarian POV, proof, close | The bones must be human; the muscles can be assisted |
| 7 — Edit        | Yes — anti-slop scan, specificity check, "you/we" count, read-aloud rhythm flagging               | AI is good at finding AI tells                       |
| 8 — Test        | Yes — generate test hypotheses, predicted outcomes                                                | Structured output again                              |

### When NOT to use AI (hard rules)

- **Never** for the Big Idea (Phase 4)
- **Never** for the hook / first sentence (Phase 6)
- **Never** for the close (Phase 6)
- **Never** for proof statements (Phase 6) — AI will fabricate; humans must verify
- **Never** for voice signature moves (Phase 6) — these are the human fingerprints
- **Never** for contrarian POV (Phase 6) — AI is trained toward consensus
- **Never** for any audience where AI-detectable copy will hurt trust (founder voice, premium B2B, anti-AI tribes like Daniel Throssell's lists)

### The "edit pass not draft pass" principle

When in doubt: **draft like a human, edit like an AI.** Have AI scan your draft for slop, specificity, rhythm, "you/we" balance — but write the bones yourself.

The reverse — have AI draft, then "edit it to sound human" — almost always fails. The slop is in the structure, not the words. You can't sand it off.

## Workflow Failure Modes

The most common ways the workflow goes wrong. Reading this list periodically is the single most-useful self-audit.

### 1. Skipping Phase 1 (Positioning)

**Symptom:** Beautiful copy that doesn't convert. "Good writing, wrong audience."

**Cause:** Writer assumes the positioning is obvious or already done. It rarely is.

**Fix:** Before writing the next paragraph, run Dunford 5+1 and diagnose Schwartz awareness/sophistication. This takes 30 minutes and saves weeks.

### 2. Skipping Phase 2 (Audience Research)

**Symptom:** Generic copy. Every sentence could be on any competitor's site. No verbatim phrases. No specific personas.

**Cause:** "I know the customer" / "no time" / "we can't afford interviews."

**Fix:** You can always mine 30 reviews in an hour. There is always time for that. There is never a project where you can't.

### 3. Skipping Phase 3 (Objection Prediction) — THE MOST-SKIPPED STEP

**Symptom:** "Let me think about it." Demos that don't close. Carts that abandon. Trials that don't convert.

**Cause:** Writer assumes the FAQ is enough, or assumes objections will be handled in sales follow-up.

**Fix:** Run the 6-layer stack every time. Generate 20 objections. Map each to a page element. This is the most leveraged 2 hours in the whole workflow.

### 4. Going Straight to Phase 6 (Drafting)

**Symptom:** Writer has many drafts, none feels right. Rewrites are getting smaller but no closer.

**Cause:** Missing Phase 4 (Big Idea) and Phase 5 (outline). Writer is searching for the idea inside paragraphs instead of in the marinate-and-think phase.

**Fix:** Stop drafting. Go back to Phase 4. Read more. Find the contradiction. The draft will write itself when you have the Big Idea.

### 5. No Phase 8 (No Testing, No Iteration)

**Symptom:** Copy ships, never gets revisited, slowly decays as the market shifts.

**Cause:** Phase 8 feels optional once Phase 7 is "done." It is not optional.

**Fix:** Phase 0 includes the success metric for a reason. If you can't measure it, you can't iterate it. Define the metric, schedule the review, calendar the next cycle.

### 6. Treating the 8 Phases as Linear / Once Instead of Cyclic

**Symptom:** Copy was written 18 months ago against an awareness diagnosis that was valid then. The market has moved to a higher sophistication level. The copy now reads as cliché.

**Cause:** Treating Phase 1 (positioning) as "done forever" rather than "current as of [date]."

**Fix:** The workflow is a **cycle**. Every 6–12 months, re-run Phase 1 and Phase 2 for any ongoing program. Phase 8 metrics feed back into Phase 1's next cycle. Positioning is the most perishable artifact in copywriting.

### 7. Confusing Phase 6 (Draft) Speed for Phase 7 (Edit) Speed

**Symptom:** Writer drafts slowly and edits fast. Output is over-polished mediocrity.

**Cause:** Trying to write and edit simultaneously. Both functions inhibit each other.

**Fix:** Draft fast (Phase 6), edit slow (Phase 7). Two separate cognitive modes. Often on two different days.

### 8. Using AI to Replace Phase 4 (Big Idea)

**Symptom:** Copy reads competent but forgettable. No line is quotable. The reader nods but doesn't lean in.

**Cause:** Writer asked AI for the Big Idea. AI is incapable of generating Big Ideas because they require noticing contradictions — and AI is trained toward consensus.

**Fix:** Phase 4 is human-only. Use AI to stress-test a Big Idea you've found. Never to find one.

### 9. Skipping the Voice Constraint Card (Phase 2)

**Symptom:** Copy across emails / pages / posts doesn't sound like the same company. Different writers produce different voices.

**Cause:** No voice constraint card; everyone writes in their personal voice.

**Fix:** Voice constraint card is non-negotiable for any brand with more than one writer or more than one channel.

### 10. The Brief Doesn't Match the Output

**Symptom:** Final copy doesn't deliver on the Phase 0 brief. The objective drifted; nobody noticed.

**Cause:** Brief was filed away after Phase 0 and never re-consulted.

**Fix:** Re-read the Phase 0 brief at the start of Phase 7. If the draft doesn't match, fix the draft — not the brief.
