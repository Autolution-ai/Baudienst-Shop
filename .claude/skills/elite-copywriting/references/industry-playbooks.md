# Industry Playbooks

Six verticals, each with its own buyer psychology, page inventory, conversion math, exemplars, and practitioner canon. Use this file when copy must be calibrated to a specific industry's conventions, not generic "good copywriting."

> Most "bad copy" is bad upstream thinking. The fastest upstream fix is reading the playbook for your buyer's vertical before writing a word. A B2B SaaS hero that wins on Linear loses on Tracksmith. A DTC PDP that works for Magic Spoon fails for Pentagram.

## B2B SaaS

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

B2B SaaS buyers are not buying for themselves. They are buying **with their career on the line**. The dominant emotion in 2025-26 is _defensibility of the decision_. The fear is not "this won't work." It's "I will look bad if this fails, and the buying committee will remember." Gartner reports 74% of buying teams experience unhealthy conflict during evaluation; 86% of deals stall at least once.

**Buying committee reality:** Average decision involves 8.2 stakeholders (up 21% in a decade), 13 for enterprise tech, 25 for technology purchases broadly, 33 influencers in true enterprise. Deals above $250K average 19 external stakeholders. 92% of B2B decisions are made by groups of two or more; 71% of enterprise software is committee-bought. Cycle length: **11.5 months average** (Forrester, 2025).

**Awareness stage by segment:**

| Segment                                         | Arrives at stage               | Implication for copy                                                                     |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **SMB PLG** (Linear, PostHog, Cursor)           | Solution-aware → Product-aware | Open with differentiation, not education. They Googled "Jira alternative."               |
| **Mid-market sales-led** (Attio, Retool, Pylon) | Problem-aware                  | Define the category, introduce a new mechanism. They feel pain, not yet a solution name. |
| **Enterprise** (Salesforce, Workday tier)       | Unaware → Problem-aware        | 6-12 month nurture; thought leadership, analyst reports, "why now" framing.              |

**Sophistication:** Stage 4-5 (Schwartz). Every CRM/PM/analytics competitor claims "AI-powered," "all-in-one," "10x faster." Only winning moves: **mechanism specificity** (Linear: "purpose-built for AI workflows," not just "AI-powered") or **identity-based positioning** ("for B2B," "for startups," "for engineers specifically").

**Sub-segment splits that change the copy:**

- **PLG:** "Get started, free," no calls, transparent pricing, in-product CTAs, video demos auto-play.
- **Sales-led:** "Talk to sales," "Get a demo," ROI calculators, security pages, SOC 2 badges prominent above the fold.
- **Hybrid (now dominant):** Free tier + "Contact sales" both visible from hero (Notion, PostHog, Linear, Vercel).
- **Vertical SaaS** (ServiceTitan, Toast): wins on industry-specific language and workflows in the copy.
- **Horizontal SaaS** (Notion, Slack): must work harder to feel personalized; leans on modular use-case landers ("Notion for engineering").

### 3. Core page/asset inventory

**Mandatory:**

- Homepage with **one positioning statement** above the fold (Pierri's "Minimum Viable Positioning")
- Pricing page. Transparency is table stakes; gated pricing reads as a red flag in PLG and SMB.
- Product / Features page(s), often broken into modular use-case landers
- Customer logo strip, ideally above the fold
- Security / Trust page (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR, sub-processors list)
- Changelog / Updates (Linear pioneered; signals velocity)
- Searchable documentation
- Demo request or sign-up flow
- Careers page (talent magnet + cultural signal)

**Optional but high-leverage:**

- **Integrations directory:** one-per-integration SEO landers (Zapier model)
- **Comparison pages** ("vs Jira," "vs Salesforce") for high-intent search capture
- **Customer story / case study pages,** segmented by ICP for enterprise
- **ROI calculator** for sales-led motions
- **Public roadmap** for community-led brands
- **Manifesto / "Why we built this"** (Linear's `/method`, Basecamp's "Shape Up")
- **Dedicated security / IT / Finance pages** when committee is large

### 4. Top 5 conversion patterns that win

1. **Specific-niche-in-the-hero positioning.** Anthony Pierri's analysis of 350+ companies: the homepage hero must answer _category + target + #1 value_ in one sentence. "AI-native CRM platform built for startups and builders" (Attio) beats "the best CRM."
2. **"For X who Y" identity hooks.** Pylon: "the only customer support platform built for B2B." Cursor: "Built to make you extraordinarily productive." Identity collapses five features into one.
3. **Numbers and proof above the fold.** "Trusted by 25,000+ companies" (Linear). "Over 80,000 startups trust Attio." PostHog inverts the cliché: "98% of our customers use PostHog for free."
4. **Three-tier pricing with anchored mid-tier highlighted.** Good-Better-Best with 2-3x price jumps; the Business tier visually flagged "Most popular." Annual discount toggle. Per-seat for collaboration tools; usage-based for infra/analytics (PostHog).
5. **Anti-friction language calibrated to the buyer.** "No credit card required" (PLG). "Never have to 'jump on a quick call' with sales" (PostHog: developer-friendly counter-norm). Linear strips it entirely with a single "Get started"; the rest of the page does the work.

### 5. Top 5 common failures

1. **Generic-positioning syndrome.** "All-in-one platform to streamline your workflow." Pierri calls this "category confusion." Fix is target-narrowing, not adjective-stacking.
2. **Hiding pricing.** 2.8% pricing-page CVR without transparent pricing vs. 4.6% with. "Contact us for pricing" reads as a red flag in SMB/mid-market.
3. **Feature-list homepages.** Listing 30 features instead of selling one outcome. Loses to a competitor who says "the one thing we do better."
4. **Buying-committee blindness.** Copy speaks only to the end user (engineer); ignores the economic buyer (VP) and the security gatekeeper (CISO). Winners build separate IT/Security/Finance pages.
5. **Slow demo-request flow.** Demo forms with 12+ fields. Top performers shrink to 3-4 (name, work email, company size, role). Calendly-style instant booking outperforms "we'll get back to you" by 2-3x.

## eCommerce / DTC

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

DTC buyers are doing one of two jobs:

1. **Replacing a commodity** (deodorant, vitamins, water) with a _better-for-X_ alternative. They fear "is this actually better or just packaging?"
2. **Buying identity** (Aviator Nation hoodie, Tracksmith singlet). They fear "is this trying too hard / will my friends recognize the brand?"

The dominant fear is **not price**. It's regret + cognitive dissonance. The buyer has 30 seconds of attention on mobile and is comparing your product to an idealized version of what they imagined they wanted. They arrive _solution-aware_ or _product-aware_ because Meta/TikTok ads brought them in.

**Sophistication:** Stage 4-5 in legacy categories (cereal, water, mattresses), Stage 2-3 for new-category brands (cricket protein, mushroom coffee).

**Sub-segment psychological splits:**

- **Subscription DTC** (AG1, ButcherBox, Magic Spoon): Identity + habit. Sells the future self, not the current product. Money-back guarantee is non-negotiable.
- **Statement DTC** (Liquid Death, Aviator Nation): Buying the _brand voice_; the product is the merch.
- **Premium DTC** (Tracksmith, Allbirds): Heritage + craft. Earned premium through editorial-grade content.
- **Telehealth / care DTC** (Hims, Wild, Hers): Discreet, "you're not alone" emotional framing; mandatory medical-trust signals.

**Mobile reality:** Mobile commerce = 57%+ of all ecomm sales (2025). Desktop and mobile conversion converging at ~2.8% each (was 3.9% / 1.8% just two years ago, [Red Stag Fulfillment](https://redstagfulfillment.com/average-conversion-rate-for-ecommerce/)). The buyer scrolls a PDP in 8-12 seconds. Hero must work in one thumb-reach.

### 4. Top 5 conversion patterns that win

1. **Hero = product, not lifestyle, on mobile.** Show the product in-frame above the fold with price/CTA within one thumb-reach. Lifestyle photography is for ads, not the homepage.
2. **Specificity as benefit.** "12-14g complete protein, 0-2g sugar, 80,000+ 5-star reviews" (Magic Spoon) beats "healthy delicious cereal." Brands with spec tables and UGC above the fold lift PDP CVR by 20-30% (Eightx). 67% of online shoppers cite image quality as the #1 buying factor.
3. **Subscribe & Save with skip-anytime language.** Magic Spoon: "Subscribe & Save 20%... Skip or cancel delivery anytime." AG1: "Pause or cancel anytime." This single phrase cuts perceived commitment in half.
4. **UGC + video on PDP.** Shoppers who watch product video are **144% more likely** to add to cart. Top brands: 5-8 PDP images + 1-2 founder/testimonial videos + reviews-with-photos + "as seen on" press logos.
5. **Cart upsell with one-tap acceptance.** "Add a 3-pack and save $9" with one-click yes. Recharge data: bundle and 3-pack upsells lift AOV 18-35%.

### 5. Top 5 common failures

1. **Hero copy describing brand mission instead of product.** "We believe in better." Customer doesn't care yet.
2. **Hiding price / shipping until checkout.** Top reason for cart abandonment; now 70%+ of carts.
3. **Subscription as a checkbox, not a choice.** Pre-checked "subscribe" boxes generate complaints and chargebacks; explicit subscribe-vs-one-time toggle with clear $ comparison wins.
4. **Slow PDP load.** 4.42% conversion drop per additional second. 2.5+ second load = lead lost.
5. **No reviews-with-photos.** 67% of sites lack buyer-generated visuals; on a $50+ product, this is the largest visible CRO leak ([Baymard](https://baymard.com/)).

## Agency / Professional Services

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

Agency buyers are buying **risk reduction by proxy**. They fear (a) being the person who hired a bad agency, (b) being condescended to, and (c) being upsold work they don't need. Decision drivers, ranked: **expertise signaling > prior portfolio > rapport > price.** Price is rarely the top factor above $20K engagements, and is the wrong thing to optimize copy around.

**Awareness stage:** Almost always _solution-aware_ on arrival. They've already decided they need an agency; they're now evaluating who. Exception: high-end consultants (Pentagram, McKinsey-tier) arrive via referral and may be _unaware_ of the specific deliverable.

**Sophistication:** Stage 5 in saturated categories ("web design agency," "branding agency," "marketing agency"). Stage 3-4 in vertical niches (HVAC marketing, Shopify Plus dev, AI implementation). David C. Baker's thesis: **specialization > generalism.** Stage 5 markets cannot be won broadly.

**Sub-segment splits that change the copy:**

- **Generalist agencies:** Race-to-the-bottom on price. Need referrals to survive.
- **Niche-down agencies:** Win on "we only do X." Charge 2-3x more.
- **Productized services** (Designjoy, Bold UI): Win on flat monthly pricing, fast turnaround, no proposals.
- **Premium creative** (Pentagram, IDEO, Bond): Win on portfolio + cultural cachet + thought leadership. Don't list prices.
- **Consulting** (Baker, Sakas): Win on books, content, and the "we'll fix your business" framing.

**Buying committee:** Smaller than B2B SaaS. Usually founder + 1-2 executives for under-$100K work; full leadership team above. Champion is often the marketing director; economic buyer is the CFO/CEO.

### 4. Top 5 conversion patterns that win

1. **Niche-down homepage hero.** "We help SaaS companies generate qualified pipeline" beats "Marketing agency." Specialization commands 2-3x rates (Baker, Enns).
2. **Case-study-led navigation.** Don't lead with services; lead with _outcomes_. "We helped [Brand] grow from $1M → $10M in 18 months" with actual numbers. Pentagram, Method, Ueno all foreground case studies first.
3. **The qualification anti-pattern.** "We won't work with you if you're..." pre-launch budgets <$X, founders who want to be in every meeting, projects under 6 months. Repels bad fits, raises perceived value, lifts close rates on filtered leads.
4. **Flat-fee productized pricing on-page.** Designjoy: "$4,995/month, pause or cancel anytime." Removes the proposal cycle. Outperforms hourly-billed agencies 3-5x on close rate for the right ICP.
5. **Authority / proof stacking.** Books authored, conference talks, press hits, "as featured in..." Agency buyers want to know you're the expert in the room before they call.

### 5. Top 5 common failures

1. **"We're a full-service agency offering X, Y, Z, A, B, C..."** Buyer can't tell what you do best.
2. **Showing logos without case studies.** Logos prove you got the meeting; case studies prove you delivered.
3. **No pricing signal anywhere.** Buyer assumes either you're priced for enterprise (and ghosts) or you're priced cheap (and they'll negotiate).
4. **Long contact forms / vague "schedule a call" CTAs** at the bottom of the funnel where you actually have intent.
5. **Pitching for free / sending lengthy speculative proposals.** Blair Enns: this signals desperation and trains clients to expect free work.

## Online Courses / Coaching / Info-Products

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

Course buyers are paying for **certainty in a future identity**. The product is not information (information is free); it's the _belief_ that this specific path will work for _them_. The dominant fear: "I'll buy another course and not finish it / not get results." Call it shelfware regret. Secondary fear: "I'll feel stupid for falling for the marketing."

**Awareness stage by price point:**

| Price tier                          | Arrives at stage                             | Asset that converts                              |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **$50-$500 (self-paced)**           | Solution-aware, often via newsletter/YouTube | Sales page; needs to feel creator's voice deeply |
| **$500-$3K (cohort-based)**         | Product-aware (followed creator for months)  | Sales page is confirmation, not persuasion       |
| **$3K-$25K (high-ticket coaching)** | Application funnel + sales call              | Page qualifies; call converts                    |

**Sophistication:** Stage 5 in saturated niches (YouTube, copywriting, side hustle). Buyers have been pitched 1,000 courses. The only winning move is **specificity of outcome + mechanism + the creator's lived proof.**

**Core objection:** "Do I really need this?" Secondary: "Can't I just do this with free YouTube videos?" Tertiary: "Will I actually finish it?"

**Cohort vs self-paced:**

- **Cohort:** Sold on community + accountability + live access ("you'll never get a Q&A with David Perell again"). Premium pricing ($2K-$10K).
- **Self-paced:** Sold on flexibility + lifetime access + lower price. Lower close rates but better LTV via upsell ladders.

### 4. Top 5 conversion patterns that win

1. **Emotional-first headlines, benefits second.** Ramit Sethi spends 80% of page real estate on desire-building, not headline. The Finisher's Formula uses emotion-driven openers + Reddit-style comment social proof.
2. **The "how I figured this out" origin story.** Creators who narrate their own struggle (Justin Welsh's burnout → solopreneur arc, Dan Koe's broke-personal-trainer story) convert 2-4x better than pure-benefit pages. Buyers are buying the creator's transformation, then their method.
3. **Stacked-value with anchor.** Russell Brunson "Perfect Webinar": present 3-5 components, each with a dollar value ($500 + $1,997 + $497 + bonuses = $5,990 total value). Then reveal price ($497-$1,997). Anchor framing.

### 5. Top 5 common failures

1. **Selling features (curriculum modules), not transformation.** "12 modules, 47 lessons." Buyer doesn't care; they want the after-state.
2. **No money-back guarantee.** For $500-$3K courses, table-stakes. Designjoy-style "75% back, no questions" or 14/30-day refunds.
3. **No payment plan.** 3-pay/4-pay options can lift conversion 25-40% on $1K+ products.
4. **Generic "expert" framing.** "World-renowned expert." Buyer needs _specific lived proof_ (revenue, students, subscriber count, time period).
5. **Fake urgency.** Real cohort dates beat fake "ends midnight tonight" countdowns; the latter trains buyers to wait and erodes brand trust.

## Personal Brand / Creator Economy

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

Personal-brand readers are buying **parasocial trust**. The product is the creator's _worldview_ delivered in serialized doses. The fear is "I'm wasting time on internet noise." Readers will unsubscribe ruthlessly within 2-3 emails if they don't feel the creator is uniquely worth their inbox.

**Awareness stages applied to creator funnels:**

| Funnel layer                              | Stage                   | Asset                   |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------- |
| **Top (X/Twitter, LinkedIn, YouTube)**    | Unaware / Problem-aware | Thread / short / video  |
| **Mid (newsletter signup)**               | Solution-aware          | Newsletter landing page |
| **Bottom (product / course / community)** | Most-aware              | Long-form sales page    |

**Sophistication:** Stage 5 in the meta-niche of "creator economy" itself; buyers have seen every "build in public" playbook. Differentiation now comes from (a) being a _deep specialist_ (Welsh = solopreneurship; Koe = mind/philosophy/business; Bloom = wealth/health frameworks), or (b) being unusually consistent and high-volume (Bloom: zero misses since May 2021).

**Buying psychology unique to creators:** The audience is not buying outcomes; they're buying _access to the person_. This is why discount-driven launches erode trust and why "I will never spam or sell your info" outperforms typical CTA copy. They want the creator to remain a person, not become a marketing machine.

## Local Service Business / Trades

### 1. What makes this vertical psychologically distinct

Local service buyers (HVAC, plumbing, roofing, dental, legal, real estate) are buying **rapid risk-relief from a stranger**. The buyer is often _in pain right now_ (AC died, pipe burst, toothache) and is making a 5-minute decision based on (a) availability, (b) trust signals, (c) price clarity, in that order. Mobile + voice search dominate.

**Awareness stage:** Almost universally _problem-aware_ and _solution-aware_ on arrival. The buyer has Googled "AC repair near me." They're not looking for content; they're looking for _the phone number that picks up_.

**Sophistication:** Stage 2-3 in trades (most websites still look 2015), Stage 4-5 in legal and dental (saturated, polished). The CRO upside is enormous in trades. Even basic improvements (mobile speed, click-to-call buttons, local trust signals) can 2-3x lead flow.

**Underrated economics:** AOV is often higher than people assume. HVAC system replacement = $8K-$25K. Roof = $10K-$50K. Legal personal injury = $5K-$100K+ in fee. Dental implant = $3K-$6K. A single converted lead can fund 6 months of ad spend.

**Buyer state matrix:**

| Buyer state                                  | Time-to-decide | Copy priority                                 |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Emergency** (pipe burst, AC out in summer) | <10 min        | Phone number, "now," "24/7"                   |
| **Planned** (replace 15-year HVAC)           | 1-4 weeks      | Financing, reviews, named technicians         |
| **Researcher** (dental implants)             | 1-3 months     | Before/after gallery, financing, named doctor |
| **High-stakes legal** (PI, criminal defense) | 1-7 days       | Contingency fee, results, named partners      |

### 3. Core page/asset inventory

**Mandatory:**

- **Homepage:** massive click-to-call button; phone number top-right always visible
- **Service pages:** one per service (AC Repair, AC Install, Heating, Ductwork); drives SEO
- **Service-area pages:** one per city/zip ("HVAC Repair in [City]"); local SEO foundation
- **"Near me" landing pages:** paid traffic only
- **Reviews / Testimonials page:** Google + Facebook reviews aggregated
- **Before/After gallery** (especially roofing, dental, remodel)
- **Contact / Schedule / Book Online:** mobile-first
- **Google Business Profile:** off-site but critical
- **Emergency service page:** urgency-coded copy

**Optional:**

- Financing page
- Membership / club program ($15/mo maintenance club = recurring revenue)
- Blog for SEO ("Why is my AC making a clicking noise?")
- Team / About page
- Awards / certifications page (NATE, GAF, BBB A+)

### 4. Top 5 conversion patterns that win

1. **24/7 click-to-call button, sticky on mobile.** 70%+ of HVAC leads come from mobile during emergencies. <2.5 second load required. Pages without sticky call buttons lose 30-50% of mobile conversion.
2. **Emergency-coded headlines.** "24/7 Emergency AC Repair. Same-Day Service if You Call Before 5 PM" beats "Welcome to Smith HVAC." Time-bound urgency on the hero converts 20-40% better than evergreen copy.
3. **Trust-signal stack above the fold.** Google Guaranteed badge, "Trusted by 36,800+ Homes," NATE/GAF/BBB logos, "Family-owned since [year]," recent 5-star review snippet. Local buyers screen for risk first.
4. **Specific city + neighborhood in copy.** "Serving Phoenix, Scottsdale, Tempe, Mesa, Chandler" (named neighborhoods) wins on Google's local pack AND user perception ("they actually cover my area"). Joy Hawkins's research: data-first content with specific local entities outranks generic AI-generated city pages.
5. **Price-anchored language.** "Service calls from $59" or "Free estimates on new systems" reduces unknown-cost fear. Even rough ranges convert better than "Call for pricing." Coupons / financing visible on hero ("$0 down financing OAC").

### 5. Top 5 common failures

1. **Slow mobile sites.** Anything over 2.5 seconds = lost lead. The single biggest CRO miss in trades.
2. **Hidden phone number / no click-to-call.** Burying the phone number below the fold or on the contact page costs 30%+ of leads.
3. **Generic stock photography.** Top trade sites now use _real_ photos of trucks, technicians, owners. Stock photos signal "national franchise" or "fly-by-night."
4. **No reviews on landing pages.** 87% of consumers read online reviews for local businesses. Embedded Google review widgets convert; testimonials in unverifiable text don't.
5. **Generic city-page SEO spam.** Hundreds of doorway pages with identical content for every zip. Joy Hawkins: this used to work; now penalized. Unique human content per location.

## Cross-Vertical Insights

Patterns visible across all six verticals as of May 2026. These are universal levers that compound differently per vertical but appear _everywhere_.

### 1. Specificity beats adjectives, universally

| Vertical | Generic                    | Specific                                                                     |
| -------- | -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| B2B SaaS | "Powerful platform"        | "Trusted by 25,000+ companies" (Linear)                                      |
| DTC      | "Healthy delicious cereal" | "12-14g protein, 0g sugar, 80,000+ 5-star reviews" (Magic Spoon)             |
| Agency   | "Award-winning agency"     | "We've grown 7 B2B SaaS companies past $10M ARR"                             |
| Course   | "Transform your life"      | "I have an Ivy League MBA. I'd just buy The Creator MBA" (Welsh testimonial) |
| Creator  | "Popular newsletter"       | "Join 196,000+ getting mindf\*cked twice a week" (Koe)                       |
| Local    | "Quality HVAC service"     | "On Your Doorstep in 60 Minutes • 4.9★ (2,847 Google Reviews)"               |

The pattern is structurally identical: **a specific number + a specific noun + a specific outcome**. This is Caples' specificity rule (the $25,397 headline beating "thousands") applied to every modern vertical.

### 2. Trust signals are vertical-coded, not interchangeable

| Vertical | Highest-leverage trust signal                                                                |
| -------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| B2B SaaS | Customer logos + SOC 2 + named case study quote                                              |
| DTC      | UGC photos in reviews + "as seen in" press + ingredient transparency                         |
| Agency   | Named past clients in case studies + books authored + speaking circuit                       |
| Course   | Video testimonials + revenue-screenshot proof + named alumni with outcomes                   |
| Creator  | Subscriber count + cadence + "haven't missed a send since X"                                 |
| Local    | Google reviews count + years in business + named technicians + certifications (NATE/GAF/BBB) |

Mixing signals across verticals fails: a SaaS site that leans on "Family-owned since 1987" is unintelligible, and an HVAC site leaning on SOC 2 is wasted ink.

### 3. Awareness-stage matching is the single most-effective move

Across every vertical, the most common failure is **writing copy for the wrong awareness stage**:

- B2B SaaS enterprise sites writing solution-aware copy to unaware buyers (need education first)
- DTC pages writing identity copy to problem-aware buyers (need to feel the benefit first)
- Agency sites writing manifesto copy to solution-aware buyers (need to see outcomes first)
- Course pages writing transformation copy to product-aware buyers (just need to confirm and offer)
- Creator sites writing detailed positioning to top-of-funnel scrollers (just need the hook)
- Local sites writing brand-story copy to emergency-state buyers (just need the phone number)

**The fix is always:** identify which awareness stage your traffic arrives at; write to _that_ stage; let the rest of the page progress through subsequent stages.

### 4. Mobile considerations differ sharply

| Vertical | Mobile load target       | Mobile-specific pattern                                                                 |
| -------- | ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| B2B SaaS | <1s (Linear, Stripe)     | Hero text scales; pricing tables stack vertically                                       |
| DTC      | <2.5s                    | Product visible above fold; thumb-reach CTA; reviews-with-photos in carousel            |
| Agency   | <2s                      | Case study grid → single column; large case-study tiles                                 |
| Course   | <2.5s                    | Video VSL with native player; long-form scroll optimized; payment plan toggle prominent |
| Creator  | <1.5s                    | Newsletter CTA above fold; single-CTA discipline critical                               |
| Local    | <2.5s (hard requirement) | Sticky call button; tap-to-call phone numbers; map embed last                           |

Across all verticals, **4.42% conversion drop per additional second of load time** (DTC data; pattern holds elsewhere). Mobile commerce is now 57%+ of DTC sales; mobile is 70%+ of local services emergency leads. Desktop-first design is functionally obsolete.

### 5. Pricing transparency is now table-stakes, but not everywhere

| Vertical              | 2026 default                      | Why                                                 |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| B2B SaaS (SMB/PLG)    | Fully transparent                 | Hidden pricing = 2.8% CVR vs 4.6% with transparency |
| B2B SaaS (Enterprise) | Tiered + "Contact for Enterprise" | Custom contract justifies opacity                   |
| DTC                   | Always visible on PDP             | Hidden price is top cart-abandonment cause          |
| Agency (productized)  | Fully transparent                 | "$4,995/mo" is the whole sell                       |
| Agency (premium)      | Fully opaque (Pentagram)          | Pricing signals downmarket                          |
| Course                | Visible at decision point         | "Reveal price after value stack"                    |
| Creator               | Optional                          | Welsh hides Creator MBA price until click-through   |
| Local                 | Price ranges visible              | "Service calls from $59" reduces unknown-cost fear  |

The 2026 rule: **either fully transparent OR fully opaque. Never "starting at" wishy-washy language**, which signals confusion in every vertical.

### 6. The AI-era trust crisis

A new pattern that didn't exist in 2023-24: buyers now suspect copy of being AI-generated, and this suspicion converts negatively across most verticals.

| Vertical | AI-mention effect                                                   | Anti-AI-suspicion move                                                 |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| B2B SaaS | **Positive** if specific (Linear: "purpose-built for AI workflows") | Show product UI, not just claims                                       |
| DTC      | Mostly negative                                                     | Real founder photo + handwritten note + UGC video                      |
| Agency   | Negative — "Did AI write this?"                                     | Named humans, photos, voice notes, video case studies                  |
| Course   | Negative                                                            | Live cohort dates, real Q&A, video testimonials                        |
| Creator  | Strongly negative                                                   | Build-in-public revenue screenshots, voice memos, replies to readers   |
| Local    | Strongly negative (Joy Hawkins thesis)                              | Real photos, named technicians, unique human content per location page |

**The unifying pattern:** _Humans buy from humans, especially in 2025-26._ The strongest defensive move is naming actual people, showing actual faces, and writing in an unmistakable voice; exactly the moves that AI-generated content cannot fake.

### 7. CTA friction must match decision velocity

| Decision velocity   | Vertical examples                             | Winning CTA                                                     |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Instant**         | DTC, local emergency, PLG SaaS                | "Add to cart," "Call now," "Get started, free"                  |
| **Hours-to-days**   | Mid-ticket course, mid-market SaaS            | "Start free trial," "Apply now," "Save my seat"                 |
| **Weeks-to-months** | Enterprise SaaS, agency, high-ticket coaching | "Book discovery call," "Request proposal," "Apply for coaching" |
| **Always-on**       | Creator newsletter, podcast                   | "Subscribe," "Listen now"                                       |

Mismatched CTAs are a top-five failure mode. A high-ticket coaching page using "Buy now" feels reckless; a DTC PDP using "Apply now" kills conversion.

### 8. Identity-based positioning is the dominant winning pattern in 2025-26

Replacing the 2020-era "all-in-one" pattern across every vertical:

- **B2B SaaS:** Linear ("for teams and agents"), Attio ("for startups and builders"), Pylon ("the only customer support platform built for B2B")
- **DTC:** Tracksmith ("for the independent runner"), Aviator Nation ("for surfers and rock-n-rollers")
- **Agency:** Designjoy ("design subscriptions for everyone"), FletchPMM ("for B2B SaaS")
- **Course:** Justin Welsh ("for solopreneurs"), Wes Kao ("for operators")
- **Creator:** Dan Koe ("for the one-person business builder"), Sahil Bloom ("for high-performers")
- **Local:** Morgan & Morgan ("For The People")

The structure is always: **[product / service] for [specific identity] who [specific outcome]**. This format works across every vertical because it answers the buyer's first unconscious question ("is this for me?") in one line.
