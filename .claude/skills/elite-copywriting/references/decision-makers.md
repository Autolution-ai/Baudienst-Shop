## The Variance Caveat — Read This First

**These are directional patterns. Individual variance within any segment dwarfs the differences between segments.** A Stage-5 Boomer behaves more like a Stage-5 Gen Z than like a Stage-2 Boomer. A risk-averse founder behaves more like a CFO than like a "typical" founder.

Three rules of use:

1. **Psychographics > demographics.** Beliefs, fears, identity, awareness stage, sophistication level, and trigger event all out-predict age/gender/role.
2. **Hypotheses, not scripts.** Use these patterns to generate testable hypotheses. Validate every hypothesis with VoC (Voice of Customer) research — review mining, sales-call transcripts, support tickets, exit surveys, customer interviews.
3. **Never stereotype.** A 58-year-old female engineer who has been on TikTok for 5 years and reads Hacker News every morning is not a "Boomer woman." She is herself. Copy that treats her as a demographic stereotype loses.

> Caveat re-stated, in case the reader skims this once: **directional, not deterministic.** Halbert's law (market > message > media) and Wiebe's law (research > wireframe > copy) both demand that you go look at the actual humans before you write a single word. The patterns below are the starting hypotheses, not the destination.

## C-Suite Personas — 9 Deep Profiles

Each profile below is a high-leverage stereotype intended as a starting hypothesis. Validate with peer-CEO interviews, sales-call transcripts, and review mining in their category before you ship copy.

### CEO

**Mental model:** 4-7 minute attention budget per asset; thinks in business outcomes (revenue, margin, market position, talent, reputation); peer-comparison-driven; the most vivid fear is reputational, not financial.

**What earns them:**

- Peer-CEO case studies — named, similar company size, similar stage, similar geography
- Business-impact framing in the first 10 seconds (revenue, margin, time-to-market — not features)
- A single-page strategic narrative (Andy Raskin-style: shift → stakes → promised land → obstacles → bridge)
- Commercial insight — teach them something they didn't know about their own market (Challenger pattern)
- Board-deck-ready slides they can forward unedited

**What loses them:**

- Feature lists, technical jargon, "transform your business" openers
- Anonymous logos ("we work with Fortune 500s")
- Demos longer than 60 seconds in the first touch
- Asking them to fill in a form to read a one-pager

**Copy assets that work:**

- Peer endorsements (video, 60-90 seconds, CEO-to-CEO)
- 1-page strategic narrative (PDF, beautifully designed)
- Forwardable email at 50-125 words with a single specific peer reference
- Exec roundtable invites (the meeting _is_ the asset)

### CFO

**Mental model:** _Will I get fired if this fails?_ Recent Gartner survey work cites figures in the ~75–80% range for **CFOs as final approver on software purchases** (up sharply from pre-2020 norms). Verify specific percentages before quoting publicly. Payback period beats upside. Conservative claim language beats hype. Math beats narrative.

**What earns them:**

- ROI calculator pre-filled with _their_ numbers (revenue, headcount, current spend in the category)
- Peer-CFO video reference at similar revenue band
- Conservative claim language ("a typical customer at your size sees 15-25% reduction in X within 6 months")
- Transparent contract terms surfaced before the sales call (no surprise charges)
- Risk reversal: pilot scope, opt-out window, mid-term review

**What loses them:**

- "10x" claims without backing data
- Vague ROI ("save time and money")
- Hidden costs that surface in the contract (implementation fees, mandatory training, premium support tiers)
- Pricing that requires a sales call to even estimate ballpark

**Copy assets that work:**

- ROI calculator (input → output, 30 seconds to use)
- Payback timeline graphic with a stated worst-case
- Peer-CFO video (2-3 minutes, named, on-camera, specific metrics)
- Security & compliance pack (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR, DPA, sub-processor list)
- Contract FAQ that pre-empts every redline they'd flag

### CTO / CIO

**Mental model:** Vendor consolidation mode (widely-cited Gartner figures place **CIOs planning consolidation at ~65–70%**). Risk-mitigator. Wants to reduce the number of vendors, not add to it. A meaningful and rising share use AI tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini) to research vendors before any sales contact — cited around 40–45% in recent buyer-behavior reports.

**What earns them:**

- Architecture diagram on a public page (no gate)
- Real certifications (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, HIPAA where relevant) with audit dates
- Public status page with historical uptime
- Working APIs with public docs (and a free-tier sandbox)
- GitHub presence — public repos, engineering team profiles, OSS contributions
- Substance-heavy engineering blog (not marketing posing as engineering)

**What loses them:**

- "Enterprise-grade" without certifications
- Opaque architecture — "contact sales for technical details"
- "Trust us" security claims with no proof
- Engineering blogs that are clearly ghostwritten by marketing

**Copy assets that work:**

- Architecture page (public, technical, current)
- Trust Center (certs, sub-processors, DPA, incident history)
- Public product roadmap (even if quarterly themes only)
- API docs with copy-paste examples and a sandbox key
- Engineering blog with code, postmortems, and named authors

### COO

**Mental model:** Implementation realism. Cross-functional rollout. SLAs with teeth (not marketing SLAs). The COO has been burned by "easy onboarding" before — they want named timelines and named people.

**What earns them:**

- Named implementation stories with explicit timelines ("Acme, 340 employees, 6-week rollout, 92% adoption at week 12")
- SLAs with concrete metrics _and_ financial penalties for breach
- Named CS contact (not "your dedicated success team")
- A "first 90 days" playbook published openly

**What loses them:**

- "Easy onboarding" or "frictionless deployment" with no detail
- SLAs that read like marketing copy (no numbers, no penalties)
- "Success will assign someone post-purchase"
- Hidden services fees for "implementation"

**Copy assets that work:**

- Implementation case studies (process-focused, not just outcome)
- 90-day playbook (week-by-week)
- Named CS org chart (actual humans, not avatars)
- SLA document published as a PDF on the site

### CMO / VP Marketing

**Mental model:** Pipeline pressure (the most-fired C-suite role; avg tenure ~3.3 years). **~40% of CMOs prioritize attribution clarity as their top 2025 initiative** (Gartner, Forrester). Brand defensibility against AI commoditization. Cares about both quantitative pipeline impact _and_ qualitative brand fit.

**What earns them:**

- Pipeline-impact case studies with attribution data (named CMOs, specific channels, $-attributed)
- Peer-CMO testimonial (CMO-to-CMO, on-camera, 90 seconds)
- Brand-quality demo — the site and product themselves signal "we get brand"
- Acknowledgment that they manage both performance _and_ brand pressure

**What loses them:**

- Volume-without-quality lead gimmicks ("get 500 leads this month")
- Dated marketing assets on the vendor's own site (their own site IS the demo)
- Buzzword-driven category claims ("AI-powered, omnichannel, end-to-end")

**Copy assets that work:**

- Pipeline-impact case studies (with attribution methodology shown, not just numbers)
- Attribution white paper (substantive, not gated, not 2,000 words of fluff)
- CMO peer roundtables and dinners (the asset is the room)
- The vendor's own brand and site as living proof

### CHRO / CPO

**Mental model:** Retention as #1 priority (**~40% of CHROs name it their top 2025 initiative**, per Gartner & SHRM). Culture, DEI, manager effectiveness, employee experience. Risk: "this vendor will surveil our employees or harm trust."

**What earns them:**

- People-impact metrics (retention, eNPS, time-to-productivity, manager effectiveness scores)
- Anonymized employee testimonials (not just executive quotes)
- Accessibility and DEI built into the product, not bolted on
- Transparent data-use policy (especially around employee data)

**What loses them:**

- "Surveil-your-employees" framing — productivity-tracking pitched as employer panopticon
- DEI as veneer (a "diversity" page that doesn't reflect product reality)
- People-data products with murky consent flows

**Copy assets that work:**

- People-impact case studies (retention deltas, engagement scores)
- Public accessibility statement with VPAT
- Employee-data handling page (consent, deletion, transparency)
- Privacy-first product framing

### General Counsel

**Mental model:** Risk containment. _What's the worst case?_ GC is rarely the buyer but is almost always the blocker if anything is missing. The GC's #1 fear is signing a contract that creates downstream liability the company didn't see.

**What earns them:**

- Public DPA (Data Processing Agreement) with no email gate
- Current certifications visible and dated
- Reasonable indemnity language in the MSA template
- A public Trust Center with sub-processors, breach history, and incident response

**What loses them:**

- "Vendor doesn't do redlines"
- Unilateral termination clauses
- Indemnity caps at fees-paid only (zero-risk to vendor)
- Sub-processors hidden behind NDA

**Copy assets that work:**

- Public DPA (PDF, current year, downloadable)
- Trust Center (certs, sub-processors, DPA, MSA template)
- MSA template (public or low-friction-shared)
- Insurance certificate

### Procurement

**Mental model:** Lowest defensible TCO. Weighted scorecards. The procurement officer is judged on negotiated savings and contract cleanliness, not on choosing the "best" product. They want a vendor that makes their job easy.

**What earns them:**

- Pricing transparency (at minimum: starting price, units of value, what's included)
- Fast and thorough RFP responses
- Named, reachable customer references at similar-size companies
- Pre-filled security questionnaires (SIG, CAIQ) ready to download

**What loses them:**

- Sandbagged early pricing that balloons in contract
- References that don't pick up the phone
- "We don't do RFPs"
- Refusing standard contract terms without negotiation

**Copy assets that work:**

- Pre-filled SIG / CAIQ / SOC 2 packs
- Reference index (segmented by industry, size, geography)
- Pricing-philosophy page (even if exact prices are quoted)
- Vendor onboarding guide (W-9, banking, insurance certs ready)

### Chief of Staff

**Mental model:** Gatekeeper. _Will my boss thank me for forwarding this, or roll their eyes?_ The CoS is the human filter on the CEO/founder's inbox. Their job is to protect the principal's attention.

**What earns them:**

- 50-125 word emails
- Subject lines of 5-7 words
- A specific named peer reference in the first sentence
- A single clear ask ("a 20-minute call this week or next")

**What loses them:**

- Long preamble before the ask
- Anything that requires editing to forward
- Aggressive follow-up cadence
- "Just bumping this to the top of your inbox"

**Copy assets that work:**

- Short, forwardable cold emails (templated but personalized)
- Exec briefings (1 page, designed)
- One-pagers tailored to the principal's known interests
- Calendar links with pre-filled context

## B2B Buying Committee — Modern Reality

The B2B sale in 2025-26 is fundamentally different from the B2B sale in 2015. The data:

| Variable                                           | 2015           | 2025-26                                                                                           |
| -------------------------------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Avg stakeholders in B2B buying group               | 5.4 (Gartner)  | **8-13** (Gartner); enterprise tech avg 13 (Forrester); deals >$250K avg 19 external stakeholders |
| % of buying journey complete before vendor contact | ~57%           | **70-80%** (6Sense)                                                                               |
| Buyers preferring rep-free experience              | minority       | **61-75%** (Gartner)                                                                              |
| Avg age of B2B buyer                               | majority Gen X | **64-71% are now Millennials + Gen Z** (Forrester 2024); for deals >$1M, majority are under 45    |
| Top referral sources to G2/Gartner/Forrester       | Google search  | **ChatGPT and Perplexity are now top-10 referral sources** — AI lives in the dark funnel          |
| Probability of purchase with 1 decision-maker      | 81% (CEB)      | 81%                                                                                               |
| Probability of purchase with 6 decision-makers     | ~31% (CEB)     | ~31%                                                                                              |

**The operational implication:** every additional stakeholder you fail to enable lowers your close rate dramatically. Modern B2B copy is committee-aware copy.

### Mobilizer vs Talker (Challenger Customer)

Brent Adamson, Matthew Dixon, Pat Spenner (2015) identified seven stakeholder archetypes inside the buying committee. Three are **Mobilizers** (capable of driving consensus and change). Four are **Talkers** (engage in conversation but don't drive deals).

**Target the Mobilizers. Don't be deceived by the Talkers.**

**Mobilizers (target these):**

- **Go-Getter** — impact-driven; sees opportunity for company progress; volunteers to lead change
- **Teacher** — insight-driven; loves new ideas; respected by peers as a thought leader
- **Skeptic** — risk-driven; pushes hard on every claim; if you win the Skeptic, you win the room

**Talkers (do not rely on these):**

- **Guide** — willing to share information; doesn't drive action
- **Friend** — easy to access; offers support but no clout
- **Climber** — interested in personal gain; can hijack the deal for their own visibility
- **Blocker** — actively opposes change; the more attention you give them, the worse it gets (sometimes — sometimes you must defuse them; see below)

### Asset Strategy by Role

What each role _needs_ you to provide them, in the asset form that matches their decision criteria:

| Role                              | What They Need (Asset)                                                                                                                          |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Mobilizer / Champion**          | Internal pitch deck (template-ready), ROI calculator pre-filled with their variables, anticipated-objections doc, peer case study at their size |
| **Economic Buyer (CFO)**          | ROI calc, payback timeline, peer-CFO video reference, contract terms FAQ                                                                        |
| **Technical Buyer (CTO/CIO)**     | Architecture page, security/compliance white paper, integration directory, API docs                                                             |
| **End User**                      | Product tour, free trial or sandbox, peer-user testimonials, day-in-the-life story                                                              |
| **Blocker**                       | Defuse via inclusion early; address their specific objection upfront (the "accusations audit" technique — Voss); don't ignore them              |
| **Procurement**                   | Pre-filled security questionnaire (SIG/CAIQ), vendor-stability indicators (funding, customer count, history), reference index                   |
| **General Counsel**               | Public DPA, Trust Center, sub-processor list, MSA template                                                                                      |
| **Chief of Staff (CEO's filter)** | Forwardable 1-page brief, short email, specific peer ref                                                                                        |

> Caveat: not every deal needs all of these. SMB deals close with a fraction of this. Enterprise deals >$250K need every asset above and several more. Calibrate to deal size and committee size.

## Founders & Entrepreneurs

**Core psychology:**

- **Speed** — they value velocity over polish; "fast and good enough" beats "perfect and slow"
- **Leverage** — they want tools that multiply their effort 10x, not 2x
- **Autonomy** — they distrust anything that locks them in (long contracts, proprietary formats)
- **Fear of being scammed** — founders have been burned; they're hyperaware of "marketing fluff"
- **Identity-as-builder** — they see themselves as makers; sell to that identity
- **Founder-to-founder selling** — they trust other founders more than they trust salespeople

**Stage matters hugely.** A pre-seed founder buys nothing like a Series B founder. Same word ("founder") — completely different buyer.

### Pre-seed / Seed

- **Decision speed:** hours to days
- **Budget context:** credit card; $0-$2K/month max
- **Buying pattern:** free tier → self-serve upgrade → minimal sales contact
- **Trigger:** "I need this _now_"
- **Copy direction:** free tier prominent, no-card trial, transparent pricing, no "talk to sales" gate, founder-written launch posts, GitHub presence

### Series A

- **Decision speed:** days to a week
- **Budget context:** $2K-$10K/month; first real procurement conversations
- **Buying pattern:** drowning in chaos, wants consolidation; will adopt a "platform" if it saves them from 5 tools
- **Trigger:** "I'm losing visibility / control / velocity as we scale"
- **Copy direction:** consolidation framing, "replace [list of tools]", peer Series-A case studies, founder-CEO testimonials

### Series B-D

- **Decision speed:** week+
- **Budget context:** $10K-$100K+ per tool
- **Buying pattern:** building systems; brings in execs; committee starts forming
- **Trigger:** "We need to professionalize / scale / standardize"
- **Copy direction:** enterprise-readiness signals (SOC 2, SSO, audit logs), case studies from companies one stage ahead, "trusted by [next stage logos]"

### Bootstrapped

- **Decision speed:** varies; usually careful
- **Budget context:** ROI-obsessed; every dollar comes from cash flow, not investor money
- **Buying pattern:** suspicious of VC-backed vendors (price-hike risk); prefers durable, profitable, indie-feeling tools
- **Trigger:** "This pays for itself"
- **Copy direction:** explicit ROI, transparent pricing, no "Series A scaling" language, founder story, lifetime/annual discount

### Solopreneur

- **Decision speed:** minutes to hours
- **Budget context:** under $50/month is the psychological line
- **Buying pattern:** DIY default; will use free tier indefinitely; upgrades only when blocked
- **Trigger:** "I'm hitting a wall on the free tier"
- **Copy direction:** generous free tier, transparent upgrade path, monthly billing option, no annual lock-in

### What Wins / Loses Founders Universally

| Wins                                                       | Loses                                                 |
| ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Founder-written launch posts and blog content              | Generic "thought leadership"                          |
| Public GitHub, status page, roadmap                        | "Enterprise" framing in early-stage tools             |
| Transparent pricing on a public page                       | "Contact sales for pricing"                           |
| Free tier or no-card trial                                 | 30-day trial requiring credit card                    |
| Speed of response (founder-replies in slack/email)         | Form → schedule → discovery call → demo → quote chain |
| Acknowledgment of who they are ("for founders building X") | Generic SMB segmentation                              |

## Mid-Level Managers — The Champion

**The defining question:** _Will this make me look smart or stupid to my boss?_

For the mid-level manager, **internal reputation > company risk**. They are not the economic buyer. They are the _champion_ — the person who has to sell your product internally to multiple stakeholders. Your real job is not to sell them; it's to give them the ammunition to sell their boss, their boss's boss, finance, security, and procurement.

Champions need:

- **Wins they can take to their boss.** Specific, measurable, attributable.
- **Stories with named peers.** "A manager at [company you respect] used this and shipped [outcome] in [timeframe]."
- **Pre-built internal artifacts.** They don't have time to build the deck. You build it for them.

### The Champion Enablement Kit

The single most useful thing you can ship for mid-managers is a _Champion Enablement Kit_ — a folder of pre-built assets they can drop into their company's processes.

Components:

- **Internal pitch deck** (editable, on-brand-template, 8-12 slides)
- **ROI calculator pre-filled with their variables** (not generic — captured from your discovery call)
- **"Likely objections" doc** with prepared rebuttals for finance, security, procurement, and the skeptical exec
- **Named CS contact** they can list as their post-purchase point of contact
- **Peer case study** at similar company size and similar function
- **Security/compliance pack** ready to forward to the CISO without modification

> "We hand champions a folder of artifacts. The folder is the product they're really buying when they choose us. They're not buying software — they're buying the social proof that lets them push for software internally." — sales pattern from a $50M ARR B2B SaaS

### What Loses Mid-Managers

- Anything that requires them to do work to forward
- Sales reps who bypass them to their boss
- Anonymous logos (the manager needs _named_ peers to point to)
- Demo flows that show off features rather than their use case

## Individual Contributors (ICs / Devs)

**The data:** Stack Overflow 2025 — **48% of developers influence purchase decisions**; **20% influence substantial stack additions**. The IC is no longer a passive end-user — they are a buying-committee member.

**Core psychology:**

- **Trust beats novelty.** A boring tool that works wins over a flashy tool with reliability issues.
- **Working beats new.** ICs have been burned by "the new hotness" too many times.
- **Anti-corporate veneer.** They hate marketing-speak, vendor jargon, and "synergy."
- **Status via craft signals.** GitHub stars, X follower count, conference talks, OSS contributions all signal "you're real."

### What Wins ICs

- **Free tier** — generous, indefinite, real functionality (not a 14-day trial)
- **Transparent pricing** — public, on a page, with no "contact sales" wall
- **Real docs** — copy-paste examples, working code samples, actual API reference
- **Public GitHub** — the company's engineering team's actual code
- **Founder presence** — the founder posting on X/Hacker News/Reddit, replying to issues, writing real engineering content
- **Postmortems** — public incident reports show maturity
- **Honest acknowledgment of weaknesses** — "we don't yet support X; here's our workaround"

### What Loses ICs

- "Enterprise-grade" without anything to back it up
- Sales-first websites where you can't try it without a demo
- Documentation generated by marketing rather than engineers
- Empty "engineering blog" with one ghostwritten post per quarter
- Founders who are invisible
- Buzzwords ("AI-powered, blockchain-enabled, cloud-native")

## Contractors, Freelancers, Solopreneurs

**The defining fear:** cash flow. Every purchase decision is filtered through "will this pay for itself this month?"

**Core psychology:**

- **Autonomy = meta-value.** They left jobs to have autonomy. Tools that re-impose constraints lose.
- **Identity-as-pro.** They want tools that signal "I'm running a real business," not "I'm a hobbyist."
- **Fear of being unprofessional.** They want tools their clients respect.
- **Fear of overpaying.** Margin is thin; every dollar matters.
- **Price-sensitive within value-sensitive.** They'll pay for quality but not for fluff.

**The psychological pricing lines:**

- **Under $50/month** — easy yes (impulse range)
- **$50-$100/month** — careful consideration; needs clear ROI
- **$100-$200/month** — annual commitment territory; needs sustained value
- **$200+/month** — major decision; needs proof of ROI before adoption

### Copy Direction

- Specific use case framing ("for freelance designers / consultants / coaches")
- ROI in client-revenue terms ("charge $X more per project")
- Annual discount option (predictable cash flow for them)
- Money-back guarantee or month-to-month flexibility
- Founder/team that visibly understands solo work
- No "team plan" upsell pressure on solo users

### What Loses Them

- Pricing that assumes a team of 5
- "Per seat" pricing where the cheapest tier still requires multiple seats
- Long-term lock-in (annual prepay without month-to-month option)
- Sales calls required for any plan
- Generic SMB marketing that talks to 50-person companies

## Employees Buying Personally

**Mental model:** Career growth, status, transformation, identity. The employee buying a course, a coaching program, a productivity tool, or a learning platform out of their own pocket is buying _who they want to become_.

**Key data:** **97% of self-paced course buyers don't finish.** Cohort-based courses dramatically outperform self-paced on completion. **The psychological line for monthly education spend is ~$200.**

### What Works

- **Cohort-based or live elements** — accountability raises completion rates 5-10x
- **Transformation framing** — "in 12 weeks, you'll be the kind of person who…"
- **Named instructor credentials** — specific results, specific companies, specific peers
- **Payment plans** — splitting $1,200 into 6×$200 dramatically increases conversion
- **Education that travels** — "you can take this skill to your next job"
- **Status signals** — certificates, alumni networks, recognizable past students

### What Loses Them

- Vague "join thousands of learners" social proof without named transformations
- All-self-paced with no live elements (high refund rate)
- Sticker prices above $2K without a payment plan
- Generic "career growth" framing without specificity
- No clear "what you'll be able to do" outcome statement

### Cross-Cutting Patterns (Women, All Stages)

- Reviews and peer testimonials carry disproportionate weight across all stages
- Ingredient/material/supply transparency increasingly expected (across age)
- Wellness, sustainability, and ethics are baseline expectations, not differentiators
- Visual representation (models, photography) matters more for trust than for many men's segments

## Generational Cohorts

> Caveat: generations are crude proxies. A 30-year-old in San Francisco tech and a 30-year-old in rural Ohio have less in common than two 30-year-olds and two 50-year-olds in the same subculture. **Subculture > generation > age.** Use generational data as a starting hypothesis only.

### Gen Z (~1997-2012)

**Key data:** Pew Research, Edelman Trust Barometer 2025, McKinsey "True Gen" (2018, updated 2024).

- Truth-seeking; mobile-first; widely-cited industry surveys (Pew, Adobe, eMarketer) find a meaningful share — variously reported around 40% — use TikTok as a primary search surface for many discovery queries, bypassing Google
- Authenticity non-negotiable; polished marketing copy reads as inauthentic
- AI-fluent — **~15% use AI tools "a lot,"** roughly 2× the general rate (Pew 2024)
- A majority rank value-alignment above price in many categories (McKinsey "True Gen" and follow-up research; specific figures vary)
- Distrust traditional advertising; trust creators and peers
- Mental health and identity surface explicitly in purchase reasoning

**Copy direction:** short-form, peer-driven, values-explicit, UGC over polished hero shots, mobile-native layouts, anti-corporate voice.

### Millennials (~1981-1996)

**Key data:** Numerator, Statista, McKinsey.

- **Largest age cohort** (29-44 in 2026); **~26.1% of US consumer spend** (Numerator)
- Experiences over stuff; transparency expected; social-proof-driven
- **~Two-thirds willing to pay more for sustainable** options
- Wellness-spend leader (~$115/mo avg)
- Heavily researched purchases; high review-reading rate

**Copy direction:** practical + transformational; transparency-led; peer testimonials prominent; experience-framing for physical products; substantive depth available for those who want it.

### Gen X (~1965-1980)

**Key data:** Numerator, Pew, BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey.

- Skeptical; pragmatic; **~34% of US consumer spend** despite smaller cohort size
- **Highest household spend** (~$95.7K/yr avg)
- **~7% more likely than average to research before buying**
- **~34% won't trust AI agentic purchase decisions** (highest skepticism cohort)
- **Often invisible in marketing** — positioning opportunity for brands that explicitly speak to them

**Copy direction:** substance; specificity; longer-form welcomed; expertise-led; no "OK Boomer" or "millennial" pandering; pragmatic outcome framing.

### Boomers (~1946-1964)

**Key data:** Edelman, Pew, FINRA Financial Capability Study.

- Trust signals dominate (**~65% of 55+ cite brand trust as #1 driver**)
- **~47% trust traditional brands over influencers**
- Longer-form welcomed (high reading-rate cohort)
- Control **50%+ of US household wealth**
- High financial literacy (~54%); high purchasing-confidence cohort

**Copy direction:** traditional trust signals (named experts, established credentials, time-in-business); longer-form; respectful tone; no condescension; no "easy for seniors" framing (patronizing).

### Silent Generation (~1928-1945)

- Formal; print-friendly; phone-friendly
- Disproportionately wealthy in healthcare, philanthropy, financial categories
- Brand-loyal across decades

**Copy direction:** formal register; substantive depth; print and phone channels remain effective; established institutional cues (named partners, professional affiliations); no digital-native shortcuts.

## Cultural & Geographic Nuance

### Hofstede's 6 Dimensions (Applied to Buying)

Geert Hofstede's framework, applied to copy decisions:

| Dimension                         | High End                                    | Low End                                       | Buying / Copy Implication                                                                                                       |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Power Distance**                | Much of Asia, LatAm, Middle East, France    | Nordics, US, AU, NZ                           | High PD: speak to titles, show hierarchy, formal address; Low PD: peer-to-peer, first names, direct                             |
| **Individualism vs Collectivism** | US, UK, AU, NL                              | China, Korea, Japan, much of LatAm            | Individualist: personal benefit framing; Collectivist: family / group / company benefit framing                                 |
| **Uncertainty Avoidance**         | Japan, Germany, Greece                      | US, Singapore, Denmark                        | High UA: demand data, certifications, conservative claims, longer consideration; Low UA: comfortable with novelty and ambiguity |
| **Masculinity vs Femininity**     | Japan, Italy, Mexico (achievement-oriented) | Sweden, Norway, Netherlands (quality-of-life) | Masculine: status, achievement, competition; Feminine: quality of life, sustainability, care                                    |
| **Long-Term Orientation**         | East Asia                                   | US, Africa, LatAm                             | LT: patience, multi-year relationship building; ST: quick results, immediate ROI                                                |
| **Indulgence vs Restraint**       | LatAm, Western Europe, US                   | Eastern Europe, East Asia, Middle East        | Indulgent: gratification framing OK; Restrained: duty, discipline, restraint framing                                            |

### Edward T. Hall — High vs Low Context

| Context          | Cultures                                                        | Copy Implication                                                                                                             |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Low-context**  | US, Germany, Scandinavia, Netherlands, Australia                | Direct, explicit, claim-led copy; specifics first; assume nothing inferred                                                   |
| **High-context** | Japan, China, Korea, Arab world, much of LatAm, Southern Europe | Relationship-led, indirect, status-aware, image-rich; meaning carried by context, relationship, and visuals as much as words |

### Regional B2B Patterns

| Region                                         | Pattern                                                                                                                                             |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **United States**                              | Direct, ROI-led, fast decisions; contract redlines expected; "we'd love your business" tone acceptable                                              |
| **United Kingdom**                             | Understated, irony-aware, slower decisions; overt enthusiasm reads as gauche; precision and dry humor land                                          |
| **DACH (Germany, Austria, Switzerland)**       | Precision, hierarchy, lengthy consideration, formal address (Sie); **German-language assets build significant trust**; titles matter                |
| **France**                                     | Formality, intellectualism; **French-language assets often required**; tradition and craftsmanship valued                                           |
| **Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland)** | Flat hierarchies, consensus-driven; **ESG and sustainability primary**; understatement valued; over-promising lost                                  |
| **Japan**                                      | Relationship-led; indirect communication; group consensus (_nemawashi_); longer sales cycles; "face" (_kao_) critical; written introductions matter |
| **China**                                      | _Guanxi_ (relationship network), face, reciprocity; rapid in consumer tech; slow and relationship-heavy in enterprise SaaS; localization essential  |
| **India**                                      | Relationship-led, English-fluent in business; _jugaad_ (frugal innovation) admired; _visvaas_ (trust) earned over time, not claimed                 |
| **Latin America**                              | Relationship-driven, expressive, family-aware; rushing to contract is disrespectful; warmth required                                                |
| **Middle East / Gulf**                         | Relationship-led, hierarchy-respecting; **introductions critical** (cold outreach largely fails); meeting in person early                           |

> Caveat: regional patterns are generalizations across diverse populations. Within any region, urban / rural, industry, and individual variance is large.

## Buying Psychology by Purchase Type

The purchase _type_ — even within the same buyer — changes copy strategy fundamentally.

| Purchase Type                                   | Key Psychology                                                    | Copy Strategy                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |

## Halbert's Universal "Why People Buy" Motives

Gary Halbert's enumeration of fundamental purchase motives — read this list before writing any hero section. The motive you're tapping into determines the hook.

**Classical list:**

- Make money
- Save money
- Save time
- Avoid effort
- Comfort
- Health
- Escape physical pain
- Escape emotional pain
- Win praise
- Feel loved
- Be in fashion
- Satisfy curiosity
- Protect family / reputation
- Be popular
- Gratify appetite
- Emulate admired peers
- Avoid criticism
- Be unique
- Express identity
- Status
- Control / power

**Modern additions:**

- Certainty (anxiety reduction in a chaotic world)
- Variety
- Connection
- Self-actualize
- Contribute
- **Avoid loss** (per Kahneman-Tversky, ~2× as strong as gain)
- **Avoid being scammed** (huge for founders, freelancers, creators)
- Free time / reclaim attention
- Optionality (preserving future choice)

**Operational rule:** every headline should be traceable to at least one of these motives. If you can't name the motive, the headline isn't doing work.

## Personality-Based Buyer Frameworks

### DISC — 4 Buying Styles

| Type               | Buying Style                            | What Wins Them                                                  |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **D — Driver**     | Results-focused, fast, impatient        | ROI; bottom-line outcomes; respect their time; cut to the chase |
| **I — Influencer** | Vision-focused, peer-driven, optimistic | Vision; peer validation; social proof; enthusiasm matched       |
| **S — Steady**     | Trust-focused, deliberate, loyal        | Trust over time; reliability; named CS contact; reassurance     |
| **C — Compliant**  | Data-focused, analytical, thorough      | Data; proof; documentation; specifications; methodical depth    |

### Big Five (OCEAN) — Geoffrey Miller's Signaling Theory

Miller's argument: products signal traits to others. Buyers select products that signal the traits they want to be associated with.

| Trait (high)               | Buying signal                          | Copy direction                                                |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **High Openness**          | Unique, edgy, novel, artisanal         | Bold typography, unusual category framing, story over feature |
| **High Conscientiousness** | Productivity, organization, discipline | Process, system, methodology, structure                       |
| **High Extraversion**      | Photographable, social, visible status | Visual primary, social proof, "be seen with this"             |
| **High Agreeableness**     | Prosocial, sustainable, ethical        | Cause-aligned, transparency, "this helps others too"          |
| **High Neuroticism**       | Safety, certainty, anxiety-reducing    | Guarantee, control, predictability, "no surprises"            |

### Enneagram — 9 Types

Each type has a distinct fear and desire pair driving purchase. Less rigorous than DISC or OCEAN, but useful as a creative-generation lens for diverse persona-coverage.

| Type                  | Core Fear                | Core Desire               | Buying Trigger                      |
| --------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **1 — Reformer**      | Being corrupt / wrong    | Being good / right        | "This is the correct choice"        |
| **2 — Helper**        | Being unloved / unwanted | Being loved / needed      | "This helps you help others"        |
| **3 — Achiever**      | Being worthless          | Being valuable            | "This makes you successful"         |
| **4 — Individualist** | Being ordinary           | Being authentic / unique  | "This expresses who you really are" |
| **5 — Investigator**  | Being incompetent        | Being capable / knowing   | "Master this domain"                |
| **6 — Loyalist**      | Being without support    | Being safe / certain      | "This is reliable / proven"         |
| **7 — Enthusiast**    | Being limited / bored    | Being free / satisfied    | "Unlock new possibilities"          |
| **8 — Challenger**    | Being controlled         | Being in control / strong | "Take command"                      |
| **9 — Peacemaker**    | Being separated / lost   | Being at peace            | "This brings ease / harmony"        |

> Caveat: personality frameworks are tools for hypothesis generation, not deterministic classification. Use them to ensure copy doesn't accidentally exclude major personality patterns. Don't try to typecast individual prospects.

## The Awareness × Persona Matrix — Summary

The most-decisive cross-section: **Schwartz awareness stage × buyer role**. A Stage-5 Boomer behaves more like a Stage-5 Gen Z than like a Stage-2 Boomer. Sort by **awareness first, role second, demographics third**.

See [awareness-sophistication.md](awareness-sophistication.md) for the full matrix and Schwartz's 7 techniques. Summary table:

| Role            | Typical Entry Stage | Time Horizon   | Top Objection                       |
| --------------- | ------------------- | -------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| CEO             | 1-3                 | <5 min / touch | "Why now / why us"                  |
| CFO             | 2-4                 | weeks          | "Show me the math"                  |
| CTO / CIO       | 3-4                 | weeks-months   | "Will this break / scale / secure"  |
| Champion        | 2-4                 | weeks          | "How do I sell this internally"     |
| End User        | 2-4                 | hours-days     | "Will I use this every day"         |
| Procurement     | 4-5                 | weeks          | "Best TCO, lowest risk"             |
| Founder         | 3-5                 | hours-days     | "Trust + speed"                     |
| Mid-manager     | 2-4                 | days-weeks     | "Will I look smart"                 |
| IC / Dev        | 4-5                 | minutes-hours  | "Trust + integration + docs"        |
| Freelancer      | 3-5                 | minutes-days   | "Worth the monthly cost"            |
| Boomer consumer | 1-3                 | days-weeks     | "Can I trust this brand"            |
| Gen Z consumer  | 1-3                 | seconds-hours  | "Is this authentic + value-aligned" |

**Operational rule:** ALWAYS diagnose stage and role before writing. Most underperforming copy targets a Stage-4 audience with Stage-1 copy, or a Stage-1 audience with Stage-4 copy. Misjudging stage is the #1 reason promotions fail (Schwartz 1966, still true).

## Final Caveat — The Whole File in One Paragraph

These are directional patterns. Individual variance dwarfs group differences. A specific human in front of you may behave nothing like the patterns above — and your job is to find out who they actually are before you write. Use this file to generate testable hypotheses; validate every hypothesis with VoC research (review mining, sales call transcripts, support tickets, exit surveys, customer interviews). Halbert's law: market > message > media. Wiebe's law: research > wireframe > copy. Go look at the actual humans before you write a single word.
