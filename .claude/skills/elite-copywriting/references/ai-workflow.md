## The 2023→2026 Shift

Three phases of the industry's relationship with generative AI:

**Phase 1 — Denial (late 2022 → mid 2023).** GPT-3.5 outputs were demonstrably bad: generic, vague, factually loose. Senior copywriters mocked it. Conversion shops banned it. The dominant frame was: "this won't replace anyone good."

**Phase 2 — Reluctant adoption as research tool (mid 2023 → mid 2024).** GPT-4 changed the math. Joanna Wiebe's framing landed across the industry: "$20/month junior copywriter." Use it for VoC synthesis, ideation, first-pass FAQs — not for the writing that matters. Pros stopped pretending they didn't use it; agencies stopped pretending they didn't use it; everyone stopped pretending.

**Phase 3 — Core workflow + "premium on humanity" backlash (mid 2024 → 2026).** Two simultaneous moves:

1. AI integrated into every serious workflow (Claude Projects, Cursor, Lex, agentic ABM tools). The conversation is no longer _whether_ to use it but _how_.
2. A cultural backlash against the texture AI produces. **"Slop"** became the dominant 2025 critique term for low-effort AI-generated text (popularized through tech press and the AI-criticism community; verify any specific dictionary "Word of the Year" attribution before quoting publicly). Brands started advertising "written by humans." Recognizable AI prose became a status signal in reverse — a tell that the writer didn't care, the brand didn't invest, the work was disposable.

The premium moved from "can you write?" to "can you write something AI couldn't have written?"

## What Got Commoditized

These categories no longer command pricing. If your offering is one of these and your competitive moat is craft, you have no moat.

- **Mid-tier generic copy** — "professional but bland" landing pages, About sections, generic product descriptions
- **SEO sweatshop content** — Google's AI Overviews + 2024–25 core updates obliterated mass-produced thin content; the entire content-mill economy collapsed
- **First drafts of nearly anything** — long-form, short-form, scripts, briefs. Cost approaches zero.
- **Headline ideation at volume** — 50 variations of "the same idea worded differently" is a prompt, not a craft
- **Translation / localization** — DeepL + Claude beat freelance translators for 80% of marketing copy
- **Format-shifting** — long blog → tweet thread, transcript → newsletter, webinar → article, blog → email. Mechanical reformatting is now a 60-second prompt.

If you charge for any of the above, you're charging for the human in the loop. Be honest about what that's actually worth.

## What Got More Valuable (The Human Premium)

Eight capabilities AI is structurally weak at — and where the pricing power moved to.

**Taste.** AI generates 50. Picking 3 that will land requires judgment trained on years of reading what works. The act of selection is the craft now.

**Voice.** Real authentic voice = idiosyncrasies — the weird word choice, the structural tic, the unexpected sentence break. AI averages those away by design. The more it sounds like everyone, the more "sounding like a specific someone" becomes valuable.

**Contrarian POV.** AI is a consensus machine — its training pulls it toward the median view. A genuinely contrarian position, defended with rigor, is the one thing AI cannot produce on its own.

**Lived specificity.** "Waking up at 6 a.m. to write" requires actually waking up at 6 a.m. to write. Specifics that aren't in any dataset — the warehouse smell, the client's exact phrase, the receipt from a particular Tuesday — read as truth because they couldn't be generated.

**Original metaphors.** AI metaphors are stale by definition; they're remixes of metaphors in the corpus. A metaphor your brain made today, from two things only you've connected, lands differently.

**Cultural fluency.** AI lags real-time culture. Slang, in-jokes, sub-community signals, what's hot this month — these require living in the culture, not querying it.

**Ethical judgment.** AI can't tell you when you've crossed from persuasion to manipulation, from urgency to coercion, from claim to lie. Someone with a conscience and a license to refuse a brief is still necessary.

**Emotional resonance.** AI emotion reads as performed. Genuine emotional registers — restrained grief, dry self-deprecation, hard-earned hope — require felt experience the model doesn't have.

## The Modern Copywriter's AI Workflow

Nine steps. Each names the right tool/model for the job.

### 1. Research mining

Feed reviews, support tickets, sales-call transcripts, Reddit threads, G2 reviews, churn-survey responses into Claude or ChatGPT for synthesis. Wiebe's two-prompt method: (1) extract verbatim phrases by emotional theme, (2) cluster by job-to-be-done.
**Tool:** Claude Opus for synthesis depth; Perplexity for live public-web mining.

### 2. Angle generation (50 → 3)

AI generates 30–50 distinct campaign angles. Human picks the 3 most differentiated to test. ChatGPT for breadth; Claude for depth and contrarian framing.
**Rule:** never ship the angle AI suggested without modification — use it as a starting dot.

### 3. Brief development + stress-test

Write your brief. Hand it to a reasoning model and say: _"Play a skeptical CMO. What's missing? What would you push back on? Where am I being vague?"_ o3 (OpenAI) or Claude Opus with extended thinking are the right tools — they catch logical gaps that 4o/Sonnet miss.

### 4. Headline variation from seed

**The cardinal rule:** write one strong human headline first. Then ask AI for 30 variations IN THAT DIRECTION. Never ask AI for headlines from scratch — you'll get median output every time.
**Tool:** Claude for tonal fidelity; ChatGPT 4o for volume.

### 5. First-draft acceleration (connective tissue)

Human writes the hooks, the big idea, the proof points, the contrarian POV. AI fills the connective tissue — feature explanations, FAQs, "what's included" sections, technical detail expansions, meta descriptions.
**Tool:** Claude Sonnet for fast, voicey fills; ChatGPT for structured blocks.

### 6. Voice matching

Claude Projects with 5–10 "this voice" samples + 3–5 "explicitly NOT this voice" samples + a voice constraint card. The "X has these traits, but not Y" technique (see [Voice Matching Methodology](#voice-matching-methodology)).
**Tool:** Claude Projects, Custom GPTs, Lex Personas — pick one and commit.

### 7. Editing pass (diagnose, don't replace)

Never ask AI to rewrite. Always ask it to diagnose: _"What are the 3 weakest sentences and why? Where am I vague when I could be specific? Where do I sound generic?"_ Then you do the rewrite. The diagnosis is the value; the rewrite is the trap.
**Tool:** Claude Opus is best at honest critique when prompted to be brutal.

### 8. A/B test hypothesis generation

Feed diagnosis (heatmap data, session recordings, drop-off rates, review themes) → 10 ranked hypotheses in the form: _"If we change [element] from [current] to [proposed], we expect [metric] to improve by [%], because [reason]."_ Rank by impact × ease.
**Tool:** o3 / Claude Opus for the reasoning chain.

### 9. Personalization at scale

For B2B: Mutiny generates per-account landing pages from CRM + transcript data. For email: Anyword or Lavender scoring. For high-volume product content: custom Claude scripts with templated voice constraints.

## 20 AI Prompts That Actually Work

Copy-paste-ready. Replace bracketed placeholders.

### VoC Mining

**1. Review synthesis**

```
I am selling [product]. Below are 50 customer reviews. Organize into a table by mood (+/0/-). For each row: the criticism, the recommendation, the feature request, and the exact verbatim phrases used. Then list the top 10 most-frequent phrases and rank the 5 dominant emotional themes.

[paste reviews]
```

**2. JTBD extraction**

```
Below are 30 customer interview transcripts. For each, identify: (1) the trigger event, (2) functional job, (3) emotional job, (4) social job, (5) alternative they were using. Output as a single table. Then summarize the dominant pattern.

[paste transcripts]
```

**3. Pain language extraction**

```
Read these 50 reviews. Pull every sentence that uses sensory, emotional, or visceral language to describe the problem. Don't paraphrase — copy verbatim. I want raw VoC phrases I can paste into headlines.

[paste reviews]
```

### Angle Generation

**4. 30 angles**

```
You are a senior conversion copywriter. Product: [X]. Audience: [Y]. Dominant pain: [Z]. Mechanism: [M]. Generate 30 distinct campaign angles. Each: (a) hook, (b) implied promise, (c) underlying psychological lever, (d) one competitor that couldn't make this claim.
```

**5. Contrarian forcing**

```
Below is the conventional positioning for [product]. Generate 10 contrarian positions — claims that are the opposite of industry consensus, that would make a competitor uncomfortable to attack, that have at least one supporting reason a buyer would accept.

[paste conventional positioning]
```

### Headline Variation

**6. Variation from a seed headline**

```
This is my strongest headline: [paste]. The hook is [pain/promise/identity]. The mechanism is [differentiator]. Generate 30 variations that preserve the underlying promise but vary structure, opening word, specificity, rhythm, emotional register. Do NOT use em-dashes, "unlock," "discover," "transform," or "revolutionary." No three-item lists.
```

**7. Headline stress-test (diagnose, don't rewrite)**

```
Here are 5 headlines. For each: rate it 1–10 on specificity, differentiation, clarity of promise, believability. Then identify the single weakest word in each. Don't rewrite — diagnose only.

[paste headlines]
```

### Brief Testing

**8. Skeptical-buyer interrogation**

```
Below is the brief for a [page type]. Play the role of a skeptical buyer in [ICP]. Produce: (1) the 3 biggest objections you'd have after reading, (2) the questions left unanswered, (3) what you'd Google immediately to fact-check, (4) what would make you bounce.

[paste brief]
```

### Objection Generation

**9. 20-objection ladder**

```
Offer: [X] at [$Y]. Audience: [Z]. Generate 20 objections ranked by likelihood. For each: (a) the surface objection, (b) the underlying fear, (c) the proof or reframe that addresses it. Include the embarrassing ones (price, ego, distrust, status risk).
```

### Voice Matching

**10. Voice analysis**

```
Below are 8 samples of [writer/brand]'s writing. Analyze: (a) sentence-length distribution, (b) signature openers, (c) signature closers, (d) words they use that others don't, (e) words they never use, (f) tonal register on "cold-professional" ↔ "warm-irreverent," (g) 3 idiosyncratic structural moves they repeat. Then write a 250-word style guide a new writer could use to imitate them.

[paste 8 samples]
```

**11. Anti-pattern definition (the "X but not Y" technique)**

```
Here are 5 samples of [target voice]. Here are 5 samples of writing that is explicitly NOT this voice — close but wrong. Tell me what makes the difference at the sentence level.

[paste 5 + 5]
```

**12. Voice constraint card generator**

```
Generate a "voice constraint card" for [brand]: (a) 10 words to always consider, (b) 10 words to never use, (c) 5 structural moves to favor, (d) 5 to avoid, (e) average sentence length, (f) the rhythm rule.
```

### Editing / Critique

**13. Weakness diagnosis (no rewriting)**

```
Below is my draft. Don't rewrite anything. Identify: (a) 3 weakest sentences and WHY, (b) every place I'm being vague when I could be specific, (c) every generic adjective that could be replaced by a number, (d) every cliché, (e) the moment a reader would stop reading. Be brutal.

[paste draft]
```

**14. AI-tell scan**

```
Scan this draft for AI tells: em-dashes, "delve," "tapestry," "in conclusion," "moreover," three-item lists, the phrase "It's not X — it's Y," "Let's break it down," "In today's [adjective] landscape." Flag every instance. Don't rewrite.

[paste draft]
```

### Industry-Specific

**15. SaaS landing page hero**

```
Write only the hero section for a SaaS landing page. Product: [X]. Audience: [specific role at specific company size]. The one thing it does better than [named competitor]: [Y]. Constraints: H1 max 9 words, subhead max 22 words, CTA max 3 words. The H1 must include either a number, a named entity, or a verb that no competitor uses. Generate 15 variations.
```

**16. eCom product page block**

```
Write a product page block for [item]. Audience: [persona]. Use only language from the 30 reviews I paste below. Structure: (a) one-line promise, (b) three-sensory description, (c) the moment of use, (d) one social-proof line using a real review phrase, (e) one objection handler. No "premium," "crafted," "discover."

[paste 30 reviews]
```

**17. B2B cold email**

```
Write a 75-word cold email to [title] at [company type]. Trigger event: [specific public signal — funding, hire, product launch]. Offer: [X]. Open referencing the trigger. Don't praise them. Don't say "I noticed." Don't ask for a meeting in the first email — ask one question they could answer in 10 seconds.
```

### A/B Test Hypothesis

**18. Test hypothesis generation**

```
I'm seeing 60% drop-off on [funnel step]. Heatmap shows [X]. Reviews mention [Y]. Generate 10 test hypotheses in this form: "If we change [element] from [current] to [proposed], we expect [metric] to improve by [%], because [reason]." Rank by expected impact × ease.
```

### Anti-Slop

**19. Specificity forcing**

```
Take this paragraph. For every adjective, ask "compared to what?" and replace with a number, comparison, or sensory detail. For every abstraction, ask "what does this look like?" and replace with the concrete instance. Output the rewrite. Should be 30% shorter.

[paste paragraph]
```

**20. Read-aloud audit**

```
Read this aloud in your head, marking every sentence where rhythm breaks, where you'd stumble, or where a human would never say it that way. Flag those. Tell me the syllabic problem in each.

[paste draft]
```

## The AI Slop Detection Checklist

If a draft has more than 2–3 of the tells below, the reader's pattern-matcher fires "AI wrote this" before they finish the paragraph. The brand penalty is real.

### Vocabulary tells

Words that trigger the AI-detection reflex in 2026 readers:

> delve · tapestry · leverage · crucial · pivotal · vibrant · nestled · boasts · garner · intricate · interplay · landscape · meticulous · testament · underscore · enduring · align with · foster · highlight · showcase · realm · navigate · embark · seamless · robust · holistic · cutting-edge · ever-evolving · in today's

Any one of these is recoverable. Three in a paragraph is fatal.

### Structural tells

- **"It's not X — it's Y" parallelism** — AI's favorite antithesis pattern, now a meme
- **Rule-of-three lists everywhere** — every sentence resolves in a tricolon
- **Transition crutches:** "In conclusion," "ultimately," "moreover," "however," "furthermore"
- **Conversational fakery:** "Let's break it down," "Here's the thing," "Great question!"
- **Setup phrases:** "In today's [adjective] landscape" / "In an ever-evolving"
- **Hedging-then-contradicting:** "Though relatively unknown, it represents…"

### Punctuation tells

- **Em-dash overuse** — AI's signature. One per paragraph max in 2026 copy.
- **Mechanical bolding** — every third phrase **bolded for emphasis** without reason
- **Title-case headings** — When Every Word Is Capitalized, It Reads As Generated

### Grammar tells

- "Serves as," "stands as," "marks the" instead of plain "is"
- "Boasts," "features" instead of "has"
- Present participles tacked onto sentence ends ("…driving engagement, fostering loyalty, building trust")
- Adverbs doing the work specificity should ("remarkably," "notably," "significantly")

### The Anti-Slop Edit Pass (8 steps)

Run this on every AI-touched draft before shipping:

1. **Replace adjectives with numbers.** "Fast" → "ships in 4 hours." "Easy" → "3 clicks." "Affordable" → "$29."
2. **Replace abstractions with sensory specifics.** "Better experience" → "the keyboard clicks like an old typewriter."
3. **Cut em-dashes by 50%.** Replace with periods or commas.
4. **Cut every instance of "in conclusion," "ultimately," "moreover," "however," "furthermore."** They almost never earn their space.
5. **Break the rule-of-three reflex.** Aim for 2-item or 4-item lists half the time. Or one. Just one.
6. **Find one truly contrarian point and amplify it.** If nothing in the draft would make a competitor uncomfortable, the draft is forgettable.
7. **Add one made-from-life detail AI couldn't have invented.** A name, a time, a place, a number from your actual records.
8. **Read it aloud.** Every sentence you stumble on, rewrite. Every sentence a human would never say, cut.

## Voice Matching Methodology

The single most consequential technique in the modern workflow. Done well, it lets AI extend a brand's voice across surfaces without diluting it. Done poorly, it produces an average of the brand and the model's defaults — which is just slop in branded packaging.

### Inputs you need before you start

- **5–10 "this voice" samples** — recent, on-brand, written by the canonical voice author. Spread across formats (long-form, social, email, product copy).
- **3–5 "explicitly NOT this voice" samples** — competitors, earlier-era brand voice, almost-right-but-wrong writing. This is what most people skip, and it's the most important input.
- **A style guide or voice constraint card** — even if rough (see Prompt 12 to generate one).

### Techniques

**Style mimicry prompt.** Feed samples → ask for analysis (sentence length, signature openers, lexicon, structural moves) → ask for 250-word style guide → use that guide as the system prompt for future tasks. (Prompt 10.)

**The "X has these traits but not Y" technique.** AI struggles with positive-only voice definitions ("warm, direct, smart") because every brand thinks it's those things. The negative space is where voice lives. Feeding 5 "right" + 5 "almost-right-but-wrong" samples produces a much sharper internal model. (Prompt 11.)

**Persistent context.** Don't re-prime voice in every chat. Use:

- **Claude Projects** — system instructions + knowledge files (samples, style guide, voice card). All chats in the project inherit voice.
- **Custom GPTs** — same pattern in ChatGPT. Add the voice card to the GPT's instructions, samples to the knowledge files.
- **Lex Personas** — Kit integration trains a persona on your newsletter archive. Lower control, higher fidelity for individual writers.

**The "edit pass, not draft pass" rule.** Never let AI generate the first draft of voice-heavy content (founder posts, brand essays, manifesto pages). Write a rough draft yourself, then let AI edit _toward_ the voice card. The voice has to originate from a human; AI can only extend.

### Tool-specific best practices

| Tool                | Setup                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Claude Projects** | System prompt = voice card. Knowledge = 8–12 samples + 3 anti-pattern samples + brand strategy doc. Set custom instructions: "Never use the words in the 'never list.' Default to short sentences. When in doubt, sound like the samples." |
| **Custom GPTs**     | Instructions = voice card + role + escalation rules ("if you're not sure, ask what would [author] do?"). Knowledge files = samples. Conversation starters = the 4–5 most common tasks.                                                     |
| **Lex (with Kit)**  | Connect the Kit account. Let Lex train on the archive. Personas auto-generate. Useful for individual creator workflows; less control than Claude/GPTs but lower setup cost.                                                                |

## Automate vs Augment vs Keep Human

| Tier                               | Examples                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Automate fully (let AI run it)** | Ad headline variations at volume, subject-line generation, meta descriptions, alt text, SEO content briefs (with E-E-A-T flags), transcript cleanup, product-description bulk generation, email merge-tag personalization, format-shifting (long → short), translation/localization, FAQ expansion from existing content |
| **Human-with-AI (collaboration)**  | VoC synthesis, first-draft acceleration of connective tissue, brief development + stress-testing, headline variation from a strong human seed, voice matching at scale, edit-pass suggestions, A/B test hypothesis generation, sales-email coaching, competitive research, persona drafting                              |
| **Human-only (do not delegate)**   | Voice and personality at the brand level, the big idea / unique hook, contrarian POV, ethics judgment, founder voice and personal essays, crisis communications, final yes/no on every shipped line, relationship-based outreach where authorship matters, anything where being caught using AI would damage trust       |

The rule of thumb: **if a buyer would be disappointed to learn AI wrote it, don't let AI write it.** That's the dividing line.

## Practitioners' Stances (2024–26)

The state of the debate, summarized by who's saying what.

**Joanna Wiebe (Copyhackers).** Pro-AI as junior copywriter. Heavy on AI for research synthesis, ideation, first-pass FAQs. Anti-AI as strategist — the big idea, positioning, and final voice stay human. Her two-prompt VoC method is now standard practice.

**Harry Dry (Marketing Examples).** Anti-slop discipline. Specificity-first. His position: AI's bottleneck isn't generation, it's taste — and taste comes from reading thousands of examples of what works. Use AI for variation; bring taste from the library.

**Eddie Shleyner (Very Good Copy).** "AI as starting dots, not finished writing." Emotion stays human. His micro-copy work is built on lived specificity that AI structurally can't generate.

**Daniel Throssell.** High-profile holdout. Publicly anti-AI in his copy. His position: "If you talk like a relationship-based business, it's about the person." He bets the brand on humans-write-this as a differentiator. The bet is currently paying.

**Stefan Georgi.** Full integration. RMBC II (his updated copywriting method) is AI-amplified end-to-end. His sales letters use AI for research, angle generation, and first draft; human for hook, big idea, and voice.

**Justin Welsh.** Human hedge. AI for volume (LinkedIn variations, course module fills); human for connection (the original essay, the personal anecdote, the founder POV).

**Dan Koe.** AI for utility, not for meaning. Use it for the mechanical work; never for the work that's supposed to mean something.

**Russell Brunson.** Fully pro-automation. "One-person empire" pitch. ClickFunnels' 2025–26 AI features lean into the automation-everything stance.

**April Dunford.** Positioning eats AI for breakfast. Her position: AI matters only insofar as it amplifies a real underlying positioning advantage. Bad positioning + AI = bad copy faster. Good positioning + AI = competitive moat.

**Peep Laja (Wynter, CXL).** AI generates plausible copy; humans validate that it actually works with the ICP. Wynter's entire thesis: testing matters more than generation in the AI era, because plausible-looking copy is now free.

## SEO + AI Collision

The traffic landscape changed faster than any decade before it. As of late 2025 / early 2026:

- **AI Overviews on ~30% of US desktop queries** by September 2025 (Semrush)
- **Organic CTR for informational queries down 61%**; paid CTR down ~65%
- **Sites cited inside AI Overviews see CTR increases up to 35%** — citation has become the new ranking
- **General info sites saw 30–40% traffic drops** in 2025 core updates
- **Expert blogs, technical sites, educational platforms: +15–45% visibility** — depth wins
- **The strategic shift: SEO → GEO (Generative Engine Optimization).** Be the source AI engines cite, not the page humans click. Sharper purpose. Fewer pages. **E-E-A-T (especially Experience) wins.**

**What this means for copywriters:**

1. **Stop writing for SERPs; start writing for citations.** Original data, named authors, dated updates, structured claims AI can extract.
2. **First-hand experience is the new ranking signal.** Generic "what is X?" pages are dead. "Here's what I learned doing X for three years" pages are alive.
3. **Density beats volume.** Fewer pages, each one definitive, beat 200 thin posts.
4. **Bylines matter again.** Author profiles, credentials, schema markup for experience. AI Overviews prefer cited expertise.
5. **Don't optimize for Google's old click; optimize for being the source.** Brand mentions and citations are the new backlinks.

## The Smell Test Checklist

Paste-and-use checklist before shipping any AI-touched copy. If you can't check every box, edit before publishing.

```
[ ] No vocabulary tells: delve, tapestry, leverage, crucial, pivotal, vibrant,
    nestled, boasts, garner, intricate, interplay, landscape, meticulous,
    testament, underscore, enduring, foster, showcase, realm, navigate,
    embark, seamless, robust, holistic, cutting-edge, ever-evolving

[ ] No "It's not X — it's Y" parallelism

[ ] No rule-of-three list in every section (mix 2s, 4s, 1s)

[ ] No "in conclusion / ultimately / moreover / however / furthermore"

[ ] Em-dashes appear at most once per ~150 words

[ ] No "Let's break it down," "Here's the thing," "Great question!"

[ ] No "In today's [adjective] landscape" / "In an ever-evolving"

[ ] Every adjective replaced with a number, comparison, or sensory detail
    where possible

[ ] Every abstraction replaced with a concrete sensory instance

[ ] At least one made-from-life detail per page (a name, a time, a number,
    a moment AI couldn't have invented)

[ ] At least one genuinely contrarian point — something a competitor would
    be uncomfortable attacking

[ ] Read aloud cleanly end-to-end — no stumbles, no sentences a human
    would never say

[ ] Final yes/no on every line by a human with taste
```

If the draft fails 3+ checks, run [Prompt 13](#editing--critique) (weakness diagnosis) and [Prompt 14](#editing--critique) (AI-tell scan) before another edit pass. Do not ship past 1 fail without justification.
