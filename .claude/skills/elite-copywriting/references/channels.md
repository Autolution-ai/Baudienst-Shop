# Channel Conventions

Every channel has its own physics: where attention lands, how long it stays, what behavior the format rewards. Great copy is channel-fluent: the same idea expressed differently for a landing page than for a TikTok hook than for a cold email.

This reference covers the 20 channels that matter in 2025-26, with hook patterns, length norms, CTA conventions, failure modes, real example openers, and the underlying psychology of each.

## Channel Selection Decision Tree

Before writing anything, decide where to play. The wrong channel makes the wrong copy. Pick based on audience awareness, budget, and brand goal.

```
START: What is the audience's awareness stage?
│
├─ UNAWARE (don't know they have the problem)
│   ├─ Goal: Build category awareness
│   ├─ Best channels: TikTok/Reels, YouTube long-form, Twitter threads, podcast guesting
│   ├─ Worst channels: Google Search ads (no query), retargeting (no audience yet)
│   └─ Asset priority: Hooks that name an unnamed pain
│
├─ PROBLEM-AWARE (know the problem, not the solution)
│   ├─ Goal: Show the solution category exists
│   ├─ Best channels: SEO content, YouTube tutorials, LinkedIn organic, Meta ads with problem hooks
│   ├─ Worst channels: Cold outreach with product pitch (too early)
│   └─ Asset priority: Educational content + nurture email sequence
│
├─ SOLUTION-AWARE (know solution categories, comparing)
│   ├─ Goal: Position your specific approach
│   ├─ Best channels: Landing pages, comparison content, Google ads on competitor queries, case studies
│   ├─ Worst channels: Brand awareness video (they're past that)
│   └─ Asset priority: Positioning page, alternatives content, demo
│
├─ PRODUCT-AWARE (know your product, haven't bought)
│   ├─ Goal: Convert
│   ├─ Best channels: Retargeting ads, abandoned cart, sales pages, demo emails
│   ├─ Worst channels: TOFU brand content (waste)
│   └─ Asset priority: Pricing page, sales page, social proof
│
└─ MOST AWARE (existing customers / leads)
    ├─ Goal: Convert / upsell / retain
    ├─ Best channels: Email broadcasts, SMS, push notifications, in-app
    ├─ Worst channels: Paid acquisition (you already have them)
    └─ Asset priority: Lifecycle email, win-back, upsell sequences
```

### Budget × Channel Cost Floor

| Monthly budget | Channels worth running                                         |
| -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **<$1K**       | Organic only — LinkedIn, X, newsletter, YouTube                |
| **$1–5K**      | + Meta/Google ads at $1–3K/mo test budget; cold outreach       |
| **$5–20K**     | + LinkedIn ads, YouTube ads, paid newsletter sponsorships      |
| **$20K+**      | All channels; can afford TV-style brand on YouTube + DR funnel |

### Brand Goal × Channel Fit

| Goal                             | Channel rank order                                           |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Direct sales**                 | Meta ads → Google ads → landing pages → email                |
| **Lead gen (B2B)**               | LinkedIn organic + ads → cold email → webinar funnel         |
| **Brand awareness**              | YouTube → TikTok → Twitter → newsletter sponsorships         |
| **Authority/thought leadership** | Newsletter → LinkedIn → podcast guesting → long-form Twitter |
| **Community**                    | Newsletter → Discord/Slack → YouTube comments → Twitter      |

## Landing Pages

The single most leveraged page on the internet for most businesses. Every paid click, every cold email reply, every social CTA lands here.

### What the hook needs to do (first 5 seconds)

Three questions answered above the fold, in order:

1. **What is this?** (Category clarity, not cleverness)
2. **For whom?** (Specific person; "for sales teams at SaaS companies" beats "for businesses")
3. **What outcome?** (Specific, falsifiable promise like "Close 30% more deals in your first quarter")

### Above-the-fold formula (the only five elements)

1. **Headline:** ≤10 words; clear outcome for a specific user. Clarity > cleverness ("Get a demo" beats "Explore the future of sales")
2. **Subheadline:** the _who_ and _how_ of the headline; 15–25 words
3. **One primary CTA:** multiple CTAs above fold = decision paralysis = conversion drop
4. **Product visual / screenshot:** proof the thing exists and looks competent
5. **One trust signal:** logo bar, rating, or named customer count

### Typical length

- **Hero section:** <60 seconds of scrolling on desktop
- **Full page:** 1,200–4,000 words for SMB SaaS; 4,000–15,000 for high-ticket or complex
- **Sections:** Hero → Logos → Problem → Solution → Features (3–5) → Social proof → Pricing/CTA → FAQ → Final CTA

### CTA conventions

- **One primary CTA repeated 2–3 times** down the page (after hero, after social proof, after pricing/FAQ)
- **Verb + outcome > generic.** "Start free trial" beats "Get started"; "Book a demo" beats "Learn more"
- **Mobile reachability:** primary CTA must always be reachable with one thumb. Sticky CTA bars on mobile lift conversion 15–25%
- **Page load:** <2.5s LCP; every additional second costs ~7% in conversion

### Social proof placement

Below hero (logo bar) → at every objection moment (pricing, security, scale). Real names + photos + numbers outperform anonymous quotes 2–3×. Place customer logos within scroll-reach of every CTA.

### FAQ as objection-handler

FAQ is not a knowledge base. Each question is **a real reason someone wouldn't buy**, rewritten as a question. Mine the 6 sources:

- Sales call recordings ("What are you worried about?")
- Support tickets (post-purchase friction)
- Cancellation reasons
- G2/Capterra reviews of competitors
- Reddit/Hacker News threads in your niche
- Failed deal post-mortems

### Common failure modes

- **Multiple competing CTAs above the fold:** "Sign up" + "Watch demo" + "Read docs" = nothing chosen
- **Vague aspirational hero:** "Transform your workflow," "Unleash your potential" (no specificity, no audience)
- **No proof above fold:** first impression is "marketing site," not "real product"
- **Hidden pricing:** for SMB SaaS, hidden pricing kills 40%+ of qualified buyers who self-disqualify
- **FAQ as ego ("What makes you different?")** rather than buyer ("Will my data be secure?")
- **Long hero copy:** if hero copy doesn't fit on a phone without scrolling, rewrite

### Example openers that work

- **Linear (linear.app):** "Linear is a purpose-built tool for planning and building products." _(category-first; no cleverness)_
- **Stripe (stripe.com):** "Financial infrastructure to grow your revenue." _(promise + audience implied: "you who want to grow")_
- **Cursor (cursor.com):** "The best way to code with AI." _(claim of #1; specific category)_
- **Attio (attio.com):** "The AI-native CRM. Build the exact CRM your business needs." _(positioning + outcome)_

### Underlying psychology

Landing pages are **decision-architecture surfaces.** Visitors arrive with a question ("Is this for me?") and an objection budget (typically 1–3 minutes). Your job is to answer the question fast enough, then spend the rest of the page killing objections in order of severity. The Fogg Behavior Model applies: B = MAT (Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Trigger). Motivation comes from the promise; Ability comes from showing pricing, complexity, time-to-value; Trigger is the CTA. Weaken any leg, conversion collapses.

## Long-Form Sales Pages

The Schwartz/Halbert tradition. Long beats short when (a) price is high, (b) skepticism is high, or (c) audience is unaware/problem-aware.

### When long beats short

| Use long sales page when             | Use short landing page when            |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Price >$500 (B2C) or >$5K (B2B)      | Price <$500 or freemium PLG            |
| Audience is cold or problem-aware    | Audience already searching with intent |
| Product is novel / paradigm-shifting | Product is in established category     |
| Many objections / "yeah-buts"        | Single dominant objection              |
| Info-products, supplements, coaching | SaaS, productivity tools, e-commerce   |

### Scannable structure (8-zone architecture)

Modern long-form sales pages are designed for both scanners (people who skim) and readers (people who buy). Build for both:

1. **Pre-head** (small text above headline; identity / category cue)
2. **Headline** (one of 8 lead types)
3. **Deck copy** (3–5 line subhead that delivers on the promise)
4. **Lead** (first 500–800 words; sets up the problem/promise)
5. **Body** (mechanism reveal → proof stack → offer)
6. **Offer + Value Stack** (item + value × 5–8 components)
7. **Risk reversal + guarantee**
8. **CTA + Scarcity + FAQ as objection-handler**

CTAs placed every 1,500 words (3–5 buttons total). Subheads, callouts, and "scan path" elements should let a 30-second skimmer get the whole pitch.

### The 8 Lead Types (Masterson & Forde, _Great Leads_)

Match lead type to awareness stage. The single most expensive mistake in long-form is the wrong lead type.

| #   | Lead                      | Best for awareness        | Example opener                                                                                   |
| --- | ------------------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1   | **Offer Lead**            | Most aware                | "Get 3 months of [product] for the price of 1 — this week only."                                 |
| 2   | **Promise Lead**          | Solution aware            | "In 90 days, you'll have an audience of 10,000 subscribers — or your money back."                |
| 3   | **Problem-Solution Lead** | Problem aware             | "If you've ever stared at your sales dashboard wondering why pipeline is dying…"                 |
| 4   | **Big Secret Lead**       | Problem aware (curiosity) | "There's one 4-letter word your accountant won't tell you. It could save you $40,000 this year." |
| 5   | **Proclamation Lead**     | Unaware (big claim)       | "Within 24 months, 40% of SaaS companies will be acquired by AI-native competitors."             |
| 6   | **Story Lead**            | Unaware                   | "On a Tuesday in March 2019, I lost $1.2M of investor money in 90 minutes…"                      |
| 7   | **Question Lead**         | Problem aware             | "Do you make this mistake when pricing your services?"                                           |
| 8   | **Command Lead**          | Direct response           | "Don't buy a single supplement until you've read this."                                          |

Modern additions: **If/Then Lead** ("If you're a freelance designer making $40k, then…") and **Contrast Lead** ("Average copywriters use AIDA. Million-dollar copywriters use…").

### Common failure modes

- **Cold lead for warm audience:** using a Story Lead when buyer is already comparing prices
- **Hot lead for cold audience:** Offer Lead when reader doesn't know the category exists
- **No mechanism:** claims without "the unique reason this works"
- **Proof-thin middle:** long page with 1 testimonial = skepticism spike
- **Stack without anchoring:** value stack with no perceived-value anchor before reveal

### Underlying psychology

Long sales pages work because **objections compound silently in short copy.** Every unaddressed "yeah-but" is a buyer who clicks away thinking they decided rationally. Long copy is permission to address every objection, in order of severity, while building belief through accumulating proof. The reader doesn't have to read it all; they self-select to the section that handles their specific objection. Schwartz called this "channeling existing desire": long form gives mass desire the runway it needs to land.

## Video Sales Letters (VSL)

The dominant high-ticket conversion format for info-products, supplements, financial offers, and coaching since ~2008. Created by Jon Benson.

### Hook in first 5 seconds

The "snap suggestion" is a pattern interrupt so unexpected the brain has to finish processing it before scrolling. Examples:

- "If you're over 40 and you still have belly fat, this 30-second video could change everything you thought you knew about insulin."
- "I'm about to show you a single sentence that closed $4.2M in deals last year."
- "Stop. Before you spend another dollar on Facebook ads, watch this."

### Jon Benson VSL Structure (30–60 min typical)

1. **Pattern-interrupt opening** ("snap suggestion")
2. **Promise + USP within first 10 slides:** the specific outcome
3. **Problem identification:** name the pain precisely
4. **Agitation:** show the cost of not solving it
5. **Solution tease:** there is a way, but don't reveal it yet
6. **Reluctant-hero story:** you (or a customer) who tried and failed before finding it
7. **Mechanism reveal:** the unique reason this works (Stefan Georgi's "mechanism is the unlock")
8. **Proof stack:** testimonials, data, case studies, credentials
9. **Offer:** what they get
10. **Value stack:** item + value, item + value, running total
11. **Bonuses**
12. **Scarcity / urgency**
13. **Risk reversal / guarantee**
14. **CTA repeated 3+ times** at increasing urgency

### Read-aloud writing style

VSLs are scripts, not essays. Rules:

- Contractions everywhere ("don't" not "do not")
- Short sentences. Long ones break the spoken rhythm.
- Plain words. Write at grade 5–7 reading level
- Read every line aloud. If you stumble, rewrite.
- One idea per sentence. One sentence per breath.

### Typical length

- **Standard VSL:** 30–45 minutes
- **3X VSL (Benson):** 45–75 minutes with three escalating pitches, each with its own CTA
- **Short-form VSL:** 8–15 minutes for warm traffic and lower price points

### Common failure modes

- **Slow open:** first 60 seconds = brand intro / "what I'll cover" → audience leaves
- **No mechanism:** claims without unique reason = generic
- **Reading style:** sounds like an essay, not a person talking
- **Single CTA at the end:** losing the buyer who decided at minute 20
- **Weak proof:** one testimonial in 45 min = skepticism wins

### Example openers that work

- **Agora financial VSL:** "There's a strange new word in the Federal Reserve's vocabulary. By the time you finish this video, you'll understand why it could decide what your savings are worth in 2027."
- **Health/supplement VSL:** "If you're over 50 and you've gained more than 15 pounds in the last 5 years, there's a single hormone (not insulin, not cortisol) that you've probably never heard of. And it could be the entire reason."
- **Info-product VSL:** "I'm a copywriter. I made $3.4M last year. And in the next 30 minutes I'm going to show you the one document I write before I write a single word of copy."

### Underlying psychology

VSLs work because **audio + video + sequenced revelation** stack three persuasion mechanics simultaneously: rhythm and tonality (Cialdini's liking), pattern interrupt + curiosity gap (Loewenstein's information-gap theory), and sunk-cost / commitment-consistency (the longer they watch, the harder it is to walk away). The "no controls" version (autoplay, hidden scrubber, no transcript) intensifies all three, at the cost of trust if the offer underdelivers.

## Email — Welcome Sequence

The first 5–7 emails after signup. The email asset with the most asymmetric payoff; open rates often 2–3× the rest of your list.

### What the hook needs to do (first email)

- Confirm they made the right decision
- Reset expectations (cadence, content style)
- Open a story loop they need email #2 to close

### Brunson's "Soap Opera Sequence" (5–7 emails)

| #   | Goal                     | What it does                                                       |
| --- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1   | **Set the stage**        | Who you are, what to expect, build anticipation. Soft. No pitch.   |
| 2   | **High drama**           | Open a story loop with conflict ("the day everything fell apart…") |
| 3   | **Epiphany**             | The moment things changed. The mechanism revealed.                 |
| 4   | **Hidden benefits**      | What the discovery actually means for the reader specifically      |
| 5   | **Urgency-driven pitch** | First real offer. Limited. With a real reason for the deadline.    |
| 6   | **Objection handling**   | Common reasons not to buy + your answers (story form)              |
| 7   | **Last call**            | The cart-close email or transition to ongoing nurture              |

### Daniel Throssell's variation ("Dark Room")

Continuous narrative: every email picks up where the last left off, like a soap opera. By email 4–5, readers feel they have to know what happens next. Pitch arrives naturally inside the story.

### Typical length

200–400 words per email. The "Hi [name], welcome to the list. Here's what you'll get..." 80-word version is dead. So is the 1,500-word essay welcome.

### CTA conventions

- Emails 1–2: no CTA, or one link to a story/asset (low friction)
- Emails 3–4: soft CTA (reply, read post)
- Email 5+: hard CTA (buy, book call)

### Common failure modes

- **Pitch in email #1:** unsubscribes spike
- **Newsletter-style "welcome to the list":** boring, predictable, immediately archived
- **Six identical emails:** same hook structure repeated → unsubs
- **No story:** pure value-bombs feel transactional; story creates parasocial connection

### Example openers that work

- "Two years ago I was eating tuna out of the can in my parents' basement."
- "I have one question for you. And it's going to sound weird."
- "Welcome. Before we begin: I made a decision about this list that you should know about up front."

### Underlying psychology

Welcome emails ride **commitment-consistency (Cialdini)** at maximum strength. The subscriber just took an action ("I want this") and is unconsciously looking for evidence they made a good decision. Confirming that decision (without selling immediately) cements the relationship. Selling too soon violates the implicit contract of the opt-in and triggers reactance.

## Email — Nurture & Broadcast

Three dominant models. Each has its own physics.

### Daily model (Ben Settle, Daniel Throssell)

- **Length:** 200–500 words
- **Structure:** Subject line tease → story open (1–3 short paragraphs) → lesson → pitch with link
- **Frequency:** every day, including weekends
- **Tone:** personality-heavy, opinionated, polarizing on purpose
- **Pitch:** soft pitch baked into the story (not "BUY NOW")
- **Subject line examples:**
  - "the email I almost didn't send"
  - "why I fired my favorite client"
  - "this is going to make some people mad"

### Weekly model (Lenny, Stratechery, Not Boring, Morning Brew)

- **Length:** 1,500–5,000 words for long-form; 500–1,000 for roundup
- **Structure:** subject headline → opener (~100 words) → main body → CTA / sponsor
- **Frequency:** 1× per week, same day same time
- **Tone:** authoritative, frame-shifting, "I read this so you don't have to"
- **Pitch:** sponsorship native section + soft CTA at end
- **Subject line examples:**
  - "Pricing is a power tool. Here's how PMs use it" (Lenny)
  - "Aggregation Theory" (Stratechery)
  - "How Notion became Notion" (Not Boring)

### Monthly long-form (Stratechery deep dive, Money Stuff, Every essays)

- **Length:** 3,000–10,000 words
- **Structure:** essay form (thesis, evidence, application, prediction)
- **Frequency:** less frequent, more memorable
- **Tone:** strategic, contrarian, narratively-driven
- **Pitch:** paid subscription model itself is the CTA

### Common failure modes

- **Algorithm-baited subject** ("THIS WILL CHANGE EVERYTHING"): short-term opens, long-term churn
- **"Issue #47" cruft** in subject line wastes 20 chars on something no one cares about
- **Pitch-heavy daily:** 30 days = unsub
- **Generic weekly:** "Hi friends, here's what we shipped this week" with no point of view

### Example openers (real, 2025–26)

- **Lenny Rachitsky:** "Almost every PM I talk to gets pricing wrong. Here's the simple framework that actually works."
- **Money Stuff (Matt Levine):** "If you are a securities lawyer, you spend a lot of time thinking about a very specific question…"
- **Not Boring (Packy McCormick):** "Notion is a $10B company. It is also, secretly, three different companies."
- **Marketing Examples (Harry Dry):** "I asked 47 founders for their best marketing tactic. One answer kept coming up."

### Underlying psychology

Nurture emails build **parasocial intimacy:** the one-sided relationship a reader feels with a writer they read often. Daily creates highest parasocial bond (and highest unsubscribe risk). Weekly is the sweet spot for B2B authority. Monthly long-form trades reach for retention and pricing power. The right cadence is the one you can sustain forever without burning out.

## Email — Launch Sequence (PLF)

Jeff Walker's Product Launch Formula. ~$1B generated by Walker's students. The canonical "no-website launch" approach.

### Pre-launch (3 videos, 7–14 days)

| Video                    | Theme                                                    | Goal                                              |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **PLC1: Opportunity**    | "Here's the big shift / why now."                        | Establish the new world; reader recognizes change |
| **PLC2: Transformation** | "Here's how it changes things — case studies."           | Show the new world is real via specific results   |
| **PLC3: Ownership**      | "Here's what owning this looks like" + cart announcement | Reader imagines themselves with the outcome       |

Each PLC supported by 2–3 emails (announcement → "have you watched?" → engagement question → re-engagement).

### Open cart (4–7 days)

Daily emails during open cart. Typical sequence:

1. **Cart open:** "It's live. Here's what you get."
2. **Story:** a customer transformation arc
3. **FAQ:** anticipating objections in story form
4. **Social proof flood:** testimonials, results, named buyers
5. **Last 48 hours:** urgency
6. **Last 24 hours:** increasing urgency
7. **Closing day:** 2–3 emails ("12 hours left," "final hours," "cart closes at midnight")

### CTA conventions

- Pre-launch: soft engagement asks (reply, watch, comment)
- Open cart: hard CTA every single email
- Day-of-close: 2–3 emails on the same day are normal and expected

### Common failure modes

- **Pre-launch with no payoff:** building anticipation without enrolling = audience fatigue
- **No real scarcity:** "cart closes" then quietly reopens = trust destroyed permanently
- **Same hook every email:** last day reads exactly like day 1
- **No story between pitches:** open cart sequence = wall of pitches = unsub

### Example subject lines (open cart)

- "It's open."
- "Sarah closed $84K her first month [proof inside]"
- "Last 48 hours (and an honest message about who this is for)"
- "I almost didn't write this email"
- "Cart closes at midnight"

### Underlying psychology

PLF works by exploiting **deadline-driven action** (Parkinson's Law + loss aversion) and **pre-suasion** (Cialdini 2016: what the reader thinks about before the offer determines their response). The 3-PLC sequence pre-suades the reader into the new worldview where buying makes sense; the cart deadline forces the decision.

## Email — Abandoned Cart

The single highest-ROI lifecycle email asset for e-commerce. Recovers ~10–30% of abandoned revenue.

### The 3-email sequence

| Email | Timing      | Tone                    | Hook                                        |
| ----- | ----------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **1** | 1–3 hours   | Soft reminder           | "Did you forget something?" + product image |
| **2** | ~24 hours   | Urgency or social proof | "Selling fast" / 3–5 reviews of the item    |
| **3** | 48–72 hours | Incentive               | Free shipping / 10% off / low-stock warning |

A well-structured 3-email sequence increases conversion by ~34%. Beyond 72 hours, conversion drops sharply and unsubscribes rise.

### Urgency progression

- Email 1: zero urgency, pure utility
- Email 2: implicit urgency (other people are buying)
- Email 3: explicit urgency (offer ends / stock low)

### CTA conventions

- Always show the product image (visual recall = decision)
- Always link directly to the populated cart (not the PDP)
- Single CTA button; no menus

### Common failure modes

- **Same offer in all 3 emails:** buyer realizes the "discount" was never real
- **Aggressive discount in email 1:** trains buyers to abandon for the discount
- **Generic copy:** "You left something in your cart" without showing the product
- **Beyond 72 hours:** past the decision window; you're just spamming

### Example subject lines

- "Your cart misses you" (gentle)
- "Still thinking about it? [3 reviews inside]" (social proof)
- "Last chance: 10% off + free shipping ends tonight" (incentive)

### Underlying psychology

Abandoned cart works on **loss aversion (Kahneman/Tversky):** the buyer already mentally owned the item by adding it to cart, and leaving without it feels like loss. The product image triggers the same mental ownership. Discount in email 3 reframes the abandonment from "I'm not sure" to "I was waiting for the right price."

## Email — Win-Back

Re-engagement of inactive subscribers / churned customers. Lower conversion than abandoned cart, but cheaper than acquiring net-new.

### 3-stage sequence (14–30 days)

| Stage                   | Theme            | Goal                                           |
| ----------------------- | ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **"We miss you"**       | Soft re-engage   | Reactivate interest; ask what they want        |
| **"Here's what's new"** | FOMO             | Show product/content evolution since they left |
| **"Last chance"**       | Decision-forcing | Stay or be removed (sunset email)              |

### Re-engagement triggers

- 90 days no open (B2C)
- 60 days no open (B2B with weekly cadence)
- Subscription cancel (immediate trigger)
- Trial expired without convert (immediate)

### Sunset emails

The final "should we remove you?" email. Counterintuitive: removing inactives **improves deliverability and sender score**, which lifts conversion across the rest of the list. Common pattern: "We'll stop sending unless you click below to stay."

### Common failure modes

- **Treating inactives like active subs:** same content cadence → spam complaints → deliverability tanks
- **Discount-only win-back:** devalues the brand; trains "leave to get discount"
- **No sunset:** inactives drag down list-wide open rates indefinitely

### Example subject lines

- "Was it something we said?"
- "Three things have changed since you left"
- "Should we say goodbye? (decide below)"

### Underlying psychology

Win-back leverages **psychological reactance reversal:** the "are you sure you want to lose this?" prompt creates a moment of decision. The sunset email is a form of intentional scarcity ("you'll be removed unless…") that gets a clean yes/no answer.

## Cold Outreach — Email

The first sales touch. Brutally simple math: most cold emails fail. The ones that work share specific traits.

### What the hook needs to do (first 2 lines)

- Reference one specific, concrete thing about _them_ (not their company in general)
- Avoid praise ("I love what you're building" = instantly deleted)
- Imply a reason for the message that isn't "I want to sell you something"

### Typical length

- **Ideal:** 50–75 words
- **Hard cap:** 100 words
- **Reply rate:** under 100 words gets ~5.4% reply; 4–7% is healthy

### Structure (4 lines)

1. **Specific opener:** concrete reference (1 line)
2. **Why I'm reaching out:** the bridge (1 line)
3. **Value/proof:** what you've done for someone like them (1 line)
4. **Single ask:** one low-friction question (1 line)

### CTA conventions

- **One question** that gets a one-word yes/no answer
- Avoid: "Can we hop on a 30-minute call to discuss synergies?"
- Use: "Worth a 15-min look?" or "Open to a quick reply?"

### Common failure modes

- **Generic personalization:** "I see you're in [industry]" is worse than no personalization
- **Multi-paragraph ask:** anything over 100 words = the recipient skims to the CTA, sees a calendar link, deletes
- **Two questions:** splits decision; both get ignored
- **Praise opening:** "I love what you're building at X" reads as sales-y immediately
- **Calendar link in cold email #1:** too much commitment too fast

### Example openers that work (2025–26)

- "Saw the Series B announcement. Congrats. I noticed your pricing page still says 'Contact sales' for the Pro tier. Quick observation about why that's costing you SMB pipeline:"
- "You mentioned on the [specific podcast] that hiring engineers is your #1 bottleneck. We've placed 14 engineers at companies your size this year. Worth a 10-min look?"
- "Read your post about why your team killed the v2 onboarding. We rebuilt onboarding for [comparable company] and lifted activation 38%. Open to a quick reply?"

### Underlying psychology

Cold email works on **the Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) inverted:** instead of asking what the prospect wants, you show you've already done the research and arrive with a specific, falsifiable observation. This signals competence, respect for their time, and category fluency. The single-question close exploits **least-effort principle:** the smaller the ask, the higher the reply rate.

## Cold Outreach — LinkedIn DMs

Different physics than email. Open rates are higher; tolerance for sales is lower.

### What the hook needs to do

- One specific observation (their post, their hire, their company news)
- No praise ("Great content!" = ignored)
- Value before ask. Share an insight, resource, or observation that helps them whether they reply or not

### Typical length

3–5 sentences. LinkedIn's UI cuts off DMs aggressively; brevity is rewarded.

### Structure

1. Specific reference to something they said/did
2. A genuine observation or value-add
3. One question OR no question (sometimes the best DM has no ask)

### CTA conventions

- One question, low-friction
- Sometimes no question at all; pure value DMs build trust for later
- Never lead with a calendar link

### Common failure modes

- **Connection request with pitch in note:** instant decline
- **"I see we're both in X — quick chat?"** is the most common opener and the most common ignore
- **Praise-then-pitch:** "Love your work! Quick question…" = transparent
- **Multi-paragraph DM with bullet points:** LinkedIn isn't email; the format reads as desperate

### Example openers that work

- "Your post on PLG vs sales-led got me thinking about [specific tension you raised]. Here's how [comparable company] resolved it; thought it might be useful: [link/screenshot]. No reply needed."
- "Saw [Name] joined as Head of Growth. Congrats on the hire. We helped [similar company] redesign their growth stack post-hire. Happy to share what worked if useful."
- "Quick observation about your pricing page: I think you're under-pricing the Pro tier by 30%. Happy to share the comp data if you want."

### Underlying psychology

LinkedIn DMs are read in a **social-context frame**, not a sales-context frame. Recipients are evaluating "is this person someone I want in my network?" before "do I want what they're selling?" Specificity + value-first signals network worth. Praise-then-pitch signals sales mode.

## Newsletters

The 2025–26 newsletter renaissance is real: substack, beehiiv, kit, and direct ESP-based publishers all grew. Style varies dramatically by archetype.

### The 6 newsletter archetypes (analyzed)

#### Morning Brew / The Hustle (snackable daily)

- **Length:** 800–1,500 words across 4–6 sections
- **Cadence:** daily, weekdays
- **Style:** casual, joke-heavy, headlines first
- **Section pattern:** Markets → top story → side stories → meme/fun → sponsor section
- **Subject line:** "☕ [Punny reference to news event]"
- **Monetization:** native sponsored sections within the newsletter

#### Lenny's Newsletter (B2B deep-dive weekly)

- **Length:** 3,000–5,000 words
- **Cadence:** weekly + 1 paid-only mid-week
- **Style:** authoritative, framework-heavy, expert interviews
- **Subject line:** specific outcome-promise headline ("How [Name] grew [Company] from 0 to $100M")
- **Monetization:** Substack paid tier (~$200/yr); sponsored deep-dives

#### Stratechery (Ben Thompson — daily strategy)

- **Length:** 2,000–4,000 words
- **Cadence:** daily for paid, weekly summary free
- **Style:** essay-form, frame-establishing, single thesis per piece
- **Subject line:** topic-as-title ("Aggregation Theory")
- **Monetization:** daily paid ($150/yr); the original newsletter SaaS model

#### Money Stuff (Matt Levine, Bloomberg — daily commentary)

- **Length:** 3,000–6,000 words
- **Cadence:** daily, weekdays
- **Style:** literary humor, footnote-heavy, dry wit
- **Subject line:** "Money Stuff: [Topic 1]; [Topic 2]; [Topic 3]"
- **Monetization:** free (Bloomberg sponsored); used as Bloomberg Terminal pipeline

#### Not Boring (Packy McCormick — biweekly deep-dive)

- **Length:** 5,000–12,000 words
- **Cadence:** 2× per week
- **Style:** narrative, business-history-as-story, illustrated
- **Subject line:** company name or thesis ("How Notion Became Notion")
- **Monetization:** "Sponsored Deep Dives," long-form sponsored essays sold for $100K+

#### Marketing Examples (Harry Dry — weekly concrete)

- **Length:** 800–1,500 words, image-heavy
- **Cadence:** weekly
- **Style:** concrete, screenshottable, "show don't tell"
- **Subject line:** specific tactic + result
- **Monetization:** course sales + sponsored placements

### Common failure modes

- **"Issue #47" cruft** in subject lines (wastes characters)
- **Algorithm-baited subject** that overpromises → unsub
- **Roundup-of-roundups:** newsletters that just curate others' content with no point of view
- **Pitch-heavy:** 3+ products mentioned per email = readers tune out

### Underlying psychology

Newsletters work on **chosen intimacy:** readers actively invited the writer into their inbox. This is the most valuable real estate in marketing. The voice that wins is the one a specific reader can't get anywhere else (Matt Levine's footnotes, Ben Thompson's frames, Lenny's frameworks, Packy's narratives). Trying to be "general" newsletters fail; trying to be specifically one thing for one person wins.

## Meta (FB/IG) Ads

The largest direct-response channel on earth. 2025–26 reality: AI creative tools mean every brand has 100+ creative variants in test, and the only edge is the hook.

### What the hook needs to do (first line / first frame)

- **Stop the scroll** in <1.5 seconds
- Either pattern-interrupt visually (image) or pattern-interrupt copy-wise (first 8 words)
- Make the next 117 characters of primary text feel necessary

### Length specs (2025–26)

- **Primary text:** ≤125 characters before "See More"; front-load the hook
- **Headline:** ≤27 chars (40 on some placements)
- **Description:** ≤27 chars
- **Video:** hook in 2–3 seconds; designed for **silent viewing (85% mute)**; captions mandatory

### Hook structures that work

| Hook                       | Pattern                                    | Example                                            |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Pattern interrupt**      | Visual or copy break from feed expectation | "Stop scrolling if you sell to dentists."          |
| **Problem-aware question** | Names the pain                             | "Tired of pipeline that dies after demo?"          |
| **Contrarian**             | Reverses common belief                     | "Everyone says cold email is dead. They're wrong." |
| **List**                   | Numbered promise                           | "3 reasons your Meta ads are losing to TikTok."    |
| **If/Then**                | Self-selection                             | "If you sell digital products, this is for you."   |
| **POV**                    | Identity hook                              | "POV: You finally fired your worst client."        |

### CTA conventions

- **Single CTA button** matched to placement (Shop Now / Sign Up / Learn More / Get Quote)
- "Learn More" is the lowest-friction generic option but loses to specific verbs
- **2025 winner:** "Get [specific outcome]" beats generic verbs by 15–25% CTR

### Common failure modes

- **Stock imagery:** kills CTR before copy is read
- **Generic "Learn More" CTA:** placement-agnostic
- **Long primary text with no "see more" hook:** entire ad fails in 0.5 seconds
- **Wrong placement aspect ratios:** 1:1 for feed, 9:16 for Reels/Stories; mixing kills delivery
- **No silent-viewing captions:** 85% of users have sound off

### Example openers (2025–26)

- "Stop scrolling if you sell to dentists." (pattern interrupt + identity)
- "I tried 47 cold email templates. Only 3 got replies. Here's what they had in common:" (list + curiosity)
- "Your accountant won't tell you this 4-letter word could save you $40K." (big secret)
- "POV: You closed $100K in your first month freelancing." (POV identity)

### Underlying psychology

Meta ads work on **involuntary attention capture:** the feed is designed to be hypnotic, and only a pattern break can interrupt it. The 2-second window is shorter than conscious deliberation; the hook has to land at the level of System 1 (Kahneman). Specificity does this better than cleverness because the brain recognizes specificity as "real" and cleverness as "marketing."

## Google Ads

Different physics: users are **searching with intent**. Keyword-intent alignment beats creativity every time.

### What the hook needs to do

- Match the search query language exactly in the headline
- Promise the specific outcome the searcher wants
- Differentiate in the description (offer, USP, urgency)

### Length specs

- **Responsive Search Ads (RSAs):** 15 headlines × 4 descriptions; Google mixes them
- **Headlines:** 30 chars each; include keyword in 50%+ of headlines
- **Descriptions:** 90 chars; lead with benefit + USP + offer
- **Callouts:** "Free shipping," "30-day returns," "24/7 support" (25 chars each)
- **Sitelinks:** sub-page mini-CTAs (Pricing, Demo, FAQ, About; 25 chars + 35 chars description)

### Asset hierarchy (in order of impact)

1. Headlines (keyword match + USP)
2. Description lines (offer + benefit)
3. Callouts (trust signals)
4. Sitelinks (deep-link to relevant page)
5. Structured snippets (categories, services)

### CTA conventions

- Direct, action-verb-first
- "Get a free quote," "Start your trial," "Book a demo"
- Match landing page CTA exactly; mismatch = bounce

### Common failure modes

- **Mismatched intent:** running commercial ads on informational queries ("how does X work?") = wasted spend
- **Keyword not in headline:** quality score tanks, CPC rises
- **Single headline asset:** defeats the point of RSAs
- **Landing page ≠ ad:** promise in ad, different promise on page = high bounce
- **No negative keywords:** broad match eats budget on irrelevant queries

### Example headline patterns

- "[Keyword] | [Specific outcome] | [Brand]" → "CRM for Agencies | Built for Client Work | Attio"
- "[Brand] [Keyword]" → "Linear Issue Tracking"
- "[Outcome] in [Time]" → "Close Deals 30% Faster"

### Underlying psychology

Google Ads work on **expressed intent:** the searcher has already decided they want X. The ad's job is **matching language and removing friction**, not persuading. Persuasion is wasted budget when intent is high; clarity wins. The exception: competitor keywords, where the ad must persuade the searcher to consider an alternative they didn't search for.

## LinkedIn Organic

The dominant B2B organic channel. 2025–26 reality: algorithm rewards comment engagement above all else.

### What the hook needs to do (first 2–3 visible lines)

LinkedIn truncates posts after 2–3 lines (varies by device) with "…see more." The hook must earn the click. Under 8 words is the new ideal.

### Hook patterns that work

- **Story-led:** "How I [outcome]." Example: "How I closed my first $50K client in 8 weeks"
- **Hot take:** "[Common belief] is wrong." Example: "Outbound sales is dead. Here's what replaced it."
- **Lesson-form:** "What I learned at [Company]." Example: "What I learned running growth at Stripe."
- **Self-deprecating:** "I made a $40K mistake last week." Earned attention
- **Numbered:** "5 things I'd do differently…" Listicle archetype

### Typical length

- **Hard cap:** 3,000 characters
- **Truncation point:** ~210 chars (varies); the "see more" cutoff
- **Sweet spot:** 1,000–1,500 characters for narrative posts
- **Carousel/document posts:** 5–10 slides

### Format conventions

- 1–2 sentence paragraphs
- Whitespace between every paragraph
- No emojis at line starts (algorithm penalty signal in 2025)
- One question at the end to drive comments

### CTA conventions

- **Comment-driving question:** "What's your take?" or "Disagree?"
- Comments > likes > shares in algorithm weight
- Avoid: "Link in bio," "DM me," outbound CTAs (algorithm penalty)

### Common failure modes

- **"Broetry" without insight:** fragmented one-sentence-per-line posts with clickbait hook and no payoff. The 2020 format that 2025 audiences have learned to scroll past.
- **Corporate fluff:** "Excited to share…" / "Humbled to announce…"
- **Engagement bait:** "Comment YES if you agree" without substance
- **Too many hashtags:** 3 maximum in 2025; 30 hashtags reads as desperate
- **Cross-posting from Twitter:** different audience, different conventions, never works

### Example openers (2025–26)

- "I fired my best client last week. Here's why." _(Justin Welsh archetype)_
- "Most copywriters use AIDA. Million-dollar copywriters use this:"
- "What I learned running growth at Notion. (The uncomfortable version.)"
- "I made a $40K mistake last week. Sharing publicly so you don't repeat it."

### The broetry critique

The "broetry" format (one sentence per line, whitespace-heavy, clickbait hook, fragmented payoff) was dominant 2018–2022 and now actively underperforms. The current winning format is **narrative with whitespace:** paragraph-style writing with line breaks between paragraphs, not between every sentence. Substance over fragmentation.

### Underlying psychology

LinkedIn organic works on **social comparison and aspirational identity**. Posts that frame the writer as someone the reader could be (or wants to be) outperform posts that frame the writer as already-arrived. Vulnerability + lesson outperforms triumph + advice. Algorithm weight on comments means content that polarizes (mildly) outperforms content that everyone agrees with.

Different from organic. Different from Meta. Most expensive ad channel per click ($8–15 CPC for B2B); wins only when targeting and offer are tight.

### Formats and when to use each

| Format               | Best for                                                                     |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Single Image**     | Lead gen with sharp identity hook                                            |
| **Document Ad**      | B2B education content (reports, guides) — outperforms image 2× for downloads |

| **Carousel**         | Multi-step value props, before/after                                         |
| **Sponsored InMail** | High-intent outreach to senior buyers; expensive but converts                |
| **Lead Gen Forms**   | In-platform forms — 3–5× higher submit rate than landing pages               |

### Length specs

- **Headlines:** under 70 chars
- **Intro text:** 150 chars before truncation
- **Single image:** 1200×627 or 1080×1080

### CTA conventions

- Lead Gen Form pre-filled with LinkedIn profile data = lowest friction
- Download / Get / View specific outcome
- Avoid generic "Learn More"

### Common failure modes

- **Treating LinkedIn like Meta:** same creative, same offer = abysmal CPC and conversion
- **No tight ICP targeting:** broadcasting wastes the $8+ CPC

- **Long intro text:** 150 char cutoff kills any unread copy

- "We helped 14 marketing leaders at PE-backed SaaS companies hit 40% pipeline growth in Q4. Here's the playbook."

### Underlying psychology

LinkedIn ads work on **explicit identity targeting:** the platform knows job title, company size, seniority, function. The most effective hook calls out that exact identity in the first 7 words ("If you're a CRO at…"). Self-selection through identity removes the wrong-audience tax that's destroying everyone else's CPC.

## Twitter / X

Returned to relevance in 2024–25 as the platform for long-form public thinking. Threading made a comeback. Algorithm rewards reply-density.

### What the hook needs to do

The hook tweet (≤250 chars effectively, though 280 max) sets up the entire thread. It must contain:

1. A specific claim
2. A curiosity gap
3. A promise to deliver in the thread

### Length norms

- **Hook tweet:** 250+ chars (use the space)
- **Thread length:** 8–12 tweets perform 47% better than shorter
- **Long-form tweet (premium):** up to 25K chars, but rarely read beyond 1,500

### Thread structures that work

| Pattern                     | Structure                                                 | Example hook                                                                         |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **List thread**             | "N tools/ideas/frameworks"                                | "10 cold email templates that got me 47% reply rates:"                               |
| **Lessons thread**          | "N lessons from [doing thing]"                            | "I shipped 12 SaaS products in 3 years. Here are 7 lessons:"                         |
| **Before-after thread**     | "I went from X to Y. Here's how:"                         | "I went from $0 to $40K MRR in 9 months. Here's the exact playbook:"                 |
| **Story thread**            | "Today I [unusual event]. Here's what happened:"          | "Today I got fired from my own company. Here's what I learned in the 6 hours since:" |
| **Counter-intuitive truth** | "Everyone says X. They're wrong."                         | "Everyone says product-market fit is binary. It's not. Here's what it actually is:"  |
| **I spent X learning Y**    | "I spent X dollars/hours learning Y so you don't have to" | "I spent $40K on copywriting courses. Here's the only framework that mattered:"      |

### CTA conventions

- Implicit: follow, reply, bookmark, screenshot
- Last tweet: re-state the hook + ask for retweet of the first tweet
- Pinned-tweet CTA for newsletter/product signup

### Common failure modes

- **LinkedIn broetry imported wholesale:** different audience, different conventions, immediately rejected
- **Numbered list of obvious things:** "1. Work hard. 2. Be consistent." = bookmarked by no one
- **Thread without payoff:** promise in hook not delivered in body = unfollow
- **Generic hook:** "Some thoughts on marketing:" loses to "I closed $400K last quarter using a single Notion template. Here it is:"

### Example openers (2025–26)

- "I spent 18 months and $40K learning copywriting from the best in the world. Everything they taught me fits in this thread:"
- "Most landing pages are 90% wrong. Here are the 12 fixes that 10x conversion (with screenshots):"
- "I closed $1.2M in agency revenue last year. The pitch deck I used is in this thread."

### Underlying psychology

Twitter works on **curiosity gaps + identity signaling**. The hook tweet must open a loop the reader can't close without reading the thread; the thread must reward them in identity terms ("I'm the kind of person who reads threads like this"). Bookmarks (silent saves) are the real currency; they drive algorithmic weight without requiring social risk.

## TikTok / Reels

The dominant attention channel for sub-35 audiences and increasingly for 35+. 2025 reality: vertical short-form is the universal format across TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts.

### What the hook needs to do (first 3 seconds)

The 3-second rule: if the viewer doesn't commit in 3 seconds, the algorithm penalizes the video for the rest of distribution. The first frame matters as much as the first words.

### Hook formats that work

| Hook                                      | Pattern                                                |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **"POV: You just…"**                      | Identity-driven; viewer imagines themselves            |
| **"Watch this if…"**                      | Self-selection; targets specific identity              |
| **"Here's what nobody tells you about…"** | Insider knowledge frame                                |
| **"Stop scrolling if you do X."**         | Pattern interrupt + identity                           |
| **Contrarian statement**                  | "Most creators are killing their reach with captions." |
| **Question hook**                         | "Why do 90% of X fail at Y?"                           |
| **Mid-sentence start**                    | "…and that's why I quit my job." (curiosity max)       |

### Typical length

- **15–60 sec** is the sweet spot for high completion
- **60–90 sec** for explainer/educational
- **3+ min** allowed on TikTok/Reels but completion drops sharply

### Production techniques

- On-screen captions (85% sound-off)
- Cuts every 1–2 seconds
- Talking-head + B-roll layering
- Visual change every ~2 seconds
- **Combine 2+ triggers:** pattern interrupt + curiosity gap + social proof

### CTA conventions

- Implicit: like, comment, follow, share
- "Comment X for the [resource]" drives algorithm weight + DM funnel
- Pinned comment for link (platform restricts in-video links)

### Common failure modes

- **Slow open:** "Hey guys, today I want to talk about…" = dead before second 3
- **Corporate aesthetic:** high production value reads as "ad" and gets scrolled
- **No captions:** 85% sound-off viewing = silent failure
- **Single-cut talking head over 30 sec:** algorithm wants motion + change
- **No specific identity hook:** "marketing tips" loses to "marketing tips for solo founders"

### Example openers (2025–26)

- "POV: You just hit $100K MRR and your accountant tells you you owe $47K in taxes you weren't prepared for."
- "Stop scrolling if you sell to dentists. I'm about to save you $20K on your next ad campaign."
- "Here's what nobody tells you about pricing software. The first time I raised prices 3x, I doubled revenue. Here's exactly what I did:"
- "Watch this if you're a freelancer charging less than $5K per project."

### Underlying psychology

TikTok/Reels work on **dopaminergic novelty stacking**. Every cut, every visual change, every audio shift triggers the brain's prediction-error system, releasing small dopamine hits that keep the viewer in the feed. The 3-second hook hijacks this: if the brain can't predict where the video is going, it has to watch to find out. Specificity + identity ("you who do X") creates parasocial inclusion that pure content cannot.

## YouTube

The long-form video platform. 2025–26 reality: 8–20 minute videos dominate; Shorts feed acquisition, long-form drives revenue and audience.

### What the hook needs to do

CTR is determined by **title + thumbnail > content**. The content's job is retention, not click. The title + thumbnail's job is the click.

### MrBeast's principles (2024–25 canonical)

- 20+ thumbnail variations per video before launch
- Closed-mouth thumbnails consistently outperform open-mouth in tests
- Pattern interrupts every 25–35 seconds
- Visual change every ~2 seconds
- "Retention curve" obsession; every 30-second drop is a content failure to fix

### Title patterns that work

- **Specific number + outcome + curiosity:** "I Tried Every Productivity Method for 30 Days"
- **Contrarian:** "Why I Stopped Reading Productivity Books"
- **Process documentation:** "How I Built a $1M Business in 12 Months"
- **Reverse-engineering:** "I Studied 100 SaaS Landing Pages. Here's What Works"

### Typical length

- **8–20 minutes** is the sweet spot for monetization + retention
- **Under 8 min** = under-monetized
- **Over 25 min** = retention drops sharply unless content is documentary-grade

### Structure for retention

1. **Open with the payoff tease** ("By the end of this video, you'll know how to…")
2. Deliver one quick proof in first 30 seconds
3. Pattern interrupts every 30–60 seconds
4. Mid-roll "if you're still watching" callout
5. Pay off the tease at minute 6–8
6. Bonus content at 12–15
7. CTA + cliffhanger to next video

### Description conventions

- First 2 lines = SEO-loaded summary
- Chapter timestamps (critical for retention + UX)
- Links to resources mentioned
- Longer-form for indexing

### CTA conventions

- Subscribe + comment in pinned comment
- "Comment X for the resource" drives algorithm weight
- Suggested next video appears in last 20 seconds

### Common failure modes

- **Slow open:** first 30 seconds without payoff tease kills retention
- **Generic title:** "My morning routine" loses to "The 5am morning routine that made me $1M last year"
- **Inconsistent thumbnail style:** viewers can't pattern-match your channel
- **No timestamps:** modern viewers expect them for skim-watching

### Example titles (current 2025–26)

- "I Spent $40,000 Learning Copywriting. Here's What Mattered."
- "Why Linear Is Beating Notion (and What It Means for Every SaaS)"
- "The 12-Minute Sales Pitch That Closed $400K"

### Underlying psychology

YouTube works on **CTR × retention curves:** both metrics independently. The thumbnail + title are pure System 1 (Kahneman): does the brain pattern-match this as "worth watching"? Retention is System 1 every 30 seconds: has the brain predicted what comes next, or is there enough novelty to keep watching? MrBeast's obsession with visual change every 2 seconds is the operational answer.

## SMS

Highest open rate of any channel (~98%); lowest tolerance for noise. One bad SMS campaign costs more in unsubs than 10 emails.

### What the hook needs to do

- Identify the brand in the first 3 characters
- Deliver value or urgency in the next 50 characters
- Get to the CTA before the preview cutoff

### Length spec

- **≤160 characters** total (multi-part messages cost more and are read less)
- Brand identifier counts toward the 160

### CTA conventions

- Single tap → mobile-optimized link
- No menus, no choices
- STOP to opt-out (compliance requirement)

### Common failure modes

- **Marketing-speak:** "We're excited to announce…" = unsub
- **Aggressive frequency:** >2 per week → unsub spike
- **No personalization:** generic blasts perform 40%+ worse than name + behavior triggers
- **Wrong time of day:** pre-7am or post-9pm = unsub spike + complaints

### Example messages that work

- "Linear: Your trial ends Friday. Reactivate in one tap: [link]"
- "BREW: Today's brief is a 4-min read on the Fed decision. ☕ [link]"
- "Hims: Sarah, your refill ships tomorrow. Reply STOP to cancel."

### Underlying psychology

SMS works on **personal-channel privilege:** the inbox is reserved for known senders. Treating it like email (broadcast tone, marketing language) violates the unwritten contract. Conversational tone + real value preserves the privilege.

## Push Notifications

Mobile and desktop. Granted permission once; revoked instantly with one bad blast.

### What the hook needs to do

- Action verb in the first 3 words
- Specific value or time-sensitivity
- Make the tap obvious

### Length spec

- **Title ≤30 characters**
- **Body ≤90 characters**

### CTA conventions

- Tap opens app to deep-linked content
- Personalization (name + behavior) outperforms generic 3–5×

### Common failure modes

- **Generic blasts:** "Don't miss our new feature!" = mute
- **Wrong time of day:** pre-7am or post-9pm = mute + uninstall risk
- **No deep link:** tap lands on homepage instead of context = annoyance

### Example notifications that work

- "Stripe: $4,200 payout sent ✓" _(transactional + reassuring)_
- "Linear: 3 new issues assigned" _(specific count + actionable)_
- "Notion: Sarah commented on your doc: 'Looks great, one Q…'" _(preview value)_

### Underlying psychology

Push works on **interrupt privilege:** most valuable when used least. The implicit contract: only notify when the user would have wanted to know. Behavioral triggers (someone replied, payment received, status changed) preserve trust; broadcast notifications erode it.
